SCIENTIFIC METHOD:
- Hypothesis: testable, falsifiable, specific; format: If [IV], then [DV], because [mechanism]
- Operational definition: exactly how abstract concept is measured (e.g., "aggression" → "hits during play session")

VARIABLE TYPES:
- Independent (IV): manipulated by researcher
- Dependent (DV): measured outcome
- Confounding: unmeasured variable correlating with both IV and DV (alternative explanation)
- Control variable: held constant to prevent confounding
- Moderator: changes STRENGTH/DIRECTION of IV→DV relationship (answers "for whom?"; not in causal chain)
- Mediator: explains MECHANISM (HOW IV affects DV); sits in causal chain: IV → Mediator → DV
  - Test: does it change the relationship (moderator) or explain it (mediator)?

EXPERIMENTAL DESIGNS:
- RCT (gold standard): random assignment + control group + IV manipulation → can establish CAUSATION
- Quasi-experimental: IV manipulation but NO random assignment → weaker causal claims
- Factorial: 2+ IVs; detects interaction effects (effect of one IV depends on level of another)
  - Main effect: overall effect of one IV averaged across levels of the other
  - Interaction: crossing lines on graph = interaction; parallel lines = no interaction

OBSERVATIONAL DESIGNS (cannot establish causation — association only):
- Cross-sectional: one time point; measures PREVALENCE; cheap, fast; cannot determine temporal order
- Prospective cohort: follows group forward; measures INCIDENCE and RELATIVE RISK; can show temporal sequence; expensive
- Case-control: starts with outcome (cases vs controls), looks BACKWARD at exposures; calculates ODDS RATIO; good for rare diseases; vulnerable to recall bias; cannot calculate incidence
- Retrospective cohort: uses historical records; can calculate RR (unlike case-control)
- Naturalistic observation: no manipulation; high ecological validity
- Case study: single individual; rich detail; cannot generalize
- Survey: Likert scales; vulnerable to social desirability, acquiescence, wording effects
- Meta-analysis: statistically combines multiple studies; increases power; concern = publication bias
- Twin/adoption studies: estimate heritability; concordance rates; MZ vs DZ comparison

DECISION TREE:
- Manipulation of IV? → YES: random assignment? YES = RCT; NO = quasi-experimental
- No manipulation? → Observational: one time point (cross-sectional), forward (cohort), disease first (case-control), combines studies (meta-analysis)

CONTROLS & BLINDING:
- Positive control: known to work (proves assay can detect effect)
- Negative control: known not to work (establishes baseline)
- Placebo: inert treatment (controls for placebo effect)
- Single blind: participant blinded (controls placebo, demand characteristics)
- Double blind: participant + researcher (also controls experimenter expectancy)
- Triple blind: + data analyst (also controls analysis bias)
- Counterbalancing: addresses order effects in within-subjects designs

VALIDITY:
- Internal: confidence IV caused DV change; threats: confounding, selection bias, history, maturation, testing effects, attrition, regression to mean
- External: generalizability; threatened by homogeneous samples, lab settings
- Ecological: real-world relevance
- Face: looks like it measures what it claims
- Content: covers full range of construct
- Construct: measures the theoretical concept (convergent + discriminant)
- Criterion: predicts/correlates with related outcomes (predictive vs concurrent)

RELIABILITY:
- Test-retest: stable over time | Inter-rater: consistent between observers
- Internal consistency: Cronbach's α > 0.7 (items measure same construct)
- Reliable but not valid = possible (broken scale always 5 lbs heavy)
- Valid but not reliable = impossible

SAMPLING:
- Probability (generalizable): simple random, stratified (sample FROM subgroups), cluster (sample ENTIRE groups), systematic (every nth)
- Non-probability (not generalizable): convenience, snowball, purposive, quota

ETHICS:
- Informed consent: voluntary, understand risks/benefits, right to withdraw
- IRB: reviews human subjects research BEFORE it begins
- IACUC: animal research oversight
- Deception: must be justified; requires debriefing
- Belmont Report (1979): Respect for persons (autonomy, informed consent) | Beneficence (maximize benefit, minimize harm) | Justice (fair distribution of burdens/benefits)
- Vulnerable populations: children, prisoners, pregnant women, cognitively impaired, economically disadvantaged

BIAS TAXONOMY:
- Hawthorne effect: behavior changes when observed
- Placebo/nocebo: belief in treatment improves/worsens outcomes
- Social desirability: report socially acceptable answers
- Demand characteristics: participants guess hypothesis
- Recall bias: differential memory accuracy (cases remember exposures better)
- Selection bias: groups differ before study starts (fix: randomization)
- Attrition bias: differential dropout between groups
- Lead-time bias: early detection appears to increase survival without improving prognosis
- Publication bias: significant results more likely published
- Experimenter expectancy (Rosenthal effect): researcher unconsciously influences results (fix: double blinding)

HIGH-YIELD RULES:
1. Only RCT with random assignment can establish causation
2. Correlation ≠ causation (third variable + directionality problems)
3. Moderator changes strength/direction; mediator explains mechanism
4. Reliable without valid possible; valid without reliable impossible
5. Randomization controls confounds; blinding controls placebo/expectancy effects
6. Belmont: Respect for persons | Beneficence | Justice
