## 1. Fertilization to Implantation

**Fertilization to implantation:**
1. **Fertilization** -- Occurs in the **ampulla** of the fallopian tube (not the uterus). Sperm undergoes the **acrosome reaction** (releases enzymes to penetrate the zona pellucida). The **cortical reaction** (cortical granule release) hardens the zona pellucida to prevent polyspermy. Meiosis II of the oocyte is completed.
2. **Cleavage** -- Rapid mitotic divisions without growth (cells get smaller). Total embryo size stays roughly the same. Occurs as the embryo travels down the fallopian tube.
3. **Morula** (~day 3-4) -- Solid ball of ~16 cells.
4. **Blastocyst** (~day 5) -- Hollow ball with:
   - **Inner cell mass (embryoblast)** -- Becomes the embryo.
   - **Trophoblast** -- Outer layer, becomes part of the **placenta**. Secretes **hCG** (detected in pregnancy tests).
   - **Blastocoel** -- Fluid-filled cavity.
5. **Implantation** (~day 6-7) -- Blastocyst implants in the endometrium. Trophoblast invades the uterine lining.

## 2. Gastrulation (formation of the three germ layers)

- The most important event in early development. The inner cell mass reorganizes into three distinct layers.
- **Invagination** of cells through the **primitive streak** creates the three layers.

## 3. Germ Layers

| Layer | Becomes | Mnemonic |
|-------|---------|----------|
| **Ectoderm** (outer) | Epidermis (skin), hair, nails, nervous system (brain, spinal cord, neural crest), lens of eye, inner ear, tooth enamel, anterior pituitary, adrenal medulla | **E**ctoderm = **E**pidermis + n**E**rvous system |
| **Mesoderm** (middle) | Muscle (all types), bone, cartilage, connective tissue, cardiovascular system (heart, blood vessels, blood), kidneys, gonads, adrenal cortex, dermis, spleen, notochord | **M**esoderm = **M**uscle |
| **Endoderm** (inner) | Lining of the GI tract, respiratory tract, bladder, liver, pancreas, thyroid, parathyroid, thymus, tonsils | **Endo**derm = **Endo** (inside) = lining of internal organs |

MCAT connection: Neural crest cells (derived from ectoderm, specifically the junction of neural plate and surface ectoderm) migrate throughout the body and give rise to: PNS ganglia, Schwann cells, melanocytes, adrenal medulla, bone/cartilage of the face, odontoblasts (dentin).

## 4. Neurulation

- The **notochord** (from mesoderm) induces the overlying ectoderm to form the **neural plate**.
- Edges of the neural plate rise as **neural folds**, flanking a central depression — the **neural groove**.
- Neural folds meet at the midline and fuse to form the **neural tube** (future brain and spinal cord). Rostral end closes first; caudal end last (~day 28).
- **Neural crest cells** pinch off at the junction and migrate away, becoming PNS ganglia, Schwann cells, melanocytes, adrenal medulla, facial bone/cartilage, and odontoblasts.
- Failure of neural tube closure: anencephaly (rostral end, lethal) or spina bifida (caudal end). Folate prevents both.

**Alar plate vs basal plate (neural tube differentiation):**
- Along the inner wall of the neural tube, a longitudinal groove called the **sulcus limitans** divides each half into two plates.
- **Alar plate (dorsal):** becomes **sensory** neurons of the spinal cord dorsal horn. "Alar = Afferent = Above."
- **Basal plate (ventral):** becomes **motor** neurons of the ventral horn. "Basal = Below = motor."
- This pattern extends up into the brainstem — cranial nerve nuclei follow the same dorsal-sensory / ventral-motor arrangement.
- Provides the structural basis for the **Bell-Magendie law** (dorsal roots sensory, ventral roots motor).

**MCAT pattern — notochord germ-layer trap:** The notochord is **mesoderm**, but it *induces* overlying *ectoderm* to form neural tissue. A common question asks what germ layer the notochord comes from — the answer is **mesoderm**, not ectoderm. Do not confuse the inducer (mesodermal notochord) with the induced (ectodermal neural plate → CNS).

## 5. Organogenesis

Following gastrulation and neurulation, the three germ layers differentiate into specific organs and structures. This occurs during weeks 3-8 (most critical period for teratogen exposure).

## 6. Fetal Circulation

The fetus does not use its lungs for gas exchange -- the **placenta** serves that function. Several shunts bypass the non-functional pulmonary circuit:

- **Umbilical vein** -- Carries oxygenated blood FROM the placenta TO the fetus. (Counterintuitive: this vein carries oxygenated blood.)
- **Ductus venosus** -- Shunts blood from the umbilical vein past the liver directly to the IVC.
- **Foramen ovale** -- Opening in the interatrial septum. Shunts blood from the right atrium to the left atrium, bypassing the lungs.
- **Ductus arteriosus** -- Connects the pulmonary artery to the aorta, diverting blood away from the lungs.
- **Umbilical arteries (2)** -- Carry deoxygenated blood FROM the fetus TO the placenta.

At birth: First breath --> lungs expand --> pulmonary resistance drops --> more blood flows through lungs --> left atrial pressure increases, closing the **foramen ovale** (becomes fossa ovalis). Rising O2 levels and declining prostaglandins cause the **ductus arteriosus** to close (becomes ligamentum arteriosum).

## 7. Gestation and Birth

- hCG from the trophoblast/early embryo maintains the **corpus luteum** until the placenta itself takes over progesterone production around weeks 8–12. Without hCG, the corpus luteum degenerates and pregnancy fails.
- **Labor** is governed by a positive feedback loop: cervical stretch -> oxytocin release from posterior pituitary -> stronger uterine contractions -> more cervical stretch -> more oxytocin. Cycle continues until delivery.
- **Fetal hemoglobin (HbF)** has higher O2 affinity than maternal HbA, allowing the fetus to extract oxygen from the maternal blood across the placenta (curve shifted left; gamma subunits bind 2,3-BPG less effectively).
