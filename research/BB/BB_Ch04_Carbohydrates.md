## 1. Classification

- By number of units: **monosaccharides** (1), **disaccharides** (2), **oligosaccharides** (3–10), **polysaccharides** (>10)
- By carbonyl: **aldoses** (aldehyde at C1) vs **ketoses** (ketone, usually at C2)
- By carbon count: trioses (3C), tetroses (4C), **pentoses** (5C — ribose, deoxyribose), **hexoses** (6C — glucose, galactose, fructose, mannose)
- **D vs L sugars** — determined by the OH on the chiral carbon **farthest from the carbonyl** (C5 in hexoses). D-sugar = OH on the right in Fischer projection. All physiologically relevant sugars are D. (Contrast: L-amino acids, D-sugars.)

## 2. Monosaccharides

- **Epimers** — differ at exactly ONE chiral center (e.g., glucose vs galactose at C4; glucose vs mannose at C2)
- **Anomers** — differ at the anomeric carbon (former carbonyl carbon) after cyclization: α (OH down in D-sugar Haworth) vs β (OH up)
- **Mutarotation** — spontaneous interconversion between α and β forms in solution through the open-chain form. In aqueous solution, glucose is ~63% β, ~37% α, <1% open chain.

## 3. Cyclic Sugars

- Cyclization: carbonyl attacks a hydroxyl within the same molecule → **hemiacetal** (from aldose) or **hemiketal** (from ketose)
- Full acetal/ketal forms upon glycosidic bond formation with another alcohol
- **Pyranose** (6-membered ring, e.g., glucose) vs **furanose** (5-membered ring, e.g., fructose, ribose)
- **Haworth projections** — the anomeric OH is drawn up (β) or down (α) for D-sugars in the standard orientation

## 4. Complex Carbohydrates

### Disaccharides
- **Maltose**: α-1,4 glucose-glucose (from starch digestion)
- **Lactose**: β-1,4 galactose-glucose (milk sugar; requires lactase)
- **Sucrose**: α,β-1,2 glucose-fructose (table sugar; non-reducing — both anomeric carbons are in the glycosidic bond)

### Glycosidic bonds
- **N-linked** (to Asn side chain) vs **O-linked** (to Ser/Thr OH)
- α vs β — determines digestibility (humans have α-glucosidases but lack β-glucosidases to break cellulose)

### Polysaccharides

| Polymer | Linkages | Source/Role |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| **Amylose** | α-1,4 only (linear) | Plant starch — minor component |
| **Amylopectin** | α-1,4 main + α-1,6 branches (~1 branch per 25 residues) | Plant starch — major component |
| **Glycogen** | α-1,4 main + α-1,6 branches (~1 per 8–12 residues; **more branched than starch**) | Animal storage — liver and muscle |
| **Cellulose** | β-1,4 glucose (linear, H-bonded fibers) | Plant cell walls; humans lack enzyme to digest |
| **Chitin** | β-1,4 N-acetylglucosamine | Arthropod exoskeletons, fungal cell walls |

### Reducing vs Non-Reducing Sugars
- **Reducing sugar** = has a free anomeric carbon (free hemiacetal/hemiketal in equilibrium with open chain). Can reduce oxidizing agents.
- **Non-reducing** = anomeric carbon is tied up in a glycosidic bond (e.g., sucrose).
- Classic tests: **Benedict's** (Cu²⁺ → Cu₂O, brick-red precipitate), **Tollens'** (Ag⁺ → Ag mirror), **Fehling's** (Cu²⁺).
- All monosaccharides are reducing. Most disaccharides are reducing (maltose, lactose) EXCEPT sucrose.

## MCAT Tips

- Glycogen branching > starch branching → glycogen can release many glucose molecules quickly (many non-reducing ends for phosphorylase to attack simultaneously).
- Humans cannot digest cellulose (no enzyme for β-1,4 glucose), which is why cellulose is "dietary fiber."
- Distinguish **epimer vs anomer vs enantiomer vs diastereomer** — the MCAT tests this vocabulary precisely. Epimers differ at ONE chiral center (are a subset of diastereomers). Anomers are a SPECIAL TYPE of epimer at the anomeric (former carbonyl) carbon specifically.
