# FC3A: Nervous and Endocrine Systems -- Deep Dive

**Section:** Bio/Biochem (BB) | **Yield:** Very High | **Kaplan:** Biology Ch. 4-5

This is one of the most tested organ system topics on the MCAT. The nervous and endocrine systems show up in standalone BB questions, in Chem/Phys (electrochemistry parallels, membrane potentials), and in Psych/Soc (neurotransmitter pathways, drug mechanisms). Master this and you score points across three sections.

---

## 1. Nervous System Organization

The nervous system divides into two physical compartments and then further by function.

### CNS vs PNS

- **Central Nervous System (CNS):** Brain + spinal cord. This is the integration and processing center. Protected by bone (skull, vertebral column), meninges (dura, arachnoid, pia), and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).
- **Peripheral Nervous System (PNS):** Everything outside the CNS -- 12 pairs of cranial nerves, 31 pairs of spinal nerves, ganglia, and sensory receptors. The PNS is the communication highway carrying signals to and from the CNS.

**MCAT connection:** The blood-brain barrier (BBB) is a CNS-specific feature formed by tight junctions between capillary endothelial cells, reinforced by astrocyte foot processes. It keeps large molecules, pathogens, and many drugs out of the brain. Lipophilic substances (O2, CO2, ethanol, steroid hormones) cross freely. This is why L-DOPA (a dopamine precursor) is given for Parkinson's instead of dopamine itself -- dopamine cannot cross the BBB, but L-DOPA can.

### Functional Divisions of the PNS

The PNS divides by direction and by voluntary control:

**By direction:**
- **Afferent (sensory):** Carries information FROM receptors TO the CNS. Think "A for Arrive" -- signals arrive at the brain.
- **Efferent (motor):** Carries commands FROM the CNS TO effectors (muscles, glands). Think "E for Exit" -- signals exit the brain.

**By voluntary control:**
- **Somatic Nervous System:** Voluntary control of skeletal muscle. One motor neuron runs directly from the CNS to the target muscle. Neurotransmitter at the neuromuscular junction is always **acetylcholine (ACh)** acting on **nicotinic receptors**.
- **Autonomic Nervous System (ANS):** Involuntary control of smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands. Uses a **two-neuron chain** -- preganglionic neuron synapses in a ganglion, then a postganglionic neuron reaches the target organ.

### Sympathetic vs Parasympathetic

This is a guaranteed MCAT topic. Know the anatomy, neurotransmitters, and effects cold.

| Feature | Sympathetic | Parasympathetic |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Nickname** | Fight-or-flight | Rest-and-digest |
| **Spinal origin** | Thoracolumbar (T1-L2) | Craniosacral (brainstem + S2-S4) |
| **Preganglionic fiber** | **Short** (ganglia close to spinal cord) | **Long** (ganglia near or in target organ) |
| **Postganglionic fiber** | **Long** (must travel to target) | **Short** (already near target) |
| **Preganglionic NT** | ACh (nicotinic receptors) | ACh (nicotinic receptors) |
| **Postganglionic NT** | **Norepinephrine (NE)** on adrenergic receptors | **ACh** on muscarinic receptors |
| **Heart rate** | Increases | Decreases |
| **Bronchioles** | Dilate | Constrict |
| **Pupils** | Dilate (mydriasis) | Constrict (miosis) |
| **GI motility** | Decreases | Increases |
| **Blood flow** | Shunted to skeletal muscle | Shunted to viscera |
| **Bladder** | Relaxes (urinary retention) | Contracts (urination) |

**Key rule:** Both divisions use ACh at the preganglionic synapse (nicotinic receptor). They differ at the postganglionic synapse -- sympathetic uses NE (adrenergic), parasympathetic uses ACh (muscarinic).

**Exception -- the adrenal medulla:** The sympathetic preganglionic neuron synapses directly on chromaffin cells of the adrenal medulla. These cells are essentially modified postganglionic neurons that release **epinephrine (80%) and norepinephrine (20%) directly into the bloodstream**. This is the neuro-endocrine bridge -- the sympathetic nervous system triggers a hormonal response. This is high-yield for integrating nervous and endocrine systems.

**Dual innervation:** Most organs receive BOTH sympathetic and parasympathetic input. The two systems generally have opposing effects, and the balance between them determines the organ's activity at any moment. The MCAT loves asking: "If parasympathetic input to the heart increases, what happens to heart rate?" Answer: it decreases, because parasympathetic slows the heart.

**Exception to dual innervation:** Blood vessels mostly receive only sympathetic innervation. Vasodilation occurs primarily by *decreasing* sympathetic tone, not by increasing parasympathetic input.

## 2. Neuron Structure and Types

### Anatomy of a Neuron

- **Cell body (soma):** Contains the nucleus, rough ER (Nissl bodies), and metabolic machinery. This is where proteins and neurotransmitters are synthesized.
- **Dendrites:** Branching extensions that receive incoming signals from other neurons. More dendrites = more input capacity.
- **Axon hillock:** The "trigger zone" where the cell body meets the axon. This is where EPSPs and IPSPs are summed and where an action potential is initiated if threshold is reached. It has the **lowest threshold** because it has the highest density of voltage-gated Na+ channels.
- **Axon:** Long projection that carries the action potential away from the cell body toward the terminal. Can be extremely long (e.g., motor neurons from spinal cord to foot).
- **Myelin sheath:** Fatty insulation around the axon that speeds up signal conduction. Not continuous -- it has gaps.
- **Nodes of Ranvier:** The gaps between myelin segments where voltage-gated ion channels are concentrated. Action potentials regenerate here during saltatory conduction.
- **Synaptic terminal (axon terminal / synaptic bouton):** The endpoint containing synaptic vesicles filled with neurotransmitter. Has voltage-gated Ca2+ channels that trigger vesicle exocytosis.

### Myelination

| Feature | Schwann Cells | Oligodendrocytes |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Location** | PNS | CNS |
| **Coverage** | One Schwann cell wraps ONE segment of ONE axon | One oligodendrocyte myelinates multiple segments on MULTIPLE axons |
| **Regeneration** | PNS neurons CAN regenerate (Schwann cells form a guide tube) | CNS neurons generally CANNOT regenerate |

**Why this matters for MCAT:** Spinal cord injuries (CNS) cause permanent damage because CNS axons do not regenerate effectively. Peripheral nerve injuries (PNS) can heal because Schwann cells guide regrowth. The MCAT may present a passage about axon regeneration research and ask you to apply this distinction.

### Neuron Types by Function

- **Sensory (afferent) neurons:** Carry signals from receptors to the CNS. Many are pseudounipolar (cell body off to the side of a single long axon).
- **Motor (efferent) neurons:** Carry signals from the CNS to muscles/glands. Typically multipolar (many dendrites, one axon).
- **Interneurons:** Found entirely within the CNS. Integrate signals between sensory and motor neurons. Critical for reflexes and complex processing.

**Reflex arcs:** Sensory neuron -> interneuron (in spinal cord) -> motor neuron. This bypasses the brain, which is why reflexes are fast and involuntary. The MCAT tests this in the context of the patellar reflex (which actually skips the interneuron -- monosynaptic stretch reflex: sensory neuron synapses directly on motor neuron).

### Glial Cells (Supporting Cast)

Glia outnumber neurons ~10:1 and are essential for nervous system function. The MCAT tests their roles.

| Glial Cell | Location | Function |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| **Astrocytes** | CNS | Form the BBB (foot processes wrap capillaries), regulate extracellular K+ and neurotransmitter levels, provide metabolic support to neurons |
| **Microglia** | CNS | Immune defense -- phagocytose pathogens and debris (the macrophages of the brain) |
| **Ependymal cells** | CNS | Line the ventricles, produce and circulate CSF |
| **Oligodendrocytes** | CNS | Myelinate CNS axons |
| **Schwann cells** | PNS | Myelinate PNS axons |
| **Satellite cells** | PNS | Support cell bodies in PNS ganglia (analogous to astrocytes) |

## 3. Neural Signaling

This is the single highest-yield subtopic in the nervous system. The MCAT tests it from multiple angles: pure biology, physics (electrical circuits, Nernst equation), and experimental design (patch clamp studies, channel mutations).

### Resting Membrane Potential

A neuron at rest sits at approximately **-70 mV** (inside negative relative to outside). This voltage exists because of:

1. **Na+/K+ ATPase:** Actively pumps **3 Na+ OUT and 2 K+ IN** per ATP hydrolyzed. This creates concentration gradients: high Na+ outside, high K+ inside. It is also directly electrogenic (net +1 charge moved out per cycle), but its main role is maintaining the gradients.

2. **K+ leak channels:** The resting membrane is much more permeable to K+ than to Na+ (~50-100x). K+ flows OUT of the cell down its concentration gradient, carrying positive charge out. This makes the inside more negative.

3. **Large intracellular anions:** Proteins and organic phosphates inside the cell are negatively charged and too large to leave. They contribute to the negative intracellular charge.

The resting potential is closest to the **K+ equilibrium potential** (~-90 mV) because K+ permeability dominates at rest, but it's not quite there because there's a small Na+ leak inward that pulls the voltage slightly positive from -90 to about -70 mV.

### Nernst Equation

The equilibrium potential for a single ion:

**E = (RT/zF) * ln([ion]outside / [ion]inside)**

At body temperature (37C), for a monovalent cation, this simplifies to:

**E = (61.5 mV / z) * log([ion]outside / [ion]inside)**

- For K+: [K+]out is low, [K+]in is high, so log of a fraction < 1 is negative. EK is approximately **-90 mV**.
- For Na+: [Na+]out is high, [Na+]in is low, so log of a number > 1 is positive. ENa is approximately **+60 mV**.

**MCAT application:** If a question says "the membrane becomes more permeable to Na+," the membrane potential moves toward +60 mV (depolarization). If it becomes more permeable to K+, it moves toward -90 mV (hyperpolarization). If Cl- channels open, ECl is approximately -70 to -80 mV, so the effect depends on the current membrane potential -- it could stabilize the resting potential or cause slight hyperpolarization.

The **Goldman equation** accounts for multiple ion permeabilities simultaneously and gives the actual membrane potential. The MCAT rarely asks you to calculate with it, but you should know it exists and that the resting potential reflects the weighted contributions of all permeable ions.

### The Action Potential

This is the "all-or-nothing" electrical signal that travels along an axon. Learn every phase.

**Phase 1: Resting State (-70 mV)**
- Voltage-gated Na+ channels: CLOSED (activation gate closed, inactivation gate open -- ready to fire)
- Voltage-gated K+ channels: CLOSED
- Na+/K+ ATPase: running, maintaining gradients

**Phase 2: Graded Potential and Threshold**
- A stimulus (neurotransmitter binding, sensory input) causes local depolarization
- If the depolarization reaches the axon hillock and hits **threshold (~-55 mV)**, an action potential fires
- Subthreshold stimuli cause only local, graded potentials that decay with distance

**Phase 3: Depolarization (rising phase)**
- Voltage-gated Na+ channels OPEN (activation gate swings open)
- Na+ rushes INTO the cell (down concentration gradient AND toward negative interior -- both driving forces align)
- Membrane potential shoots toward ENa (+60 mV), reaching about **+30 to +40 mV** before Na+ channels inactivate
- This is a **positive feedback loop**: depolarization opens more Na+ channels, which causes more depolarization

**Phase 4: Repolarization (falling phase)**
- Voltage-gated Na+ channels INACTIVATE (the inactivation gate closes, a separate gate from the activation gate -- the channel is now blocked regardless of voltage)
- Voltage-gated K+ channels OPEN (these are slower to respond -- "delayed rectifier" channels)
- K+ rushes OUT of the cell (down its concentration gradient, away from now-positive interior)
- Membrane potential drops back toward resting

**Phase 5: Hyperpolarization (undershoot)**
- K+ channels are slow to close, so K+ continues leaving even after the membrane hits -70 mV
- Membrane potential dips below resting to about **-80 to -90 mV** (closer to EK)
- K+ channels finally close, and the Na+/K+ ATPase restores the original ion distributions
- Membrane returns to -70 mV

### All-or-Nothing Principle

Once threshold is reached, the action potential fires at full amplitude every time. You cannot have a "half" action potential. **Signal intensity is coded by frequency of action potentials, not by amplitude.** A stronger stimulus causes more action potentials per second, not bigger ones.

**MCAT trap:** A question might describe a stimulus that causes a membrane potential change to -60 mV (above threshold of -55 mV). This does NOT fire an action potential -- it must reach -55 mV. But once it reaches -55 mV, whether it got there barely or with a massive stimulus, the AP is the same size.

### Refractory Periods

These limit how fast a neuron can fire and ensure one-way propagation.

- **Absolute refractory period:** Occurs during the time when Na+ channels are **inactivated** (inactivation gate closed). No stimulus, no matter how strong, can trigger another AP. The Na+ channels must first return to the closed-but-ready state (resting state) before they can open again. This ensures the AP propagates in one direction -- the region just behind the AP is in absolute refractory, so the signal cannot travel backward.

- **Relative refractory period:** Occurs during hyperpolarization, when K+ channels are still open. An AP CAN be generated, but only by a **stronger-than-normal stimulus**, because the membrane is farther from threshold. This limits firing frequency.

### Saltatory Conduction

In myelinated neurons, ions can only flow across the membrane at **nodes of Ranvier** (where voltage-gated channels are concentrated). The myelin acts as an insulator, so the current "jumps" from node to node.

**Why it's faster:**
1. Current spreads passively through the myelinated segment very quickly (like electricity through a wire)
2. The AP only needs to be regenerated at the nodes, not continuously along the entire axon
3. Fewer ion channels need to open and close, so less metabolic energy is used

**Conduction velocity depends on:**
- **Myelination:** Myelinated >> unmyelinated
- **Axon diameter:** Larger diameter = faster (less internal resistance, like a wider pipe)
- **Temperature:** Higher temperature = faster (up to a point) -- enzyme and channel kinetics speed up

In unmyelinated fibers, the AP propagates continuously, which is much slower.

**MCAT connection:** Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a demyelinating disease of the CNS. Loss of myelin slows or blocks signal conduction, causing motor weakness, sensory loss, and visual disturbances. Guillain-Barre syndrome is the PNS equivalent.

### Synaptic Transmission

The step-by-step process at a chemical synapse:

1. **AP arrives at the synaptic terminal** (presynaptic neuron)
2. **Depolarization opens voltage-gated Ca2+ channels** in the presynaptic membrane
3. **Ca2+ flows INTO the terminal** (Ca2+ is high outside, low inside -- always)
4. **Ca2+ triggers synaptic vesicle fusion** with the presynaptic membrane (via SNARE proteins -- this is how botulinum toxin works: it cleaves SNAREs and blocks ACh release, causing paralysis)
5. **Neurotransmitter is released into the synaptic cleft** by exocytosis
6. **NT binds to receptors on the postsynaptic membrane**
7. This causes either an **EPSP** (excitatory postsynaptic potential -- depolarization, moves toward threshold) or an **IPSP** (inhibitory postsynaptic potential -- hyperpolarization, moves away from threshold)
8. **NT is removed** from the cleft by: enzymatic degradation (e.g., acetylcholinesterase breaks down ACh), reuptake into the presynaptic neuron (e.g., serotonin reuptake -- this is what SSRIs block), or diffusion away

**Critical detail:** The signal from a single synapse is usually not enough to fire the postsynaptic neuron. The postsynaptic neuron integrates multiple inputs through summation.

### Summation

- **Temporal summation:** Rapid, repeated firing of the SAME presynaptic neuron. EPSPs pile up before the previous one decays, building toward threshold.
- **Spatial summation:** Multiple presynaptic neurons fire at the same time, and their EPSPs (or EPSPs and IPSPs) combine at the axon hillock.

The axon hillock does the math: if the net sum of all EPSPs minus all IPSPs reaches threshold, an AP fires. If not, no AP.

## 4. Neurotransmitters

Know the major ones, their locations, functions, and associated diseases. This crosses into Psych/Soc heavily.

### Acetylcholine (ACh)

- **Where:** Neuromuscular junction (NMJ), all preganglionic autonomic neurons, postganglionic parasympathetic neurons, and within the CNS (basal forebrain -- memory circuits)
- **Receptors:** Nicotinic (ligand-gated ion channel, Na+ influx, fast, at NMJ and ganglia) and Muscarinic (G-protein coupled receptor, slower, at parasympathetic target organs and CNS)
- **Removal:** Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) cleaves ACh into acetate + choline in the cleft. Choline is recycled.
- **Clinical:** Alzheimer's disease involves loss of cholinergic neurons in the basal forebrain. Drugs like donepezil are AChE inhibitors (increase ACh at the synapse). Nerve gases (sarin) and organophosphate pesticides also inhibit AChE -- causing continuous stimulation, muscle paralysis by overstimulation, and death.
- **Myasthenia gravis:** Autoimmune attack on nicotinic ACh receptors at the NMJ -> muscle weakness. Treated with AChE inhibitors.
- **Curare:** Competitive antagonist at nicotinic receptors -> paralysis. Succinylcholine: depolarizing blocker at NMJ -> paralysis (used in surgery).

### Norepinephrine (NE) and Epinephrine (Epi)

- **NE:** Postganglionic sympathetic NT; also a CNS neurotransmitter (locus coeruleus -- arousal, alertness)
- **Epi:** Released as a hormone from the adrenal medulla into the blood (also present in some CNS pathways)
- **Receptors:** Alpha-1 (smooth muscle contraction, vasoconstriction), Alpha-2 (presynaptic inhibition, decreased NE release), Beta-1 (heart -- increases rate and contractility), Beta-2 (bronchodilation, vasodilation in skeletal muscle)
- **Removal:** Reuptake (MAO and COMT degrade catecholamines intracellularly)
- **Synthesis pathway:** Tyrosine -> L-DOPA -> Dopamine -> NE -> Epi (this biosynthetic pathway is high-yield)

### Dopamine (DA)

- **Pathways:**
  - **Mesolimbic:** VTA -> nucleus accumbens. Reward, motivation, pleasure. Overactive in schizophrenia (positive symptoms -- hallucinations, delusions).
  - **Nigrostriatal:** Substantia nigra -> basal ganglia (caudate and putamen). Motor control. Degeneration here causes **Parkinson's disease** (tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia).
  - **Mesocortical:** VTA -> prefrontal cortex. Executive function, working memory. Underactive in schizophrenia (negative symptoms -- flat affect, avolition).
  - **Tuberoinfundibular:** Hypothalamus -> anterior pituitary. Inhibits prolactin release.
- **Clinical connections:** Cocaine blocks dopamine reuptake -> increased DA in synapse -> euphoria, addiction. Amphetamines increase DA release AND block reuptake. Antipsychotics (haloperidol) are D2 receptor antagonists -- they treat positive symptoms but can cause movement side effects (tardive dyskinesia) because they also affect the nigrostriatal pathway.

### Serotonin (5-HT)

- **Where:** Raphe nuclei in the brainstem. Projects widely throughout the brain.
- **Functions:** Mood regulation, sleep, appetite, pain modulation
- **Clinical:** Low serotonin is associated with **depression**. SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors, e.g., fluoxetine/Prozac) block serotonin reuptake, increasing its concentration in the synapse. Also linked to OCD, anxiety, eating disorders.
- **MCAT note:** Serotonin is synthesized from **tryptophan** (an essential amino acid). Melatonin is derived from serotonin (in the pineal gland -- circadian rhythm connection).

### GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid)

- **The main INHIBITORY neurotransmitter in the CNS**
- **Mechanism:** Opens Cl- channels, allowing Cl- to flow INTO the cell, causing hyperpolarization (IPSP)
- **Clinical:** Benzodiazepines (Valium, Xanax) and barbiturates both enhance GABA activity at GABA-A receptors (ligand-gated Cl- channels), but at different binding sites. Alcohol also potentiates GABA. This is why combining benzos + alcohol is dangerous -- additive CNS depression.
- **Huntington's disease:** Loss of GABAergic neurons in the basal ganglia (caudate nucleus) -> hyperkinetic movements (chorea)

### Glutamate

- **The main EXCITATORY neurotransmitter in the CNS**
- **Receptors:** NMDA (also requires glycine co-agonist and membrane depolarization to remove Mg2+ block -- important for learning/memory/LTP), AMPA, kainate
- **Clinical:** Excitotoxicity -- excessive glutamate release (e.g., during stroke) causes overactivation of receptors, Ca2+ overload, and neuronal death

### Endorphins and Enkephalins

- **Endogenous opioid peptides** -- the body's own painkillers
- **Mechanism:** Inhibit pain signaling by binding opioid receptors (mu, delta, kappa)
- **Released during:** Exercise (runner's high), stress, pain
- **Clinical:** Morphine, heroin, and other opioid drugs mimic endorphins at opioid receptors

### Quick Reference Table

| NT | Type | Key Location | Key Function | Disease Link |
|----|------|-------------|--------------|-------------|
| ACh | Excitatory (at NMJ) | NMJ, autonomic ganglia, parasympathetic, basal forebrain | Muscle contraction, parasympathetic effects, memory | Alzheimer's (low), myasthenia gravis |
| NE | Excitatory | Sympathetic postganglionic, locus coeruleus | Fight-or-flight, arousal | Depression (low) |
| Dopamine | Excitatory/modulatory | VTA, substantia nigra | Reward, motor control | Parkinson's (low in nigrostriatal), schizophrenia (high in mesolimbic) |
| Serotonin | Modulatory | Raphe nuclei | Mood, sleep, appetite | Depression (low) |
| GABA | **Inhibitory** | Widely in CNS | Reduces neural activity | Huntington's, anxiety |
| Glutamate | **Excitatory** | Widely in CNS | Main excitatory signal, learning | Excitotoxicity (stroke) |
| Endorphins | Inhibitory (of pain) | Pain pathways | Analgesia, euphoria | Opioid addiction |

## 5. Brain Anatomy

Know the major structures, their functions, and what happens when they're damaged. The MCAT loves lesion studies.

### Cerebral Cortex

The cortex is the outer layer of the cerebrum, divided into four lobes. The cortex is responsible for higher-order thinking, sensory perception, motor commands, and language.

**Frontal Lobe:**
- **Prefrontal cortex:** Executive function (planning, decision-making, personality, impulse control, working memory). Damage causes personality changes (Phineas Gage), poor judgment, impaired planning.
- **Primary motor cortex (precentral gyrus):** Sends motor commands to skeletal muscles. Organized as a motor homunculus -- body parts with finer control (hands, face) have larger cortical representation. Damage causes contralateral paralysis.
- **Broca's area** (inferior frontal gyrus, usually LEFT hemisphere): Speech PRODUCTION. Damage causes Broca's aphasia -- the patient understands language but cannot produce fluent speech. Speech is halting, effortful, and telegraphic ("want...food...now"). Comprehension is intact.

**Parietal Lobe:**
- **Primary somatosensory cortex (postcentral gyrus):** Receives touch, pressure, pain, temperature information. Organized as a sensory homunculus (lips, hands overrepresented). Contralateral representation.
- Also involved in spatial awareness and integrating sensory information.
- Damage to the RIGHT parietal lobe can cause **hemispatial neglect** -- the patient ignores the left side of space.

**Temporal Lobe:**
- **Primary auditory cortex:** Processes sound.
- **Wernicke's area** (superior temporal gyrus, usually LEFT hemisphere): Language COMPREHENSION. Damage causes Wernicke's aphasia -- the patient speaks fluently but the words make no sense ("word salad"). The patient does not understand language and is often unaware of the deficit.
- Also processes memory and emotion (hippocampus and amygdala are in the medial temporal lobe).

**Mnemonic for aphasia:** **B**roca's = **B**roken speech. **W**ernicke's = **W**ordy but wrong.

**Occipital Lobe:**
- **Primary visual cortex:** Processes visual information. Damage causes cortical blindness (eyes work, brain cannot interpret).

**Lateralization:**
- **Left hemisphere:** Language (in most right-handed people), logic, analytical processing
- **Right hemisphere:** Spatial processing, facial recognition, emotional prosody, creativity
- The corpus callosum connects the two hemispheres. Split-brain patients (corpus callosum severed) show lateralization effects on the MCAT.

### Limbic System

The emotional brain. Located deep within the temporal lobes and diencephalon.

- **Amygdala:** Fear, aggression, emotional memory. Assigns emotional significance to stimuli. Damage causes inability to recognize fear in faces, inability to form fear memories (Kluver-Bucy syndrome).
- **Hippocampus:** Formation of new declarative (explicit) memories -- conversion of short-term memory to long-term memory. Does NOT store old memories (those are distributed in the cortex). Damage causes **anterograde amnesia** (can't form new memories) but old memories are intact. Famous case: patient H.M.
- **Hypothalamus:** Master regulator -- see endocrine section below. Controls homeostasis, drives (hunger, thirst, sex, temperature regulation), and the autonomic nervous system. Links nervous and endocrine systems.

### Thalamus

- **Sensory relay station:** ALL sensory information EXCEPT olfaction passes through the thalamus before reaching the cortex. Olfaction goes directly from olfactory bulb to olfactory cortex (this is why smells are so strongly linked to memory -- direct connection to limbic structures).
- Also involved in consciousness, sleep, and alertness.

### Cerebellum

- **Motor coordination, balance, posture, motor learning** (procedural memory like riding a bike)
- Does NOT initiate movement -- it fine-tunes it
- Damage causes **ataxia** (uncoordinated movement, difficulty with balance, slurred speech, intention tremor)
- Located posterior and inferior to the cerebrum, connected via cerebellar peduncles

### Brainstem

Connects the brain to the spinal cord. Contains critical autonomic centers.

- **Midbrain:** Contains the superior and inferior colliculi (visual and auditory reflexes), part of the reticular activating system (RAS -- consciousness and arousal), and the substantia nigra (dopamine for motor control).
- **Pons:** Relay between cerebrum and cerebellum. Contains pneumotaxic and apneustic centers that modulate breathing. Also part of RAS.
- **Medulla oblongata:** Controls **vital functions** -- heart rate, blood pressure, breathing rate, vomiting, swallowing, coughing. Damage to the medulla is often fatal. Contains the cardiovascular center and respiratory rhythm center.

### Basal Ganglia

- Deep cerebral nuclei (caudate, putamen, globus pallidus) involved in **motor initiation and selection**
- Work with the motor cortex to plan and execute voluntary movement
- **Parkinson's disease:** Loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra -> reduced dopamine to basal ganglia -> difficulty initiating movement (bradykinesia), resting tremor, rigidity ("cogwheel rigidity"), shuffling gait
- **Huntington's disease:** Genetic (autosomal dominant, CAG trinucleotide repeat on chromosome 4) loss of GABAergic neurons in the caudate nucleus -> inability to suppress unwanted movement -> chorea (involuntary, dance-like movements), cognitive decline, psychiatric symptoms. Onset typically age 30-50.

## 6. Endocrine System

The endocrine system uses **hormones** -- chemical messengers released into the blood that act on distant target cells. It works alongside the nervous system to maintain homeostasis, but operates on a slower timescale (seconds to hours vs milliseconds).

### Hormone Types: Peptide vs Steroid

This is one of the most important conceptual distinctions on the MCAT. Nearly every hormone question requires you to know this framework.

**Peptide/Protein Hormones (and amino acid derivatives like epinephrine):**
- **Hydrophilic** -- dissolve in blood, travel freely, CANNOT cross the cell membrane
- Bind to **cell-surface receptors** (usually G-protein coupled receptors or receptor tyrosine kinases)
- Act through **second messenger systems** (cAMP, IP3/DAG, Ca2+)
- Effects are **fast** (seconds to minutes) because they activate existing proteins
- Generally have **short half-lives** (degraded quickly)
- Examples: insulin, glucagon, GH, ADH, oxytocin, epinephrine, all anterior pituitary hormones

**Steroid Hormones (and thyroid hormones):**
- **Lipophilic** -- derived from cholesterol (steroids) or tyrosine (thyroid hormones)
- CANNOT dissolve in blood -- travel bound to **carrier proteins** (e.g., thyroid-binding globulin, sex hormone-binding globulin)
- **Cross the cell membrane** freely
- Bind to **intracellular receptors** (cytoplasmic or nuclear receptors)
- The hormone-receptor complex acts as a **transcription factor** -- binds DNA and alters **gene expression**
- Effects are **slow** (hours to days) because they require new protein synthesis
- Generally have **longer half-lives** (carrier proteins protect them from degradation)
- Examples: cortisol, aldosterone, testosterone, estrogen, progesterone, T3/T4, calcitriol (active vitamin D)

**MCAT trap:** Thyroid hormones (T3/T4) are amino acid derivatives but behave like steroid hormones -- they are lipophilic, cross membranes, bind intracellular receptors, and alter gene expression. Epinephrine is also an amino acid derivative but behaves like a peptide hormone -- it is hydrophilic and uses surface receptors. You MUST know these exceptions.

### The Hypothalamic-Pituitary Axis

This is the command center of the endocrine system and the key integration point between the nervous and endocrine systems. The hypothalamus is a neural structure that controls a glandular structure (pituitary). This is high-yield.

**Hypothalamus:** Receives neural input about internal state and external environment. Converts neural signals into hormonal signals.

**Anterior Pituitary (adenohypophysis):**
- NOT directly connected to the hypothalamus by neurons
- Connected via the **hypothalamic-hypophyseal portal system** -- a specialized capillary network that carries releasing/inhibiting hormones directly from the hypothalamus to the anterior pituitary
- The hypothalamus releases hormones into the portal blood, and these hormones stimulate or inhibit anterior pituitary cells
- The anterior pituitary then releases its own hormones into the systemic circulation

**Posterior Pituitary (neurohypophysis):**
- IS a direct neural extension of the hypothalamus
- Hormones (ADH and oxytocin) are MADE in hypothalamic neurons (supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei), travel down axons, and are STORED in the posterior pituitary
- Released directly into the blood from axon terminals when those hypothalamic neurons fire

### Anterior Pituitary Hormones

**Mnemonic: FLAT PIG** -- FSH, LH, ACTH, TSH, Prolactin, Ignore, GH

| Hormone | Hypothalamic Control | Target | Effect |
|---------|---------------------|--------|--------|
| **GH** (Growth Hormone / Somatotropin) | GHRH stimulates; Somatostatin inhibits | Liver, bone, muscle | Stimulates IGF-1 release from liver -> growth. Increases blood glucose (diabetogenic). Promotes protein synthesis. |
| **TSH** (Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone) | TRH stimulates | Thyroid gland | Stimulates T3/T4 synthesis and release |
| **ACTH** (Adrenocorticotropic Hormone) | CRH stimulates | Adrenal cortex | Stimulates cortisol (and some aldosterone/androgen) release |
| **FSH** (Follicle-Stimulating Hormone) | GnRH stimulates | Gonads | Females: follicle development, estrogen production. Males: spermatogenesis (acts on Sertoli cells) |
| **LH** (Luteinizing Hormone) | GnRH stimulates | Gonads | Females: ovulation trigger, corpus luteum formation, progesterone production. Males: testosterone production (acts on Leydig cells) |
| **Prolactin** | Dopamine INHIBITS (tonic inhibition); TRH stimulates | Mammary glands | Milk production. Only anterior pituitary hormone under primarily inhibitory control. |

**Clinical connection:** Prolactin is tonically inhibited by dopamine. If the pituitary stalk is severed (cutting off hypothalamic input), all anterior pituitary hormones DECREASE except prolactin, which INCREASES because its inhibitory signal is lost. This is a classic MCAT question.

### Posterior Pituitary Hormones

| Hormone | Made in | Stimulus | Target | Effect |
|---------|---------|----------|--------|--------|
| **ADH** (Antidiuretic Hormone / Vasopressin) | Supraoptic nucleus of hypothalamus | High blood osmolarity, low blood volume | Kidney collecting ducts | Inserts aquaporin channels -> water reabsorption -> concentrated urine, also causes vasoconstriction at high levels |
| **Oxytocin** | Paraventricular nucleus of hypothalamus | Cervical stretch (labor), nipple stimulation (nursing) | Uterus, mammary glands | Uterine contractions during labor, milk letdown during nursing |

**ADH clinical connections:**
- **Diabetes insipidus (central):** Low ADH production -> massive dilute urine, dehydration, extreme thirst
- **SIADH:** Excess ADH -> too much water retention -> hyponatremia (low blood sodium)
- Alcohol inhibits ADH -> increased urination -> dehydration (hangover mechanism)

### Thyroid Gland

**T3 (triiodothyronine) and T4 (thyroxine):**
- Made from **tyrosine + iodine** in thyroid follicular cells
- T4 is the main circulating form; T3 is the **active form** (T4 is converted to T3 in peripheral tissues)
- Act like steroid hormones: cross membranes, bind nuclear receptors, alter gene expression
- **Increase basal metabolic rate (BMR)** -- increase O2 consumption, heat production, heart rate, and alertness
- Essential for normal growth and brain development in children (congenital hypothyroidism = cretinism)
- **Regulation:** Hypothalamus (TRH) -> Anterior pituitary (TSH) -> Thyroid (T3/T4) -> negative feedback on hypothalamus and pituitary

**Hyperthyroidism (Graves' disease):** Autoimmune -- antibodies mimic TSH and continuously stimulate the thyroid. Symptoms: weight loss, heat intolerance, increased HR, exophthalmos (bulging eyes), anxiety. TSH is LOW (negative feedback from high T3/T4).

**Hypothyroidism (Hashimoto's thyroiditis):** Autoimmune destruction of thyroid tissue. Symptoms: weight gain, cold intolerance, fatigue, bradycardia, myxedema. TSH is HIGH (pituitary tries to stimulate a failing thyroid).

**Calcitonin:**
- Produced by **parafollicular C cells** of the thyroid
- **Lowers blood Ca2+** by inhibiting osteoclast activity (reduces bone resorption) and promoting Ca2+ excretion in kidneys
- Clinically less important than PTH but tested on the MCAT
- Remember: **calciTONin TONes down calcium**

### Parathyroid Glands

Four small glands embedded in the posterior thyroid.

**PTH (Parathyroid Hormone):**
- Released when blood Ca2+ is **LOW**
- **Raises blood Ca2+** through three mechanisms:
  1. **Bone resorption:** Stimulates osteoclasts (indirectly, via osteoblasts) to break down bone and release Ca2+ and PO4^3-
  2. **Kidney reabsorption:** Increases Ca2+ reabsorption in the distal convoluted tubule (DCT); decreases phosphate reabsorption (phosphate excretion increases -- this prevents CaPO4 precipitation)
  3. **Vitamin D activation:** Stimulates the kidney enzyme 1-alpha-hydroxylase, which converts 25-hydroxyvitamin D to **1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (calcitriol)**, the active form. Calcitriol then increases Ca2+ absorption from the intestine.

**Mnemonic:** PTH does three things -- it acts on **B**one, **K**idney, and (via vitamin D) **I**ntestine. Think "PTH Brings Kalcium In."

**Calcium homeostasis summary:**
- Low Ca2+ -> PTH up, calcitonin down -> Ca2+ rises
- High Ca2+ -> PTH down, calcitonin up -> Ca2+ falls
- Vitamin D is the third player -- facilitates intestinal Ca2+ absorption

### Adrenal Glands

Each adrenal gland has two distinct regions that are functionally different organs.

**Adrenal Cortex (outer):** Produces steroid hormones from cholesterol. Three zones (remember: **GFR -- salt, sugar, sex**):

| Zone | Hormone | Function |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Zona Glomerulosa** | **Aldosterone** (mineralocorticoid) | Increases Na+ reabsorption and K+ secretion in kidney collecting duct -> water follows Na+ -> increases blood volume and BP. Regulated by RAAS (renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system) and blood K+ levels. |
| **Zona Fasciculata** | **Cortisol** (glucocorticoid) | Increases blood glucose (gluconeogenesis, protein catabolism, lipolysis). Anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive. Released in response to stress via CRH -> ACTH. Chronically elevated cortisol = Cushing's syndrome. |
| **Zona Reticularis** | **DHEA** (androgen) | Weak androgen, converted to testosterone/estrogen peripherally. Contributes to secondary sex characteristics, especially in females. |

**Cortisol and stress:** CRH (hypothalamus) -> ACTH (anterior pituitary) -> Cortisol (adrenal cortex). Cortisol feeds back negatively on both the hypothalamus and pituitary. Cortisol follows a diurnal rhythm (highest in the morning). Chronic stress -> chronically elevated cortisol -> immune suppression, muscle wasting, hyperglycemia, visceral fat deposition.

**Aldosterone and RAAS:** Low blood pressure -> kidney releases renin -> renin converts angiotensinogen (from liver) to angiotensin I -> ACE (in lungs) converts angiotensin I to angiotensin II -> angiotensin II causes vasoconstriction AND stimulates aldosterone release -> aldosterone causes Na+/water retention -> BP increases. ACE inhibitors are anti-hypertension drugs that block this cascade.

**Adrenal Medulla (inner):** This is the nervous-endocrine crossover point.

- Modified sympathetic postganglionic neurons (chromaffin cells)
- Release **epinephrine (~80%) and norepinephrine (~20%)** directly into the blood
- Triggered by sympathetic preganglionic neurons (using ACh)
- Effects: increased heart rate, bronchodilation, glycogenolysis, lipolysis, blood shunted to muscles
- These hormones produce the same effects as direct sympathetic stimulation but through the bloodstream, so effects are more widespread and longer-lasting

### Pancreas (Endocrine Function)

The pancreas is both exocrine (digestive enzymes) and endocrine. The endocrine portion consists of the **islets of Langerhans**.

| Cell Type | Hormone | Stimulus | Effect |
|-----------|---------|----------|--------|
| **Beta cells** | **Insulin** | High blood glucose (fed state) | Promotes glucose uptake (GLUT4 in muscle/adipose), glycogenesis, lipogenesis, protein synthesis. LOWERS blood glucose. |
| **Alpha cells** | **Glucagon** | Low blood glucose (fasted state) | Promotes glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis, lipolysis, ketogenesis in the liver. RAISES blood glucose. |
| **Delta cells** | Somatostatin | Various | Inhibits both insulin and glucagon release (paracrine regulator) |

**Insulin is the only hormone that lowers blood glucose.** Multiple hormones raise it (glucagon, cortisol, epinephrine, GH). This makes sense from a survival perspective -- hypoglycemia is more immediately dangerous than hyperglycemia.

**Diabetes mellitus:**
- **Type 1:** Autoimmune destruction of beta cells -> no insulin production. Requires exogenous insulin. Onset typically in youth.
- **Type 2:** Insulin resistance -> cells don't respond to insulin. Beta cells compensate by producing more insulin initially, but eventually burn out. Associated with obesity and lifestyle. Most common form.

**MCAT integration:** After a meal, blood glucose rises -> beta cells release insulin -> insulin binds receptor tyrosine kinase on target cells -> GLUT4 transporters are inserted into the membrane of muscle and adipose tissue -> glucose enters cells -> blood glucose falls. In the fasted state, glucagon dominates -> liver breaks down glycogen and makes new glucose -> blood glucose is maintained for the brain (which uses GLUT1 and GLUT3, insulin-independent transporters).

### Gonads

- **Testes:** Produce **testosterone** (Leydig cells, stimulated by LH) and support spermatogenesis (Sertoli cells, stimulated by FSH). Testosterone: male secondary sex characteristics, anabolic effects (muscle, bone growth), sex drive, spermatogenesis. Inhibin B (from Sertoli cells) feeds back to inhibit FSH.
- **Ovaries:** Produce **estrogen** (granulosa cells, stimulated by FSH) and **progesterone** (corpus luteum, stimulated by LH). Estrogen: female secondary sex characteristics, endometrial proliferation, bone density. Progesterone: endometrial maintenance, pregnancy support.

### Pineal Gland

- Produces **melatonin** (derived from serotonin, which is derived from tryptophan)
- Regulated by light/dark cycles via the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus
- Melatonin increases in darkness -> promotes sleep
- Key for **circadian rhythm** regulation

### Feedback Mechanisms

**Negative feedback (most hormones):**
The product of a hormonal cascade inhibits upstream components. Example: T3/T4 inhibit TRH and TSH release. Cortisol inhibits CRH and ACTH. This maintains homeostasis.

**Positive feedback (rare but tested):**
The product amplifies the stimulus. Examples:
- **Oxytocin during labor:** Uterine contractions -> cervical stretch -> more oxytocin -> stronger contractions -> more stretch -> cycle continues until delivery
- **LH surge at ovulation:** Rising estrogen from the mature follicle eventually triggers a POSITIVE feedback response (only at high sustained levels) -> LH surge -> ovulation
- **Blood clotting cascade:** Activated clotting factors activate more clotting factors

**MCAT rule:** If a question asks "what happens when you administer exogenous hormone X," think negative feedback. Exogenous cortisol -> ACTH drops. Exogenous testosterone -> LH and FSH drop (basis of male hormonal contraception). Exogenous T4 -> TSH drops.

## 7. Nervous-Endocrine Integration: The Big Picture

The MCAT rewards students who can connect these two systems. Here are the key integration points:

1. **Hypothalamus is the bridge:** It's a neural structure that controls the pituitary gland (endocrine). It receives neural input (temperature, stress, circadian signals) and converts it to hormonal output.

2. **Sympathetic -> adrenal medulla:** Preganglionic sympathetic neurons (neural) stimulate chromaffin cells to release epinephrine and NE (hormonal) into the blood. This amplifies and prolongs the fight-or-flight response beyond what direct sympathetic innervation alone can achieve.

3. **Stress response integration:** Acute stress activates BOTH the sympathetic nervous system (immediate: increased HR, vasoconstriction, pupil dilation) AND the HPA axis (minutes: CRH -> ACTH -> cortisol for sustained metabolic support). The nervous system handles the first seconds; the endocrine system sustains the response.

4. **Blood glucose regulation:** Sympathetic activation stimulates glucagon release and inhibits insulin release (alpha-adrenergic effect on beta cells). Epinephrine directly promotes glycogenolysis in muscle and liver. Cortisol promotes gluconeogenesis. All systems converge to ensure glucose is available during stress.

5. **Circadian rhythms:** The SCN (hypothalamic nucleus) receives light input from retinal ganglion cells (neural) and controls melatonin release from the pineal gland (endocrine) and cortisol rhythm via the HPA axis.

6. **ADH and blood pressure:** Baroreceptors in the carotid sinus and aortic arch (neural) detect low blood pressure -> signal to the hypothalamus -> ADH release from posterior pituitary (endocrine) -> water retention -> blood volume and pressure increase.

## High-Yield Summary: What to Memorize

- **Sympathetic:** thoracolumbar, short pre/long post, NE on targets, fight-or-flight
- **Parasympathetic:** craniosacral, long pre/short post, ACh on targets, rest-and-digest
- **Resting potential:** -70 mV, maintained by Na+/K+ ATPase (3 out, 2 in) and K+ leak
- **AP sequence:** Rest -> threshold (-55) -> depolarization (Na+ in) -> repolarization (K+ out) -> hyperpolarization -> rest
- **Absolute refractory:** Na+ channels inactivated, cannot fire. **Relative refractory:** hyperpolarized, need stronger stimulus.
- **Saltatory conduction:** Myelin insulates, AP jumps between nodes of Ranvier
- **Synapse:** AP -> Ca2+ influx -> vesicle fusion -> NT release -> EPSP or IPSP
- **Anterior pituitary:** GH, TSH, ACTH, FSH, LH, Prolactin (portal system from hypothalamus)
- **Posterior pituitary:** ADH and oxytocin (made in hypothalamus, stored/released from posterior pit)
- **Peptide hormones:** hydrophilic, surface receptors, second messengers, fast
- **Steroid hormones:** lipophilic, carrier proteins in blood, intracellular receptors, gene expression, slow
- **PTH raises Ca2+** (bone resorption, kidney reabsorption, vitamin D activation); **calcitonin lowers Ca2+**
- **Insulin lowers glucose** (only one that does); glucagon, cortisol, epinephrine, GH all raise it
- **Adrenal cortex:** GFR = salt (aldosterone), sugar (cortisol), sex (DHEA)
- **Adrenal medulla:** epi and NE into blood (sympathetic nervous -> endocrine bridge)
- **Prolactin:** only anterior pituitary hormone under tonic inhibition (by dopamine); stalk cut -> prolactin goes UP, everything else goes DOWN
- **Negative feedback:** most hormones. Positive feedback: oxytocin in labor, LH surge at ovulation.
