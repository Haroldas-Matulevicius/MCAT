# FC3B: Organ System Structure and Function

> **Scope:** All major organ systems except nervous and endocrine (covered separately). This is one of the most heavily tested areas on the Bio/Biochem section. Know the anatomy, the physiology, AND the reasoning behind each mechanism.

## 1. Respiratory System

### 1.1 Anatomy: Nasal Cavity to Alveoli

Air moves through a conducting zone (no gas exchange) before reaching the respiratory zone (gas exchange happens here).

**Conducting zone (in order):**

- **Nasal cavity** -- Warms, humidifies, and filters incoming air. Mucus traps particles; turbinates (conchae) increase surface area for conditioning. This is why breathing through the nose is more effective at air preparation than mouth breathing.
- **Pharynx** -- Shared passageway for air and food. Divided into nasopharynx, oropharynx, laryngopharynx.
- **Larynx** -- Contains the vocal cords. The **epiglottis** folds over the laryngeal opening during swallowing to prevent aspiration. This is a cartilaginous structure.
- **Trachea** -- Rigid tube kept open by C-shaped cartilage rings (open side faces posteriorly toward the esophagus, allowing it to expand during swallowing). Lined with ciliated pseudostratified columnar epithelium -- the **mucociliary escalator** moves trapped debris upward toward the pharynx.
- **Bronchi** -- Trachea bifurcates into left and right main bronchi (right is shorter, wider, more vertical -- aspirated objects more commonly lodge here). Bronchi branch into secondary (lobar) and tertiary (segmental) bronchi.
- **Bronchioles** -- Smaller airways lacking cartilage. Smooth muscle in their walls allows bronchoconstriction and bronchodilation (parasympathetic = constrict, sympathetic = dilate). **Terminal bronchioles** are the last purely conducting structures.

**Respiratory zone:**

- **Respiratory bronchioles** -- First site where some gas exchange occurs (scattered alveoli in walls).
- **Alveolar ducts and alveolar sacs** -- Lead to clusters of **alveoli**, the primary gas exchange units.
- **Alveoli** -- Tiny, thin-walled sacs (~300 million in both lungs) with enormous total surface area (~70 m^2). Two key cell types:
  - **Type I pneumocytes** -- Thin, flat cells forming most of the alveolar surface. Gas exchange occurs across these cells. They are squamous epithelial cells optimized for diffusion (very thin barrier).
  - **Type II pneumocytes** -- Cuboidal cells that secrete **surfactant** (dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine, a phospholipid). Surfactant reduces surface tension, preventing alveolar collapse (atelectasis) during expiration. Without surfactant, smaller alveoli would collapse into larger ones (LaPlace's law: P = 2T/r, so smaller radius means higher collapsing pressure for the same surface tension).

MCAT connection: Premature infants often lack sufficient surfactant, leading to neonatal respiratory distress syndrome (NRDS). Surfactant production typically matures by ~35 weeks gestation.

### 1.2 Ventilation Mechanics

Breathing is driven by **pressure gradients** between the atmosphere and the alveoli. This connects directly to **Boyle's law**: P1V1 = P2V2. When volume increases, pressure decreases (and vice versa).

**Inspiration (active process):**
1. The **diaphragm contracts** (flattens downward) and the **external intercostals contract** (lift the rib cage up and out).
2. Thoracic cavity volume increases.
3. By Boyle's law, intrapleural and intra-alveolar pressures drop below atmospheric pressure.
4. Air flows into the lungs down the pressure gradient.

**Expiration (passive at rest):**
1. The diaphragm and external intercostals relax.
2. Elastic recoil of the lungs and chest wall decreases thoracic volume.
3. Intra-alveolar pressure rises above atmospheric pressure.
4. Air flows out.

**Forced expiration** recruits the internal intercostals and abdominal muscles (active process).

**Key pressures:**
- **Intra-alveolar (intrapulmonary)** -- Pressure inside the alveoli. Fluctuates above and below atmospheric during breathing.
- **Intrapleural** -- Pressure in the pleural space (between visceral and parietal pleura). Always negative relative to atmospheric and intra-alveolar at rest. This negative pressure keeps the lungs inflated. A **pneumothorax** (air entering the pleural space) equalizes pressures and causes lung collapse.
- **Transpulmonary pressure** = intra-alveolar minus intrapleural. Must remain positive to keep lungs expanded.

### 1.3 Lung Volumes and Capacities

Know these cold. They are commonly tested in passage-based calculations.

**Volumes (4, non-overlapping):**
- **Tidal Volume (TV)** -- Volume of air inhaled/exhaled in a normal breath (~500 mL).
- **Inspiratory Reserve Volume (IRV)** -- Additional volume that can be inhaled beyond TV (~3000 mL).
- **Expiratory Reserve Volume (ERV)** -- Additional volume that can be forcefully exhaled beyond TV (~1200 mL).
- **Residual Volume (RV)** -- Volume remaining in lungs after maximum exhalation (~1200 mL). Cannot be measured by spirometry (requires body plethysmography or helium dilution).

**Capacities (combinations of volumes):**
- **Vital Capacity (VC)** = TV + IRV + ERV (~4700 mL) -- Maximum air that can be moved in one breath.
- **Total Lung Capacity (TLC)** = VC + RV (~5900 mL) -- Total air the lungs can hold.
- **Functional Residual Capacity (FRC)** = ERV + RV (~2400 mL) -- Air remaining after a normal exhalation.
- **Inspiratory Capacity (IC)** = TV + IRV (~3500 mL).

MCAT connection: In obstructive diseases (asthma, COPD), RV and FRC increase (air trapping). In restrictive diseases (fibrosis), all volumes decrease because the lungs cannot fully expand.

### 1.4 Gas Exchange

Gas exchange occurs by **simple diffusion** driven by **partial pressure gradients**. This connects to **Dalton's law**: the total pressure of a gas mixture equals the sum of the partial pressures of each component (P_total = P1 + P2 + ...).

**At the alveoli (external respiration):**
- Alveolar PO2 (~100 mmHg) > deoxygenated blood PO2 (~40 mmHg) --> O2 diffuses into blood.
- Deoxygenated blood PCO2 (~46 mmHg) > alveolar PCO2 (~40 mmHg) --> CO2 diffuses into alveoli.

**At the tissues (internal respiration):**
- Blood PO2 (~100 mmHg) > tissue PO2 (~40 mmHg) --> O2 diffuses into tissues.
- Tissue PCO2 (~46 mmHg) > blood PCO2 (~40 mmHg) --> CO2 diffuses into blood.

The rate of diffusion is governed by **Fick's law**: rate is proportional to (surface area x solubility x partial pressure difference) / (membrane thickness). CO2 is ~20x more soluble than O2, so it diffuses more efficiently despite a smaller pressure gradient.

### 1.5 Oxygen-Hemoglobin Dissociation Curve (VERY HIGH-YIELD)

This is one of the most tested curves on the entire MCAT. Know it inside and out.

**Hemoglobin structure:** A tetramer with 4 subunits (2 alpha, 2 beta in adult HbA), each containing a heme group with an Fe2+ ion that binds one O2. Total: 4 O2 per hemoglobin.

**Sigmoidal (S-shaped) curve:** This shape reflects **cooperative binding**. Binding the first O2 is hardest (hemoglobin starts in the **T-state** / tense state, low affinity). Each subsequent O2 binding shifts the molecule toward the **R-state** (relaxed state, high affinity), making subsequent binding easier. This creates the steep middle portion of the curve.

**Reading the curve:**
- X-axis: PO2 (mmHg)
- Y-axis: % hemoglobin saturation
- At PO2 = 100 mmHg (lungs): ~98% saturated -- hemoglobin loads efficiently.
- At PO2 = 40 mmHg (tissues): ~75% saturated -- hemoglobin unloads ~25% of its O2.
- At PO2 = 26 mmHg: 50% saturated -- this is the **P50** value. Shifts in the curve change P50.

**RIGHT SHIFT (decreased affinity, increased unloading to tissues):**
Mnemonic: Think of exercising muscle -- it needs MORE O2 delivered.
- **Increased temperature** -- Active muscles generate heat.
- **Increased CO2** -- Active metabolism produces CO2.
- **Increased H+ (decreased pH)** -- This is the **Bohr effect**. CO2 + H2O <-> H2CO3 <-> H+ + HCO3-. More CO2 means more H+. The H+ binds to hemoglobin, stabilizing the T-state, promoting O2 release. This is physiologically elegant: tissues that are most metabolically active (highest CO2 and H+) get the most O2 delivered.
- **Increased 2,3-BPG** (2,3-bisphosphoglycerate) -- Produced by RBCs during glycolysis (Rapoport-Luebering shunt). 2,3-BPG binds in the central cavity of deoxyhemoglobin, stabilizing the T-state. Levels increase at high altitude (chronic hypoxia), improving O2 delivery to tissues.

**LEFT SHIFT (increased affinity, decreased unloading):**
- **Fetal hemoglobin (HbF)** -- Has 2 alpha + 2 gamma subunits. The gamma subunits bind 2,3-BPG less effectively, so HbF has a higher O2 affinity than adult HbA. This allows the fetus to "steal" O2 from maternal blood across the placenta. On the curve, HbF is shifted LEFT.
- **Carbon monoxide (CO) poisoning** -- CO binds hemoglobin with ~200x the affinity of O2. It locks the remaining subunits in the R-state, dramatically increasing affinity for the remaining O2. The result: hemoglobin holds onto O2 and refuses to release it to tissues. Patient suffocates even with "bound" O2. The curve shifts LEFT and flattens.
- Decreased temperature, decreased CO2, decreased H+ (increased pH), decreased 2,3-BPG.

### 1.6 CO2 Transport

CO2 is transported in blood in three forms:

1. **Dissolved CO2** (~7%) -- Directly dissolved in plasma. This is what is measured as PCO2.
2. **Carbaminohemoglobin** (~23%) -- CO2 binds directly to amino groups on hemoglobin (not to the heme iron -- that is where O2 and CO bind). Deoxyhemoglobin binds CO2 more readily than oxyhemoglobin (**Haldane effect**: deoxygenation of blood enhances CO2 loading).
3. **Bicarbonate (HCO3-)** (~70%) -- The dominant form. The reaction: **CO2 + H2O <-> H2CO3 <-> H+ + HCO3-**. This is catalyzed by **carbonic anhydrase** (found inside RBCs). The H2CO3 (carbonic acid) is an unstable intermediate that rapidly dissociates.

**The Chloride Shift:**
When HCO3- is produced inside the RBC, it is transported out into the plasma via the **HCO3-/Cl- antiporter** (band 3 protein). Cl- moves into the RBC to maintain electrical neutrality. This is the **chloride shift**. At the lungs, the process reverses: HCO3- re-enters the RBC, is converted back to CO2, and CO2 is exhaled.

MCAT connection: The Bohr effect and Haldane effect work synergistically. At the tissues: CO2 loading promotes O2 unloading (Bohr). O2 unloading promotes CO2 loading (Haldane). At the lungs: opposite.

## 2. Cardiovascular System

### 2.1 Heart Anatomy and Blood Flow

**Chambers and flow path (follow the blood):**

Deoxygenated blood returns via **superior and inferior vena cava** --> **right atrium** --> through **tricuspid valve** (right AV valve) --> **right ventricle** --> through **pulmonary valve** (semilunar) --> **pulmonary arteries** --> lungs (gas exchange) --> **pulmonary veins** --> **left atrium** --> through **mitral (bicuspid) valve** (left AV valve) --> **left ventricle** --> through **aortic valve** (semilunar) --> **aorta** --> systemic circulation.

Key points:
- **Arteries** carry blood AWAY from the heart (not always oxygenated -- pulmonary arteries carry deoxygenated blood).
- **Veins** carry blood TOWARD the heart (pulmonary veins carry oxygenated blood).
- The left ventricle has the thickest wall because it pumps against the highest pressure (systemic circulation).
- **Chordae tendineae** and **papillary muscles** anchor the AV valve leaflets, preventing prolapse (inversion) during ventricular systole.

### 2.2 Cardiac Cycle

The cardiac cycle consists of **systole** (contraction) and **diastole** (relaxation).

**Phases in detail:**

1. **Atrial systole** -- Atria contract, pushing the last ~20% of blood into ventricles (ventricles are already ~80% filled passively). This corresponds to the P wave on the ECG.
2. **Isovolumetric contraction** -- Ventricles begin contracting. AV valves snap shut (both AV and semilunar valves are closed). Volume stays constant while pressure rises rapidly. AV valve closure produces the **first heart sound (S1 = "lub")**.
3. **Ventricular ejection** -- Ventricular pressure exceeds arterial pressure. Semilunar valves open. Blood is ejected into the aorta and pulmonary artery. This corresponds to the QRS complex and early T wave.
4. **Isovolumetric relaxation** -- Ventricles relax. Semilunar valves snap shut (both sets of valves again closed). Volume stays constant while pressure drops. Semilunar valve closure produces the **second heart sound (S2 = "dub")**. The dicrotic notch on the aortic pressure tracing marks this moment.
5. **Passive ventricular filling** -- AV valves open. Blood flows passively from atria into ventricles. This accounts for ~80% of ventricular filling.

**Heart sounds summary:**
- **S1** = AV valves close (beginning of systole, "lub")
- **S2** = semilunar valves close (beginning of diastole, "dub")
- Murmurs indicate turbulent flow through abnormal valves (stenosis = narrowed, regurgitation = leaky).

### 2.3 Cardiac Output

**CO = HR x SV** (Cardiac Output = Heart Rate x Stroke Volume)

- **Heart rate** is modulated by the autonomic nervous system. Sympathetic (norepinephrine on beta-1 receptors) increases HR. Parasympathetic (ACh on muscarinic receptors via the vagus nerve) decreases HR.
- **Stroke volume** depends on three factors:
  - **Preload** -- Degree of ventricular stretch at end of diastole (related to venous return). **Frank-Starling mechanism**: increased preload --> increased stretch --> increased force of contraction --> increased SV. Up to a point -- overstretching leads to decreased function (heart failure).
  - **Contractility** (inotropy) -- Intrinsic strength of contraction independent of preload. Sympathetic stimulation and circulating epinephrine increase contractility. Digitalis increases contractility by raising intracellular Ca2+.
  - **Afterload** -- Pressure the ventricle must overcome to eject blood (essentially systemic vascular resistance for the left ventricle). Increased afterload (e.g., hypertension, aortic stenosis) decreases SV because the heart must work harder to push blood out.

### 2.4 Conduction System

The heart generates its own electrical impulses (**autorhythmicity / automaticity**). No external nerve input is needed for the heart to beat -- the nervous system only modulates rate.

**Pathway:**
1. **SA node** (sinoatrial node) -- The pacemaker. Located in the right atrial wall. Fires at ~60-100 bpm. Depolarization spreads across the atria, causing atrial contraction (P wave on ECG).
2. **AV node** (atrioventricular node) -- Located at the junction of atria and ventricles. Introduces a ~0.1 sec **delay** to allow atria to finish emptying before ventricles contract (PR interval on ECG).
3. **Bundle of His** -- Conducts impulse from AV node into the interventricular septum.
4. **Bundle branches** -- Left and right branches carry the signal down either side of the septum.
5. **Purkinje fibers** -- Rapidly distribute the signal throughout the ventricular myocardium, causing coordinated ventricular contraction from apex to base (QRS complex on ECG).

MCAT connection: Pacemaker cells have **funny channels** (HCN channels) that allow slow Na+ leak during diastole, causing gradual depolarization to threshold. This is different from the stable resting potential of contractile myocytes.

### 2.5 Blood Pressure Regulation

**Mean Arterial Pressure (MAP)** = CO x Total Peripheral Resistance (TPR). MAP is approximately diastolic + 1/3(systolic - diastolic).

**Short-term regulation: Baroreceptors**
- Located in the carotid sinus and aortic arch.
- Detect stretch (increased BP --> more stretch --> increased firing).
- High BP: baroreceptors fire more --> brainstem increases parasympathetic output and decreases sympathetic --> HR and TPR decrease --> BP drops.
- Low BP: baroreceptors fire less --> opposite response --> BP increases.

- **Vasovagal syncope (clinical):** Sudden parasympathetic surge (triggered by emotional shock, sight of blood, prolonged standing) -> vagally mediated bradycardia + vasodilation -> BP drop -> fainting. Classic baroreflex-related clinical scenario; often used as MCAT passage hook.

**Long-term regulation: RAAS (Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System)**
1. Low blood pressure/volume detected by **juxtaglomerular (JG) cells** in the kidney.
2. JG cells release **renin** into the blood.
3. Renin cleaves **angiotensinogen** (from liver) to **angiotensin I**.
4. **ACE** (angiotensin-converting enzyme, in lung capillaries) converts angiotensin I to **angiotensin II**.
5. Angiotensin II effects:
   - Potent **vasoconstriction** (raises TPR immediately)
   - Stimulates **aldosterone** release from the adrenal cortex --> Na+ and water reabsorption in the kidney --> increases blood volume
   - Stimulates **ADH** release from the posterior pituitary --> water reabsorption
   - Stimulates thirst

**ANP (Atrial Natriuretic Peptide):**
- Released by atrial cardiomyocytes when stretched by high blood volume.
- Promotes Na+ and water excretion (natriuresis and diuresis).
- Opposes RAAS -- reduces blood volume and BP.

### 2.6 Vessel Types

| Vessel | Key Features | Function |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| **Arteries** | Thick walls, elastic tissue | Conduct blood under high pressure; elastic recoil maintains pressure during diastole |
| **Arterioles** | Thick smooth muscle layer | **Major site of resistance regulation** (TPR). Vasodilation/constriction controls blood flow to capillary beds |
| **Capillaries** | Single endothelial cell layer, very thin | Gas, nutrient, and waste exchange. **Slowest flow velocity** (largest total cross-sectional area) |
| **Venules** | Thin walls | Collect blood from capillaries |
| **Veins** | Thin walls, contain **valves** | Return blood to heart. Low pressure system. Valves prevent backflow. Skeletal muscle pump and respiratory pump assist venous return |

**Starling forces at capillaries:** Fluid movement across capillary walls depends on the balance of hydrostatic pressure (pushes fluid out) and oncotic/osmotic pressure (pulls fluid in, due to plasma proteins like albumin). At the arteriolar end, hydrostatic > oncotic --> fluid filters out. At the venular end, oncotic > hydrostatic --> fluid reabsorbs. Net slight excess of filtration is collected by lymphatics.

### 2.7 Blood Composition and Coagulation

**Blood composition:**
- **Plasma** (~55%) -- Water, proteins (albumin, globulins, fibrinogen), electrolytes, dissolved gases, nutrients, wastes.
- **Formed elements** (~45%) -- RBCs (99%), WBCs, platelets. The percentage of blood volume occupied by RBCs is the **hematocrit**.

**Red blood cells (erythrocytes):**
- **Biconcave disc shape** -- Maximizes surface area for gas exchange.
- **No nucleus, no mitochondria, no organelles** -- More room for hemoglobin; rely entirely on anaerobic glycolysis for ATP (cannot consume the O2 they carry).
- Lifespan: ~120 days. Produced in bone marrow (**erythropoiesis**), regulated by **erythropoietin (EPO)** from the kidneys.
- Old RBCs are recycled in the **spleen** (and liver). Heme --> biliverdin --> bilirubin (processed by liver, excreted in bile).

**Coagulation cascade (simplified, know the big picture):**
1. **Vascular spasm** -- Injured vessel constricts to reduce blood flow.
2. **Platelet plug formation** -- Platelets adhere to exposed collagen (via von Willebrand factor), activate, release ADP and thromboxane A2, recruit more platelets. This is the **primary hemostatic plug**.
3. **Coagulation cascade** -- Series of serine protease activations (intrinsic and extrinsic pathways converge on the **common pathway**). End result: **prothrombin --> thrombin** (via factor Xa). Thrombin converts **fibrinogen --> fibrin**. Fibrin forms a mesh that stabilizes the platelet plug (secondary hemostasis). Factor XIII cross-links fibrin strands.
4. **Fibrinolysis** -- **Plasmin** (from plasminogen) eventually dissolves the clot after tissue repair.

### 2.8 Blood Types

**ABO system:**
- Determined by glycoprotein antigens on the RBC surface.
- **Type A**: A antigen on RBCs, anti-B antibodies in plasma.
- **Type B**: B antigen on RBCs, anti-A antibodies in plasma.
- **Type AB**: Both antigens, no antibodies. **Universal recipient** (can receive from anyone because no antibodies to attack donor cells).
- **Type O**: No antigens, both anti-A and anti-B antibodies. **Universal donor** (no antigens to be attacked by recipient).

**Genetics:** The ABO gene has three alleles -- I^A, I^B, i. I^A and I^B are **codominant** to each other. Both are **dominant** over i (recessive). This is a classic MCAT genetics problem.

**Rh factor:**
- Rh+ = has D antigen. Rh- = lacks D antigen.
- Rh- mother carrying Rh+ fetus can develop anti-Rh antibodies after first exposure (usually at delivery). Subsequent Rh+ pregnancies risk **erythroblastosis fetalis** (hemolytic disease of the newborn). Prevented by **RhoGAM** (anti-D immunoglobulin) given to Rh- mothers.

### 2.9 Fetal Circulation

The fetus does not use its lungs for gas exchange -- the **placenta** serves that function. Several shunts bypass the non-functional pulmonary circuit:

- **Umbilical vein** -- Carries oxygenated blood FROM the placenta TO the fetus. (Counterintuitive: this vein carries oxygenated blood.)
- **Ductus venosus** -- Shunts blood from the umbilical vein past the liver directly to the IVC.
- **Foramen ovale** -- Opening in the interatrial septum. Shunts blood from the right atrium to the left atrium, bypassing the lungs.
- **Ductus arteriosus** -- Connects the pulmonary artery to the aorta, diverting blood away from the lungs.
- **Umbilical arteries (2)** -- Carry deoxygenated blood FROM the fetus TO the placenta.

At birth: First breath --> lungs expand --> pulmonary resistance drops --> more blood flows through lungs --> left atrial pressure increases, closing the **foramen ovale** (becomes fossa ovalis). Rising O2 levels and declining prostaglandins cause the **ductus arteriosus** to close (becomes ligamentum arteriosum).

## 3. Immune System (HIGH-YIELD)

### 3.1 Innate Immunity

The innate immune system provides **immediate, nonspecific** defense. No memory is formed. It is the first line of defense.

**Physical and chemical barriers:**
- Skin (keratinized epithelium), mucous membranes, stomach acid (pH ~2), lysozyme (in tears, saliva), defensins (antimicrobial peptides), normal flora (competitive exclusion).

**Cellular components:**
- **Neutrophils** -- Most abundant WBC. **First responders** to infection. Phagocytes. Short-lived. Release reactive oxygen species and form neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs).
- **Macrophages** -- Derived from monocytes. Phagocytose pathogens and dead cells. **Antigen-presenting cells (APCs)** -- process and present antigens on **MHC II** to helper T cells. Release cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-1, IL-6) to amplify inflammation.
- **Dendritic cells** -- The most effective APCs. Found in tissues exposed to the environment (skin, mucosal lining). Capture antigens, migrate to lymph nodes, present on MHC II to activate T cells. Bridge between innate and adaptive immunity.
- **Natural killer (NK) cells** -- Lymphocytes of the innate system (no antigen-specific receptors). Kill virus-infected cells and tumor cells by detecting the absence of MHC I ("missing self" hypothesis). Release **perforin** (pore-forming) and **granzymes** (trigger apoptosis).
- **Mast cells** -- Found in connective tissues. Release **histamine** (vasodilation, increased permeability) and other mediators. Key players in allergic responses.
- **Eosinophils** -- Combat parasitic infections. Also involved in allergic inflammation.
- **Basophils** -- Release histamine and heparin. Rare in blood.

**Complement system:**
A cascade of plasma proteins (C1-C9) activated by three pathways (classical, lectin, alternative). All converge on C3 --> C3a + C3b.
- **C3b** -- Opsonization (tags pathogens for phagocytosis).
- **C3a, C5a** -- Anaphylatoxins (trigger inflammation, recruit immune cells).
- **C5b-C9** -- Form the **membrane attack complex (MAC)**, which punches holes in pathogen membranes causing lysis.

**Inflammation cascade:**
1. Tissue damage/infection --> mast cells release histamine --> **vasodilation** + **increased capillary permeability**.
2. Fluid, proteins, and immune cells move from blood to tissue (edema = swelling).
3. Neutrophils arrive first (attracted by chemokines), then macrophages.
4. Classic signs: **redness** (rubor), **heat** (calor), **swelling** (tumor), **pain** (dolor), loss of function.

**Interferons:**
- Released by virus-infected cells. Alert neighboring cells to upregulate antiviral defenses (degrade viral RNA, inhibit protein synthesis). They do NOT directly kill the virus -- they signal danger.
- Type I interferons (IFN-alpha, IFN-beta) -- Antiviral.
- Type II interferon (IFN-gamma) -- Activates macrophages and NK cells.

**Fever:**
- Pyrogens (exogenous from pathogens or endogenous like IL-1, IL-6, TNF) act on the hypothalamus to raise the body temperature set point.
- Benefit: many pathogens grow optimally at 37 degrees C; elevated temperature enhances immune cell activity and inhibits pathogen replication.

### 3.2 Adaptive Immunity -- Humoral (B Cell-Mediated)

**Humoral immunity** targets **extracellular** pathogens (bacteria in blood, toxins, free viruses before they enter cells). The effectors are **antibodies**.

**B cell activation and clonal selection:**
1. Each B cell expresses a unique **B cell receptor (BCR)**, which is a membrane-bound antibody.
2. When a B cell encounters its matching antigen, it binds and internalizes it.
3. The B cell presents processed antigen on **MHC II** to a **CD4+ helper T cell** (T_H2 specifically). The helper T cell provides a second signal (cytokines like IL-4, IL-5).
4. The activated B cell undergoes **clonal expansion** (rapid proliferation) and differentiates into:
   - **Plasma cells** -- Antibody-secreting factories. Short-lived but produce massive amounts of antibodies.
   - **Memory B cells** -- Long-lived. Enable faster, stronger secondary response upon re-exposure.
5. This is **clonal selection theory**: only B cells with receptors matching the antigen are selected and expanded.

**Antibody (immunoglobulin) structure:**
- Y-shaped protein with 2 **heavy chains** and 2 **light chains**, held together by disulfide bonds.
- **Variable region (Fab)** -- The tips of the Y. Antigen-binding site. Unique to each antibody -- determines specificity.
- **Constant region (Fc)** -- The stem of the Y. Determines antibody class/isotype. Recognized by immune cell receptors and complement.

**Five antibody classes:**

| Class | Features | Function |
|-------|----------|----------|
| **IgG** | Most abundant in blood. Monomer. **Only class that crosses the placenta** | Opsonization, complement activation, neutralization. Provides passive immunity to newborns |
| **IgM** | Pentamer (5 units joined by J chain). First produced in a primary response | Excellent at agglutination and complement activation. Used for blood typing |
| **IgA** | Dimer in secretions (joined by J chain and secretory component). Found in mucus, saliva, breast milk, tears | Mucosal immunity. Prevents pathogen attachment to epithelial surfaces. Protects newborns via breast milk |
| **IgE** | Monomer. Very low serum concentration | Binds mast cells and basophils. Triggers histamine release. Involved in **allergic reactions** and defense against **parasites** |
| **IgD** | Monomer. Found on surface of mature naive B cells | Functions as a B cell receptor. Role in B cell activation (not well understood) |

Mnemonic for class switching order: **MADGE** (IgM first, then IgA, IgD, IgG, IgE -- though this is simplified; class switching is driven by cytokines from helper T cells).

### 3.3 Adaptive Immunity -- Cell-Mediated (T Cell-Mediated)

**Cell-mediated immunity** targets **intracellular** pathogens (viruses inside cells, intracellular bacteria) and abnormal self-cells (cancer, transplant).

**T cell maturation:**
- T cell precursors originate in the **bone marrow** but mature in the **thymus**.
- In the thymus, T cells undergo **positive selection** (must recognize self-MHC) and **negative selection** (must NOT strongly react to self-antigens). T cells that fail either are eliminated. This establishes **self-tolerance**.

**Major T cell types:**

**CD4+ Helper T cells (T_H):**
- Recognize antigens presented on **MHC II** (found on **APCs**: macrophages, dendritic cells, B cells).
- Do NOT directly kill anything. They coordinate the immune response by releasing **cytokines**.
  - T_H1 --> activate macrophages and CD8+ cells (cell-mediated)
  - T_H2 --> activate B cells (humoral response)
  - T_H17 --> recruit neutrophils, fight extracellular bacteria/fungi

**CD8+ Cytotoxic T cells (T_C):**
- Recognize antigens presented on **MHC I** (found on **all nucleated cells**).
- Directly kill infected or abnormal cells by releasing **perforin** (creates pores) and **granzymes** (activate apoptosis).
- This is why virus-infected cells and cancer cells are targeted: they display abnormal peptides on MHC I.

### 3.4 MHC I vs MHC II

This distinction is extremely high-yield and commonly tested.

| Feature | MHC I | MHC II |
|---------|-------|--------|
| **Found on** | ALL nucleated cells (+ platelets). NOT on RBCs | Only on **APCs** (macrophages, dendritic cells, B cells) |
| **Presents** | Endogenous antigens (proteins made INSIDE the cell -- viral proteins, tumor antigens, self-peptides) | Exogenous antigens (proteins taken in from OUTSIDE via phagocytosis or endocytosis) |
| **Recognized by** | **CD8+** cytotoxic T cells | **CD4+** helper T cells |
| **Function** | Allows immune system to detect and kill infected/abnormal self-cells | Allows APCs to "show" what they have engulfed and activate the adaptive response |

Think of it this way: MHC I is a "window" into what every cell is making. If a cell is making viral proteins, MHC I displays them, and CD8+ T cells come kill that cell. MHC II is a "report" from professional immune cells about what they have found in the environment.

### 3.5 Active vs Passive Immunity

- **Active immunity** -- The individual's own immune system generates the response. Memory cells are formed.
  - **Natural active**: Infection.
  - **Artificial active**: Vaccination.
  - Slow onset, long-lasting.
- **Passive immunity** -- Preformed antibodies are transferred. No memory cells formed.
  - **Natural passive**: Maternal IgG across placenta, IgA in breast milk.
  - **Artificial passive**: Injection of antiserum/antitoxin (e.g., rabies immunoglobulin).
  - Immediate protection, short-lived (antibodies degrade).

### 3.6 Hypersensitivity Reactions

| Type | Name | Mechanism | Timing | Examples |
|------|------|-----------|--------|----------|
| **I** | Immediate/Anaphylactic | **IgE** binds mast cells/basophils --> histamine release | Seconds to minutes | Allergies, anaphylaxis, asthma (allergic), hay fever |
| **II** | Cytotoxic | **IgG/IgM** antibodies bind antigens on cell surfaces --> complement or NK cell destruction | Hours | Hemolytic transfusion reactions, hemolytic disease of newborn, autoimmune hemolytic anemia |
| **III** | Immune complex | **Antigen-antibody complexes** deposit in tissues --> complement activation --> inflammation | Hours | Serum sickness, lupus (SLE), rheumatoid arthritis |
| **IV** | Delayed-type | **T cell** mediated (no antibodies) | 24-72 hours | TB skin test (PPD), contact dermatitis (poison ivy), transplant rejection |

Key: Types I-III are antibody-mediated. Type IV is cell-mediated. Type IV is the only one that takes days.

## 4. Digestive System

### 4.1 Mechanical and Chemical Digestion by Location

**Oral cavity:**
- Mechanical: **Mastication** (chewing) breaks food into smaller pieces, increasing surface area.
- Chemical: **Salivary amylase** (ptyalin) begins **starch digestion** (hydrolyzes alpha-1,4 glycosidic bonds). Optimal pH ~7. Inactivated by stomach acid.
- **Lingual lipase** (from serous glands at the back of the tongue) begins fat digestion. Works in the stomach at low pH.

**Esophagus:**
- Conducts food via **peristalsis** (coordinated smooth muscle contractions). No significant digestion occurs here.
- **Lower esophageal sphincter (LES)** prevents reflux. Dysfunction --> GERD.

**Autonomic regulation of GI motility:**
- **Parasympathetic** (mainly via the **vagus nerve**) -> increased peristalsis, increased glandular secretions, increased overall digestive activity. "Rest and digest."
- **Sympathetic** (via **splanchnic nerves** from thoracolumbar spinal cord) -> decreased peristalsis, decreased secretions, blood shunted away from gut to skeletal muscle and heart. "Fight or flight" -- digestion is a low priority during stress.

**Stomach:**
- Mechanical: **Churning** by muscular walls (three muscle layers: oblique, circular, longitudinal) mixes food with gastric juices, producing **chyme**.
- Chemical:
  - **Chief cells** secrete **pepsinogen** (inactive zymogen). Activated to **pepsin** by HCl (acid) in the stomach lumen. Pepsin is an endopeptidase that begins **protein digestion**. Optimal pH ~2.
  - **Parietal cells** secrete **HCl** (creates acidic environment for pepsin, kills bacteria) and **intrinsic factor** (required for vitamin B12 absorption in the ileum). H+/K+ ATPase (proton pump) on apical surface.
  - **G cells** secrete **gastrin** (hormone) into blood.
  - **Mucous cells** secrete mucus and bicarbonate to protect the stomach lining from acid autodigestion.
  - **D cells** secrete somatostatin (inhibits gastrin release).
- The stomach does NOT absorb much (some water, alcohol, aspirin).

**Small intestine** (major site of digestion AND absorption):
Three regions: **duodenum** (most digestion), **jejunum** (most absorption), **ileum** (B12 and bile salt absorption).

- **Pancreatic enzymes** (released into the duodenum):
  - **Trypsinogen** --> activated to **trypsin** by **enterokinase/enteropeptidase** (brush border enzyme). Trypsin then activates other zymogens (chymotrypsinogen --> chymotrypsin, procarboxypeptidase --> carboxypeptidase, proelastase --> elastase). This cascade prevents premature activation (autodigestion of the pancreas = pancreatitis).
  - **Pancreatic lipase** -- Digests triglycerides into 2 fatty acids + monoglyceride. Requires **colipase** (anchors lipase to lipid droplet) and **bile salts** (emulsify large fat globules into smaller droplets, increasing surface area).
  - **Pancreatic amylase** -- Continues starch digestion.
  - **Nucleases** -- Digest nucleic acids.
  - **Sodium bicarbonate** -- Secreted by pancreatic duct cells. Neutralizes acidic chyme entering the duodenum, raising pH to ~7-8 (optimal for pancreatic enzymes).

- **Brush border enzymes** (on microvilli of enterocytes):
  - Disaccharidases: maltase, sucrase, lactase (break disaccharides into monosaccharides).
  - Peptidases: aminopeptidase, dipeptidase (final protein digestion).
  - Enterokinase/enteropeptidase (activates trypsinogen).

**Large intestine (colon):**
- Absorbs **water, electrolytes (especially Na+), and vitamins** (K and some B vitamins produced by gut bacteria).
- Bacteria ferment undigested material.
- Forms and stores feces. No significant enzymatic digestion.

### 4.2 Digestive Hormones

| Hormone | Source | Trigger | Actions |
|---------|--------|---------|---------|
| **Gastrin** | G cells (stomach antrum) | Peptides/amino acids in stomach, stomach distension, vagal stimulation | Stimulates HCl secretion by parietal cells, stimulates gastric motility, promotes mucosal growth |
| **Secretin** | S cells (duodenum) | Acidic chyme entering duodenum (low pH) | Stimulates pancreatic **bicarbonate** secretion, inhibits gastric acid secretion, stimulates bile secretion from liver |
| **CCK** (cholecystokinin) | I cells (duodenum/jejunum) | Fatty acids and amino acids in duodenum | Stimulates pancreatic **enzyme** secretion, stimulates **gallbladder contraction** (bile release), stimulates bile duct relaxation (sphincter of Oddi), inhibits gastric emptying, promotes satiety |

Mnemonic: **S**ecretin handles the **S**odium bicarbonate (acid neutralization). **C**CK handles **C**ontraction of gallbladder and enzyme release.

**GIP** (gastric inhibitory peptide/glucose-dependent insulinotropic peptide): Released by K cells of the duodenum in response to glucose and fat. Decreases gastric motility and stimulates insulin release.

### 4.3 Liver Functions

The liver is a metabolic powerhouse with diverse functions:

- **Bile production** -- Bile salts (made from cholesterol) emulsify fats. Bile is stored and concentrated in the **gallbladder** between meals, then released into the duodenum via the common bile duct.
- **Detoxification** -- Metabolizes drugs, alcohol, and toxins (cytochrome P450 enzymes). Converts ammonia (NH3, toxic) to **urea** via the **urea cycle** for excretion by kidneys.
- **Gluconeogenesis** -- Synthesizes glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors (amino acids, lactate, glycerol) during fasting.
- **Glycogen storage and glycogenolysis** -- Stores glucose as glycogen after meals; breaks it down between meals to maintain blood glucose.
- **Plasma protein synthesis** -- Albumin (oncotic pressure), clotting factors (fibrinogen, prothrombin), complement proteins.
- **Cholesterol and lipoprotein synthesis** -- Produces VLDL; converts cholesterol to bile salts.
- **First-pass metabolism** -- Blood from the GI tract goes to the liver via the **hepatic portal vein** before reaching systemic circulation. Drugs taken orally are partially metabolized by the liver before reaching target tissues (reducing bioavailability).
- **Bilirubin processing** -- Conjugates bilirubin (from heme breakdown) for excretion in bile.
- **Storage** -- Vitamins (A, D, B12, iron).

### 4.4 Absorption

Surface area maximization is critical: **circular folds** (plicae circulares) > **villi** > **microvilli** (brush border).

- **Carbohydrates**: Absorbed as monosaccharides (glucose, fructose, galactose). Glucose and galactose via Na+-dependent cotransport (SGLT1). Fructose via facilitated diffusion (GLUT5). All exit basolateral side via GLUT2.
- **Proteins**: Absorbed as amino acids, dipeptides, and tripeptides. Amino acids via Na+-dependent cotransport and facilitated diffusion.
- **Fats**: Fatty acids and monoglycerides enter enterocytes by simple diffusion. Inside, they are re-esterified to triglycerides and packaged with cholesterol, phospholipids, and apolipoproteins into **chylomicrons**. Chylomicrons are too large for blood capillaries, so they enter the **lacteals** (lymphatic capillaries in villi) and eventually reach the blood via the thoracic duct.
- **Water**: Absorbed throughout the small and large intestines by osmosis.
- **Iron**: Absorbed in the duodenum. Stored as ferritin. Transported in blood bound to transferrin.
- **Vitamin B12**: Requires intrinsic factor (from parietal cells) for absorption in the terminal ileum.

## 5. Renal/Excretory System (HIGH-YIELD)

### 5.1 Nephron Structure

The nephron is the functional unit of the kidney. Each kidney contains ~1 million nephrons.

**Components (in order of fluid flow):**

1. **Glomerulus** -- A tuft of fenestrated capillaries (within Bowman's capsule). Blood enters via the **afferent arteriole** and exits via the **efferent arteriole**. High hydrostatic pressure forces fluid (filtrate) through the filtration barrier.
   - **Filtration barrier**: fenestrated capillary endothelium + basement membrane + podocyte foot processes (with filtration slits). Allows passage of water, ions, glucose, amino acids, urea, small molecules. Blocks cells, large proteins (albumin normally does NOT pass).

2. **Bowman's capsule** -- Cup-like structure that collects filtrate. The filtrate here is essentially protein-free plasma.

3. **Proximal convoluted tubule (PCT)** -- The workhorse of reabsorption. Reabsorbs ~65-70% of filtrate.
   - Reabsorbs: glucose (100%, via SGLT), amino acids, Na+ (Na+/K+ ATPase on basolateral side drives it), HCO3-, water (obligatory, follows solutes), urea (passive), phosphate, K+.
   - Secretes: H+, organic acids, drugs (penicillin), toxins.
   - Has **brush border** (microvilli) for high surface area and abundant **mitochondria** for active transport.
   - Water reabsorption here is NOT regulated by hormones -- it is obligatory and constitutive.

4. **Loop of Henle** -- Creates the **medullary concentration gradient** necessary for urine concentration.
   - **Descending limb**: Permeable to **water**, impermeable to solutes. Water leaves by osmosis as the tubule descends into the hypertonic medulla. Filtrate becomes more concentrated.
   - **Thin ascending limb**: Impermeable to water, passively permeable to NaCl (NaCl diffuses out).
   - **Thick ascending limb**: Impermeable to water, actively transports NaCl out (Na+/K+/2Cl- cotransporter, the target of **loop diuretics** like furosemide). Dilutes the filtrate while making the medulla more concentrated.

5. **Distal convoluted tubule (DCT)** -- Fine-tuning of reabsorption and secretion.
   - Reabsorbs Na+ and Ca2+.
   - **Aldosterone** acts here (and on collecting duct): stimulates Na+ reabsorption and K+ secretion.
   - **PTH** (parathyroid hormone) stimulates Ca2+ reabsorption here.
   - Secretes H+ and K+.
   - Contains the **macula densa** cells (part of juxtaglomerular apparatus) -- senses NaCl concentration in filtrate and signals JG cells to adjust renin release.

6. **Collecting duct** -- Final concentration of urine.
   - **ADH** (antioxidant diuretic hormone, from posterior pituitary) inserts **aquaporin-2 channels** into the apical membrane, allowing water reabsorption. Without ADH, the collecting duct is impermeable to water, and dilute urine is produced (diabetes insipidus).
   - **Aldosterone** also acts here (Na+ reabsorption, K+ secretion).
   - Passes through the hypertonic medulla -- if ADH is present, water follows the osmotic gradient out of the duct, concentrating the urine.
   - Also involved in H+ secretion (acid-base balance) via intercalated cells.

### 5.2 The Three Renal Processes

| Process | Where | What Happens |
|---------|-------|--------------|
| **Filtration** | Glomerulus | Pressure-driven bulk flow of plasma (minus proteins and cells) into Bowman's capsule. GFR ~125 mL/min (~180 L/day). We reabsorb 99% of this. |
| **Reabsorption** | PCT (most), Loop, DCT, collecting duct | Useful substances move from tubular lumen back into peritubular capillaries. Can be passive (water, urea) or active (glucose, amino acids, Na+). |
| **Secretion** | PCT, DCT, collecting duct | Substances move from peritubular capillaries into the tubular lumen. Important for K+, H+, drugs, toxins. This is the mechanism for fine-tuning composition. |

**Amount excreted = Amount filtered - Amount reabsorbed + Amount secreted**

### 5.3 Countercurrent Multiplier Mechanism

This is conceptually tricky but very high-yield. The purpose is to create an **osmotic gradient** in the medulla (300 mOsm at cortex to ~1200 mOsm at inner medulla) that allows the collecting duct to produce concentrated urine.

**How it works:**
1. The thick ascending limb actively pumps NaCl out into the medullary interstitium (but is impermeable to water). This makes the medulla salty.
2. The descending limb is permeable to water -- water leaves into the now-hypertonic medulla, concentrating the filtrate inside.
3. As concentrated filtrate rounds the hairpin turn and enters the ascending limb, it has more NaCl to pump out.
4. This positive feedback loop "multiplies" the gradient along the length of the loop.
5. The **vasa recta** (peritubular capillaries running parallel to the Loop of Henle in a hairpin) act as **countercurrent exchangers** -- they follow the gradient passively, picking up solutes on the way down and losing them on the way up. This removes reabsorbed water without washing out the gradient.

**Urea recycling** also contributes: ADH increases urea permeability in the inner medullary collecting duct, allowing urea to be deposited in the medullary interstitium (accounts for ~50% of the medullary osmolality).

### 5.4 RAAS in Detail (Renal Perspective)

1. Decreased renal perfusion, decreased NaCl at macula densa, or sympathetic activation --> **juxtaglomerular (JG) cells** (modified smooth muscle of afferent arteriole) release **renin**.
2. Renin cleaves angiotensinogen (from liver) --> angiotensin I.
3. ACE (in lungs) converts angiotensin I --> **angiotensin II**.
4. Angiotensin II:
   - Constricts **efferent arteriole** preferentially --> maintains GFR despite low blood pressure.
   - Systemic vasoconstriction --> increases blood pressure.
   - Stimulates **aldosterone** from adrenal cortex --> Na+ reabsorption in DCT/collecting duct --> water follows --> blood volume increases.
   - Stimulates ADH release and thirst.
   - Stimulates Na+ reabsorption in PCT directly.

### 5.5 ADH Mechanism

- **Stimulus**: High plasma osmolarity detected by osmoreceptors in the hypothalamus, or low blood volume/pressure detected by baroreceptors.
- **Release**: From the **posterior pituitary** (synthesized in hypothalamus, transported to posterior pituitary).
- **Action**: Binds V2 receptors on collecting duct cells --> activates cAMP/PKA signaling --> **aquaporin-2 channels** are inserted into the apical membrane via exocytosis.
- **Result**: Water is reabsorbed from the collecting duct into the hypertonic medullary interstitium --> concentrated urine, diluted blood.
- **Alcohol inhibits ADH release** --> dilute urine --> dehydration (hangover mechanism).

### 5.6 Acid-Base Balance

The kidneys are critical for long-term pH regulation (the bicarbonate buffer system and respiratory compensation are faster but limited).

**Acidosis (blood pH too low):**
- Kidneys secrete more H+ into the tubular lumen (intercalated cells in collecting duct).
- Kidneys reabsorb more HCO3- (primarily in PCT, using carbonic anhydrase).
- Kidneys generate new HCO3- by metabolizing glutamine --> NH4+ (excreted) + HCO3- (returned to blood).

**Alkalosis (blood pH too high):**
- Kidneys secrete less H+.
- Kidneys excrete HCO3- (reduce reabsorption).

MCAT connection: Understand how the kidneys compensate for respiratory acid-base disorders. Respiratory acidosis (high CO2, low pH) --> kidneys retain HCO3- and secrete H+. Respiratory alkalosis (low CO2, high pH) --> kidneys excrete HCO3-.

### 5.7 Renal Clearance

**Clearance (C)** = the volume of plasma completely cleared of a substance per unit time.

**C = (U x V) / P**

Where:
- U = urine concentration of the substance (mg/mL)
- V = urine flow rate (mL/min)
- P = plasma concentration of the substance (mg/mL)

**Key benchmark: Inulin** (or creatinine as a clinical proxy)
- Inulin is freely filtered, not reabsorbed, not secreted.
- Therefore, **inulin clearance = GFR** (~125 mL/min).

**Interpreting clearance:**
- Clearance of substance X > GFR --> substance is filtered AND secreted (e.g., PAH at low concentrations).
- Clearance of substance X < GFR --> substance is filtered AND reabsorbed (e.g., glucose, amino acids, Na+).
- Clearance of substance X = GFR --> substance is only filtered (e.g., inulin).
- Clearance = 0 --> substance is completely reabsorbed (e.g., glucose at normal plasma levels).

**PAH (para-aminohippuric acid):**
- At low concentrations, PAH is completely cleared from plasma (filtered + secreted).
- PAH clearance approximates **renal plasma flow (RPF)** (~625 mL/min).
- **Filtration fraction (FF)** = GFR / RPF (~20%). About 20% of plasma is filtered at the glomerulus.

## 6. Musculoskeletal System

### 6.1 Sarcomere Structure

The sarcomere is the functional unit of contraction, bounded by two **Z-lines** (Z-discs).

| Structure | Composition | What Happens During Contraction |
|-----------|------------|-------------------------------|
| **Z-line** | Anchoring protein disc where thin filaments attach | Z-lines move CLOSER together (sarcomere shortens) |
| **I-band** | Region of thin filaments ONLY (light band) | **SHORTENS** (thin filaments slide inward) |
| **A-band** | Entire length of thick filaments (dark band) | **STAYS THE SAME** (thick filaments don't change length) |
| **H-zone** | Region of thick filaments ONLY (center of A-band) | **SHORTENS** (thin filaments slide over thick filaments into this zone) |
| **M-line** | Center of sarcomere, anchors thick filaments | Stays in the center |

Mnemonic: **H**-zone and **I**-band get shorter. **A**-band stays the same. Think "HI" shrinks.

- **Thin filaments**: Actin (with tropomyosin and troponin complexes).
- **Thick filaments**: Myosin (with globular heads that contain ATPase activity).
- **Titin**: Giant elastic protein connecting the Z-line to the M-line. Provides passive elasticity and prevents over-stretching.

### 6.2 Sliding Filament Theory (Step by Step)

The thick and thin filaments do not change length. Instead, they **slide past each other**, shortening the sarcomere.

**Cross-bridge cycling:**
1. **ATP binds myosin** --> myosin detaches from actin (rigor is released). Without ATP, myosin stays locked to actin (explains rigor mortis).
2. **ATP is hydrolyzed** to ADP + Pi --> myosin head is "cocked" into the high-energy position.
3. **Myosin binds actin** (forming a cross-bridge) -- this can only occur when tropomyosin has moved off the binding sites (see below).
4. **Power stroke** --> Pi is released, then ADP is released. The myosin head pivots, pulling the thin filament toward the M-line (sarcomere shortens).
5. Myosin remains bound to actin until a new ATP binds --> cycle repeats.

**Regulation (tropomyosin-troponin system):**
- At rest, **tropomyosin** covers the myosin-binding sites on actin, preventing cross-bridge formation.
- **Troponin** is a complex of three subunits:
  - **TnC** -- Binds Ca2+ (the regulatory trigger).
  - **TnI** -- Inhibitory subunit (holds tropomyosin in the blocking position).
  - **TnT** -- Binds tropomyosin.
- When **Ca2+** binds TnC, the troponin complex changes conformation, pulling tropomyosin out of the way, exposing the binding sites on actin.

### 6.3 Excitation-Contraction Coupling

This is the process linking the nerve signal to muscle contraction.

1. Motor neuron releases **ACh** at the **neuromuscular junction** (NMJ).
2. ACh binds nicotinic receptors on the motor end plate --> Na+ influx --> **depolarization** of the sarcolemma (muscle cell membrane).
3. **Action potential** propagates along the sarcolemma and down the **T-tubules** (transverse tubules -- invaginations of the sarcolemma that penetrate deep into the muscle fiber).
4. T-tubule depolarization activates **DHP receptors** (voltage-sensitive, on T-tubule membrane), which are mechanically coupled to **ryanodine receptors** (Ca2+ release channels on the **sarcoplasmic reticulum** / SR).
5. Ryanodine receptors open --> **Ca2+ floods out of the SR** into the sarcoplasm.
6. Ca2+ binds troponin C --> tropomyosin moves --> cross-bridge cycling begins.
7. Relaxation: Ca2+ is actively pumped back into the SR by **SERCA** (SR Ca2+-ATPase). Ca2+ falls, troponin releases Ca2+, tropomyosin re-covers binding sites, contraction stops.

### 6.4 Muscle Fiber Types

| Feature | Type I (Slow Oxidative) | Type IIa (Fast Oxidative) | Type IIb/IIx (Fast Glycolytic) |
|---------|------------------------|--------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Contraction speed** | Slow | Fast | Fast |
| **Fatigue resistance** | High | Moderate | Low |
| **Primary metabolism** | Aerobic (oxidative phosphorylation) | Aerobic/anaerobic | Anaerobic (glycolysis) |
| **Myoglobin** | High (red fibers) | High | Low (white fibers) |
| **Mitochondria** | Many | Many | Few |
| **Examples** | Postural muscles, marathon runners | Middle-distance activities | Sprinting, power lifting |

### 6.5 Muscle Type Comparison

| Feature | Skeletal | Cardiac | Smooth |
|---------|----------|---------|--------|
| **Striated?** | Yes | Yes | No |
| **Nuclei** | Multinucleated (syncytium) | 1-2 per cell (central) | 1 per cell (central) |
| **Voluntary?** | Yes (somatic motor) | No (autonomic) | No (autonomic) |
| **Mechanism** | Troponin-tropomyosin on actin | Troponin-tropomyosin on actin | **Calmodulin** binds Ca2+, activates **MLCK** (myosin light chain kinase) which phosphorylates myosin |
| **Special features** | T-tubules, no gap junctions | **Intercalated discs** with gap junctions (electrical syncytium), autorhythmicity | Gap junctions in single-unit smooth muscle, no T-tubules, no sarcomeres |
| **Ca2+ source** | SR (primary) | SR + extracellular (through L-type Ca2+ channels) | Extracellular (primary) + SR (IP3-mediated release) |

MCAT connection: The key difference in smooth muscle regulation is that it uses **calmodulin-MLCK** instead of troponin-tropomyosin. Ca2+-calmodulin activates MLCK, which phosphorylates myosin, allowing it to bind actin.

### 6.6 Bone

**Bone types:**
- **Compact (cortical) bone** -- Dense, outer layer. Organized into **osteons** (Haversian systems): concentric lamellae around a central canal containing blood vessels.
- **Spongy (cancellous/trabecular) bone** -- Inner mesh-like structure. Contains **red bone marrow** (hematopoiesis) in trabecular spaces.

**Bone cells:**

| Cell | Function | Mnemonic |
|------|----------|----------|
| **Osteoblasts** | **Build** bone (deposit osteoid matrix, which mineralizes) | Blast = Build |
| **Osteoclasts** | **Break down** (resorb) bone. Multinucleated cells derived from monocytes. Use acid and enzymes | Clast = Crumble |
| **Osteocytes** | Mature bone cells (derived from trapped osteoblasts). Mechanosensors. Maintain bone matrix, communicate via canaliculi | Cyte = Cell (maintenance) |

**Bone remodeling regulation:**
- **PTH** (parathyroid hormone) -- Stimulates osteoclasts (indirectly via RANKL signaling from osteoblasts) --> bone resorption --> releases Ca2+ into blood. Raises blood Ca2+.
- **Calcitonin** (from thyroid C cells) -- Inhibits osteoclasts --> decreases bone resorption --> lowers blood Ca2+. (Opposite of PTH.)
- **Vitamin D** -- Promotes Ca2+ absorption from the gut and works with PTH to increase blood Ca2+.

**Ossification:**
- **Intramembranous ossification** -- Bone forms directly from mesenchyme (no cartilage template). Produces flat bones (skull, clavicle).
- **Endochondral ossification** -- Bone replaces a hyaline cartilage template. Produces long bones (femur, humerus). Growth occurs at the **epiphyseal plate** (growth plate) until it closes (ossifies) at maturity.

## 7. Reproductive System and Development

### 7.1 Male Reproductive System

**Spermatogenesis** occurs in the **seminiferous tubules** of the testes, beginning at puberty and continuing throughout life.

**Process:**
1. **Spermatogonia** (2n, diploid) undergo mitosis to self-renew and produce primary spermatocytes.
2. **Primary spermatocytes** (2n) undergo **meiosis I** --> 2 **secondary spermatocytes** (n, haploid).
3. **Secondary spermatocytes** (n) undergo **meiosis II** --> 4 **spermatids** (n).
4. **Spermiogenesis** -- Spermatids mature into **spermatozoa** (no further division, just morphological changes: develop acrosome, flagellum; shed cytoplasm).

Total: 1 spermatogonium --> 4 functional sperm. The entire process takes ~64-72 days.

**Supporting cells:**
- **Sertoli cells** -- Located in the seminiferous tubules. Nurse cells that support developing sperm. Functions: nourishment, phagocytosis of defective sperm, form the **blood-testis barrier** (tight junctions), secrete **inhibin** (negative feedback on FSH), secrete **androgen-binding protein (ABP)** to maintain local testosterone concentration, respond to FSH.
- **Leydig cells (interstitial cells)** -- Located between the seminiferous tubules. Produce **testosterone** in response to **LH**.

**Hormonal control:**
- **GnRH** (hypothalamus) --> **FSH + LH** (anterior pituitary).
- **LH** --> Leydig cells --> **testosterone** (drives spermatogenesis, secondary sex characteristics). Testosterone provides negative feedback on GnRH and LH.
- **FSH** --> Sertoli cells --> supports spermatogenesis. **Inhibin** from Sertoli cells provides negative feedback on FSH.

**Sperm pathway:** Seminiferous tubules --> epididymis (storage and maturation) --> vas deferens --> ejaculatory duct (joins seminal vesicle duct) --> urethra.

**Accessory glands:** Seminal vesicles (fructose for energy, ~60% of semen volume), prostate (alkaline fluid to neutralize vaginal acidity), bulbourethral (Cowper's) glands (pre-ejaculate, lubricating mucus).

### 7.2 Female Reproductive System

**Oogenesis** begins during fetal development, pauses, and resumes at puberty.

**Process:**
1. **Oogonia** (2n) undergo mitosis during fetal development to produce **primary oocytes** (2n).
2. Primary oocytes begin **meiosis I** but **arrest in prophase I** before birth. They remain arrested in primordial follicles until puberty (or until that follicle is recruited -- could be decades).
3. At puberty, each month, one primary oocyte resumes meiosis I --> produces 1 **secondary oocyte** (n, large, gets most of the cytoplasm) + 1 **first polar body** (small, degenerates). The division is unequal.
4. The secondary oocyte begins **meiosis II** but **arrests in metaphase II**. This is what is ovulated.
5. Meiosis II is completed ONLY if **fertilization** occurs --> 1 **ovum** + 1 **second polar body**.

Total: 1 oogonium --> 1 functional ovum (+ 2-3 polar bodies that degenerate).

### 7.3 The Menstrual Cycle

The menstrual cycle (~28 days) has two concurrent cycles:

**Ovarian cycle:**
1. **Follicular phase** (days 1-14): FSH stimulates follicle development. The developing follicle secretes **estrogen**. One dominant follicle matures (Graafian follicle). Rising estrogen initially provides **negative feedback** on FSH and LH (keeping them low).
2. **Ovulation** (day ~14): When estrogen reaches a critical high threshold, it switches to **positive feedback**, triggering an **LH surge** (and smaller FSH surge). The LH surge triggers ovulation (release of the secondary oocyte from the follicle).
3. **Luteal phase** (days 15-28): The ruptured follicle becomes the **corpus luteum**, which secretes **progesterone** (and some estrogen). Progesterone maintains the uterine lining and provides **negative feedback** on GnRH, FSH, and LH. If no fertilization, the corpus luteum degenerates (~day 26) --> progesterone drops --> menstruation begins. If fertilization occurs, **hCG** from the embryo maintains the corpus luteum until the placenta takes over (~weeks 8-12).

**Uterine cycle:**
1. **Menstrual phase** (days 1-5): Shedding of the endometrial lining due to progesterone and estrogen withdrawal.
2. **Proliferative phase** (days 6-14): Estrogen from developing follicles stimulates endometrial thickening and vascularization.
3. **Secretory phase** (days 15-28): Progesterone from the corpus luteum stimulates the endometrium to secrete glycogen and nutrients, preparing for implantation.

Key MCAT points:
- Estrogen = dominant hormone of the follicular phase (proliferative phase of uterus).
- Progesterone = dominant hormone of the luteal phase (secretory phase of uterus).
- Positive feedback of estrogen on LH is unique and commonly tested.
- Birth control pills contain synthetic estrogen and progesterone -- they provide constant negative feedback, suppressing FSH/LH, preventing follicle development and ovulation.

### 7.4 Embryology

**Fertilization to implantation:**
1. **Fertilization** -- Occurs in the **ampulla** of the fallopian tube (not the uterus). Sperm undergoes the **acrosome reaction** (releases enzymes to penetrate the zona pellucida). The **cortical reaction** (cortical granule release) hardens the zona pellucida to prevent polyspermy. Meiosis II of the oocyte is completed.
2. **Cleavage** -- Rapid mitotic divisions without growth (cells get smaller). Total embryo size stays roughly the same. Occurs as the embryo travels down the fallopian tube.
3. **Morula** (~day 3-4) -- Solid ball of ~16 cells.
4. **Blastocyst** (~day 5) -- Hollow ball with:
   - **Inner cell mass (embryoblast)** -- Becomes the embryo.
   - **Trophoblast** -- Outer layer, becomes part of the **placenta**. Secretes **hCG** (detected in pregnancy tests).
   - **Blastocoel** -- Fluid-filled cavity.
5. **Implantation** (~day 6-7) -- Blastocyst implants in the endometrium. Trophoblast invades the uterine lining.

**Gastrulation (formation of the three germ layers):**
- The most important event in early development. The inner cell mass reorganizes into three distinct layers.
- **Invagination** of cells through the **primitive streak** creates the three layers.

### 7.5 Germ Layers

| Layer | Becomes | Mnemonic |
|-------|---------|----------|
| **Ectoderm** (outer) | Epidermis (skin), hair, nails, nervous system (brain, spinal cord, neural crest), lens of eye, inner ear, tooth enamel, anterior pituitary, adrenal medulla | **E**ctoderm = **E**pidermis + n**E**rvous system |
| **Mesoderm** (middle) | Muscle (all types), bone, cartilage, connective tissue, cardiovascular system (heart, blood vessels, blood), kidneys, gonads, adrenal cortex, dermis, spleen, notochord | **M**esoderm = **M**uscle |
| **Endoderm** (inner) | Lining of the GI tract, respiratory tract, bladder, liver, pancreas, thyroid, parathyroid, thymus, tonsils | **Endo**derm = **Endo** (inside) = lining of internal organs |

MCAT connection: Neural crest cells (derived from ectoderm, specifically the junction of neural plate and surface ectoderm) migrate throughout the body and give rise to: PNS ganglia, Schwann cells, melanocytes, adrenal medulla, bone/cartilage of the face, odontoblasts (dentin).

**Neurulation:**
- The **notochord** (from mesoderm) induces the overlying ectoderm to form the **neural plate**.
- The neural plate folds inward to form the **neural groove**, which closes to become the **neural tube** (future brain and spinal cord).
- **Neural crest cells** pinch off at the junction and migrate away.
- Failure of neural tube closure: anencephaly (cranial end) or spina bifida (caudal end).

**Organogenesis:** Following gastrulation and neurulation, the three germ layers differentiate into specific organs and structures. This occurs during weeks 3-8 (most critical period for teratogen exposure).

## 8. Integumentary and Lymphatic Systems (Brief)

### 8.1 Integumentary System (Skin)

**Layers (superficial to deep):**
- **Epidermis** -- Stratified squamous epithelium. Avascular (no blood vessels). Layers from deep to superficial:
  - Stratum basale (stem cells, melanocytes, Merkel cells)
  - Stratum spinosum (Langerhans cells = dendritic/immune cells)
  - Stratum granulosum (keratohyalin granules, lipid secretion)
  - Stratum lucidum (only in thick skin: palms, soles)
  - Stratum corneum (dead, keratinized cells -- main barrier)
- **Dermis** -- Connective tissue with blood vessels, nerves, hair follicles, sweat glands, sebaceous glands. Contains collagen and elastin. Two layers: papillary (loose CT, dermal papillae for fingerprints) and reticular (dense irregular CT).
- **Hypodermis (subcutaneous layer)** -- Adipose tissue for insulation and energy storage. Not technically part of the skin but tested alongside it.

**Thermoregulation:**
- **Heat dissipation (too hot):** Vasodilation of dermal blood vessels (more blood to surface = more radiative heat loss). Sweating (evaporative cooling).
- **Heat conservation (too cold):** Vasoconstriction of dermal blood vessels (shunts blood to core). Piloerection (goosebumps -- limited effectiveness in humans). Shivering (muscle contraction generates heat).
- Controlled by the **hypothalamus** (the body's thermostat).

**Other skin functions:** Physical barrier, UV protection (melanin from melanocytes), vitamin D synthesis (UV-B converts 7-dehydrocholesterol to cholecalciferol in the skin), sensation (mechanoreceptors, thermoreceptors, nociceptors), immune defense (Langerhans cells).

### 8.2 Lymphatic System

**Functions:**
1. **Fluid return** -- Collects excess interstitial fluid (lymph) and returns it to the blood via the thoracic duct (drains into left subclavian vein). Prevents edema.
2. **Fat absorption** -- Lacteals in intestinal villi absorb dietary fats packaged as chylomicrons.
3. **Immune surveillance** -- Lymph passes through lymph nodes, where immune cells (B and T cells, macrophages) can detect and respond to pathogens.

**Key lymphoid organs:**
- **Lymph nodes** -- Filter lymph. Contain B cells (in follicles/germinal centers) and T cells (paracortex). Swollen nodes = active immune response.
- **Spleen** -- Filters blood (not lymph). **Red pulp**: destroys old/damaged RBCs, acts as blood reservoir. **White pulp**: contains lymphoid tissue for immune response against blood-borne pathogens.
- **Thymus** -- Site of T cell maturation (positive and negative selection). Most active in childhood; involutes (shrinks) with age.
- **MALT** (mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue) -- Includes tonsils, Peyer's patches (in ileum), and appendix. Immune surveillance at mucosal surfaces.

MCAT connection: The lymphatic system lacks a pump. Lymph is moved by skeletal muscle contraction, respiratory movements, and smooth muscle in lymph vessel walls. One-way valves prevent backflow.

## Quick-Reference: Cross-System Integration Points

These connections are gold for MCAT discrete and passage-based questions:

1. **Respiratory + Renal**: Both regulate blood pH. Lungs handle acute changes (adjust CO2 in minutes). Kidneys handle chronic compensation (adjust HCO3- over hours to days).

2. **Cardiovascular + Renal**: RAAS connects them directly. Blood pressure regulation is a team effort between heart (CO), vessels (TPR), and kidneys (volume regulation).

3. **Digestive + Cardiovascular**: Hepatic portal system delivers absorbed nutrients to the liver first. Lymphatics (lacteals) handle fat transport independently.

4. **Immune + All Systems**: MHC I on all nucleated cells means every organ system interacts with CD8+ T cells. Inflammation and immune response affect every tissue.

5. **Musculoskeletal + Respiratory**: Diaphragm and intercostals are skeletal muscles driving ventilation. Their contraction mechanics directly apply sliding filament theory.

6. **Renal + Musculoskeletal (Bone)**: PTH acts on both -- kidneys (Ca2+ reabsorption, phosphate excretion, vitamin D activation) and bone (Ca2+ mobilization via osteoclasts).

7. **Reproductive + Immune**: Fetal HbF left-shifted curve for O2 transfer. Maternal IgG crosses placenta (passive immunity). The placenta is an immunological interface.

8. **Boyle's law (ventilation) + Dalton's law (gas exchange) + Henry's law (gas solubility)**: Physics principles directly explain respiratory physiology. The MCAT loves testing these connections.
