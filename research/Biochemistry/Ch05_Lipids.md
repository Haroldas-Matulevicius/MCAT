## 1. Structural Lipids

### Phospholipids — amphipathic
- **Glycerophospholipids**: glycerol backbone + 2 fatty acids + phosphate + polar head group
  - Phosphatidylcholine (PC) — most abundant
  - Phosphatidylethanolamine (PE)
  - **Phosphatidylserine (PS)** — normally on inner leaflet; flips to outer leaflet in apoptosis → "eat me" signal for macrophages
  - Phosphatidylinositol (PI) — precursor to PIP2, PIP3 in signaling
- **Sphingomyelin** — sphingosine backbone + fatty acid (forms ceramide) + phosphocholine; abundant in myelin sheath

### Cholesterol
- Steroid nucleus: **4 fused rings** (3 cyclohexane + 1 cyclopentane)
- Inserted into membrane with OH near phospholipid heads, rigid ring system with the hydrophobic tails
- **Fluidity buffer** (see Ch 8): reduces fluidity at high T, prevents crystallization at low T
- Precursor to:
  - Steroid hormones (cortisol, aldosterone, sex hormones)
  - Bile salts (liver)
  - Vitamin D (via skin + liver + kidney)

### Waxes
- Long-chain fatty acid esterified to a long-chain alcohol
- Waterproofing (plant cuticle, earwax, beeswax)

## 2. Signaling Lipids

### Steroid Hormones (intracellular receptor signaling)
- Lipophilic → cross plasma membrane directly
- Bind cytoplasmic/nuclear receptors that act as transcription factors
- Slow (hours) but long-lasting effects — require new mRNA/protein synthesis
- Examples: cortisol, aldosterone, estrogen, testosterone, progesterone

### Eicosanoids (from arachidonic acid)
- 20-carbon polyunsaturated fatty acids
- **Cyclooxygenase (COX)** pathway → **prostaglandins** and **thromboxanes**
  - Inflammation, pain, fever, platelet aggregation, uterine contraction
  - NSAIDs (aspirin, ibuprofen) inhibit COX
- **Lipoxygenase** pathway → **leukotrienes**
  - Bronchoconstriction, immune cell chemotaxis (asthma, allergies)

### Fat-Soluble Vitamins: A, D, E, K
- Stored in liver/adipose (unlike water-soluble Bs, Cs which are excreted)
- **A (retinoids)**: vision (rhodopsin), gene expression (nuclear receptor RAR/RXR)
- **D**: calcium absorption, bone metabolism
- **E (tocopherol)**: antioxidant (membrane)
- **K**: γ-carboxylation of clotting factors II, VII, IX, X (warfarin target)

### Sphingolipids
- Ceramide — signaling in apoptosis
- Gangliosides (sphingolipid + oligosaccharide with sialic acid) — abundant in neurons; GM2 accumulates in Tay-Sachs

### Phosphoinositides
- PIP2 cleaved by PLC → **IP3** (ER Ca²⁺ release) + **DAG** (PKC activation) — Gq pathway
- PIP2 → PIP3 by PI3K — RTK/insulin pathway

## 3. Energy Storage Lipids

### Triacylglycerols (TAG / triglycerides)
- Three fatty acids esterified to glycerol (each via ester bond)
- Stored in adipose tissue (white fat) as anhydrous droplets
- Energy density ~9 kcal/g vs ~4 kcal/g for carbs/proteins (more reduced → more electrons to harvest in β-oxidation)
- Stored without water (unlike glycogen, which is hydrated) → compact energy

### Fatty Acids
- Long hydrocarbon chain with carboxylic acid head
- **Saturated**: no double bonds; pack tightly; solid at room T (butter, lard)
- **Unsaturated**: cis C=C double bonds introduce kinks → cannot pack as tightly → liquid at room T (oils)
- **Trans fats** (from partial hydrogenation of vegetable oils): double bond is trans, no kink, packs like saturated → linked to cardiovascular disease
- **Essential fatty acids** (cannot be synthesized): linoleic acid (ω-6, precursor to arachidonic acid), α-linolenic acid (ω-3)

### Nomenclature
- Numbering from carboxyl (C1) e.g., "16:0" = 16 carbons, 0 double bonds (palmitate)
- ω-numbering from methyl end e.g., "ω-3" means first double bond 3 carbons from the methyl end
- Common names: palmitic (16:0), stearic (18:0), oleic (18:1 ω-9), linoleic (18:2 ω-6), linolenic (18:3 ω-3)

## MCAT Tips

- **Why TAG > glycogen for long-term storage?** Fats are more reduced (more C-H bonds = more energy per oxidation), anhydrous (no water weight), and give ~9 kcal/g vs ~4 for glycogen (which is also heavily hydrated).
- **Trans fat exception**: trans double bonds do NOT introduce kinks — they behave structurally like saturated fats. This is why trans fats have similar cardiovascular risk to saturated fats.
- Know the **two essential fatty acids** (linoleic ω-6, α-linolenic ω-3) and that all other FAs can be synthesized de novo from acetyl-CoA.
- PIP2 is the single phospholipid that shows up in multiple signaling pathways — know it's cleaved by PLC (Gq) to IP3 + DAG, and phosphorylated by PI3K (RTK insulin) to PIP3.
