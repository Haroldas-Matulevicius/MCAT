## 1. Mechanisms of Hormone Action

The endocrine system uses **hormones** -- chemical messengers released into the blood that act on distant target cells. It works alongside the nervous system to maintain homeostasis, but operates on a slower timescale (seconds to hours vs milliseconds).

### Hormone Types: Peptide vs Steroid

This is one of the most important conceptual distinctions on the MCAT. Nearly every hormone question requires you to know this framework.

**Peptide/Protein Hormones (and amino acid derivatives like epinephrine):**
- **Hydrophilic** -- dissolve in blood, travel freely, CANNOT cross the cell membrane
- Bind to **cell-surface receptors** (usually G-protein coupled receptors or receptor tyrosine kinases)
- Act through **second messenger systems** (cAMP, IP3/DAG, Ca2+)
- Effects are **fast** (seconds to minutes) because they activate existing proteins
- Generally have **short half-lives** (degraded quickly)
- Examples: insulin, glucagon, GH, ADH, oxytocin, epinephrine, all anterior pituitary hormones

**Steroid Hormones (and thyroid hormones):**
- **Lipophilic** -- derived from cholesterol (steroids) or tyrosine (thyroid hormones)
- CANNOT dissolve in blood -- travel bound to **carrier proteins** (e.g., thyroid-binding globulin, sex hormone-binding globulin)
- **Cross the cell membrane** freely
- Bind to **intracellular receptors** (cytoplasmic or nuclear receptors)
- The hormone-receptor complex acts as a **transcription factor** -- binds DNA and alters **gene expression**
- Effects are **slow** (hours to days) because they require new protein synthesis
- Generally have **longer half-lives** (carrier proteins protect them from degradation)
- Examples: cortisol, aldosterone, testosterone, estrogen, progesterone, T3/T4, calcitriol (active vitamin D)

**MCAT trap:** Thyroid hormones (T3/T4) are amino acid derivatives but behave like steroid hormones -- they are lipophilic, cross membranes, bind intracellular receptors, and alter gene expression. Epinephrine is also an amino acid derivative but behaves like a peptide hormone -- it is hydrophilic and uses surface receptors. You MUST know these exceptions.

## 2. The Hypothalamic-Pituitary Axis

This is the command center of the endocrine system and the key integration point between the nervous and endocrine systems. The hypothalamus is a neural structure that controls a glandular structure (pituitary). This is high-yield.

**Hypothalamus:** Receives neural input about internal state and external environment. Converts neural signals into hormonal signals.

**Anterior Pituitary (adenohypophysis):**
- NOT directly connected to the hypothalamus by neurons
- Connected via the **hypothalamic-hypophyseal portal system** -- a specialized capillary network that carries releasing/inhibiting hormones directly from the hypothalamus to the anterior pituitary
- The hypothalamus releases hormones into the portal blood, and these hormones stimulate or inhibit anterior pituitary cells
- The anterior pituitary then releases its own hormones into the systemic circulation

**Posterior Pituitary (neurohypophysis):**
- IS a direct neural extension of the hypothalamus
- Hormones (ADH and oxytocin) are MADE in hypothalamic neurons (supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei), travel down axons, and are STORED in the posterior pituitary
- Released directly into the blood from axon terminals when those hypothalamic neurons fire

### Anterior Pituitary Hormones

**Mnemonic: FLAT PIG** -- FSH, LH, ACTH, TSH, Prolactin, Ignore, GH

| Hormone | Hypothalamic Control | Target | Effect |
|---------|---------------------|--------|--------|
| **GH** (Growth Hormone / Somatotropin) | GHRH stimulates; Somatostatin inhibits | Liver, bone, muscle | Stimulates IGF-1 release from liver -> growth. Increases blood glucose (diabetogenic). Promotes protein synthesis. |
| **TSH** (Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone) | TRH stimulates | Thyroid gland | Stimulates T3/T4 synthesis and release |
| **ACTH** (Adrenocorticotropic Hormone) | CRH stimulates | Adrenal cortex | Stimulates cortisol (and some aldosterone/androgen) release |
| **FSH** (Follicle-Stimulating Hormone) | GnRH stimulates | Gonads | Females: follicle development, estrogen production. Males: spermatogenesis (acts on Sertoli cells) |
| **LH** (Luteinizing Hormone) | GnRH stimulates | Gonads | Females: ovulation trigger, corpus luteum formation, progesterone production. Males: testosterone production (acts on Leydig cells) |
| **Prolactin** | Dopamine INHIBITS (tonic inhibition); TRH stimulates | Mammary glands | Milk production. Only anterior pituitary hormone under primarily inhibitory control. |

**Clinical connection:** Prolactin is tonically inhibited by dopamine. If the pituitary stalk is severed (cutting off hypothalamic input), all anterior pituitary hormones DECREASE except prolactin, which INCREASES because its inhibitory signal is lost. This is a classic MCAT question.

### Posterior Pituitary Hormones

| Hormone | Made in | Stimulus | Target | Effect |
|---------|---------|----------|--------|--------|
| **ADH** (Antidiuretic Hormone / Vasopressin) | Supraoptic nucleus of hypothalamus | High blood osmolarity, low blood volume | Kidney collecting ducts | Inserts aquaporin channels -> water reabsorption -> concentrated urine, also causes vasoconstriction at high levels |
| **Oxytocin** | Paraventricular nucleus of hypothalamus | Cervical stretch (labor), nipple stimulation (nursing) | Uterus, mammary glands | Uterine contractions during labor, milk letdown during nursing |

**ADH clinical connections:**
- **Diabetes insipidus (central):** Low ADH production -> massive dilute urine, dehydration, extreme thirst
- **SIADH:** Excess ADH -> too much water retention -> hyponatremia (low blood sodium)
- Alcohol inhibits ADH -> increased urination -> dehydration (hangover mechanism)

## 3. Endocrine Organs and Hormones

### Thyroid Gland

**T3 (triiodothyronine) and T4 (thyroxine):**
- Made from **tyrosine + iodine** in thyroid follicular cells
- T4 is the main circulating form; T3 is the **active form** (T4 is converted to T3 in peripheral tissues)
- Act like steroid hormones: cross membranes, bind nuclear receptors, alter gene expression
- **Increase basal metabolic rate (BMR)** -- increase O2 consumption, heat production, heart rate, and alertness
- Essential for normal growth and brain development in children (congenital hypothyroidism = cretinism)
- **Regulation:** Hypothalamus (TRH) -> Anterior pituitary (TSH) -> Thyroid (T3/T4) -> negative feedback on hypothalamus and pituitary

**Hyperthyroidism (Graves' disease):** Autoimmune -- antibodies mimic TSH and continuously stimulate the thyroid. Symptoms: weight loss, heat intolerance, increased HR, exophthalmos (bulging eyes), anxiety. TSH is LOW (negative feedback from high T3/T4).

**Hypothyroidism (Hashimoto's thyroiditis):** Autoimmune destruction of thyroid tissue. Symptoms: weight gain, cold intolerance, fatigue, bradycardia, myxedema. TSH is HIGH (pituitary tries to stimulate a failing thyroid).

**Calcitonin:**
- Produced by **parafollicular C cells** of the thyroid
- **Lowers blood Ca2+** by inhibiting osteoclast activity (reduces bone resorption) and promoting Ca2+ excretion in kidneys
- Clinically less important than PTH but tested on the MCAT
- Remember: **calciTONin TONes down calcium**

### Parathyroid Glands

Four small glands embedded in the posterior thyroid.

**PTH (Parathyroid Hormone):**
- Released when blood Ca2+ is **LOW**
- **Raises blood Ca2+** through three mechanisms:
  1. **Bone resorption:** Stimulates osteoclasts (indirectly, via osteoblasts) to break down bone and release Ca2+ and PO4^3-
  2. **Kidney reabsorption:** Increases Ca2+ reabsorption in the distal convoluted tubule (DCT); decreases phosphate reabsorption (phosphate excretion increases -- this prevents CaPO4 precipitation)
  3. **Vitamin D activation:** Stimulates the kidney enzyme 1-alpha-hydroxylase, which converts 25-hydroxyvitamin D to **1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (calcitriol)**, the active form. Calcitriol then increases Ca2+ absorption from the intestine.

**Mnemonic:** PTH does three things -- it acts on **B**one, **K**idney, and (via vitamin D) **I**ntestine. Think "PTH Brings Kalcium In."

**Calcium homeostasis summary:**
- Low Ca2+ -> PTH up, calcitonin down -> Ca2+ rises
- High Ca2+ -> PTH down, calcitonin up -> Ca2+ falls
- Vitamin D is the third player -- facilitates intestinal Ca2+ absorption

### Adrenal Glands

Each adrenal gland has two distinct regions that are functionally different organs.

**Adrenal Cortex (outer):** Produces steroid hormones from cholesterol. Three zones (remember: **GFR -- salt, sugar, sex**):

| Zone | Hormone | Function |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Zona Glomerulosa** | **Aldosterone** (mineralocorticoid) | Increases Na+ reabsorption and K+ secretion in kidney collecting duct -> water follows Na+ -> increases blood volume and BP. Regulated by RAAS (renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system) and blood K+ levels. |
| **Zona Fasciculata** | **Cortisol** (glucocorticoid) | Increases blood glucose (gluconeogenesis, protein catabolism, lipolysis). Anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive. Released in response to stress via CRH -> ACTH. Chronically elevated cortisol = Cushing's syndrome. |
| **Zona Reticularis** | **DHEA** (androgen) | Weak androgen, converted to testosterone/estrogen peripherally. Contributes to secondary sex characteristics, especially in females. |

**Cortisol and stress:** CRH (hypothalamus) -> ACTH (anterior pituitary) -> Cortisol (adrenal cortex). Cortisol feeds back negatively on both the hypothalamus and pituitary. Cortisol follows a diurnal rhythm (highest in the morning). Chronic stress -> chronically elevated cortisol -> immune suppression, muscle wasting, hyperglycemia, visceral fat deposition.

**Aldosterone and RAAS:** Low blood pressure -> kidney releases renin -> renin converts angiotensinogen (from liver) to angiotensin I -> ACE (in lungs) converts angiotensin I to angiotensin II -> angiotensin II causes vasoconstriction AND stimulates aldosterone release -> aldosterone causes Na+/water retention -> BP increases. ACE inhibitors are anti-hypertension drugs that block this cascade.

**Adrenal Medulla (inner):** This is the nervous-endocrine crossover point.

- Modified sympathetic postganglionic neurons (chromaffin cells)
- Release **epinephrine (~80%) and norepinephrine (~20%)** directly into the blood
- Triggered by sympathetic preganglionic neurons (using ACh)
- Effects: increased heart rate, bronchodilation, glycogenolysis, lipolysis, blood shunted to muscles
- These hormones produce the same effects as direct sympathetic stimulation but through the bloodstream, so effects are more widespread and longer-lasting

### Pancreas (Endocrine Function)

The pancreas is both exocrine (digestive enzymes) and endocrine. The endocrine portion consists of the **islets of Langerhans**.

| Cell Type | Hormone | Stimulus | Effect |
|-----------|---------|----------|--------|
| **Beta cells** | **Insulin** | High blood glucose (fed state) | Promotes glucose uptake (GLUT4 in muscle/adipose), glycogenesis, lipogenesis, protein synthesis. LOWERS blood glucose. |
| **Alpha cells** | **Glucagon** | Low blood glucose (fasted state) | Promotes glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis, lipolysis, ketogenesis in the liver. RAISES blood glucose. |
| **Delta cells** | Somatostatin | Various | Inhibits both insulin and glucagon release (paracrine regulator) |

**Insulin is the only hormone that lowers blood glucose.** Multiple hormones raise it (glucagon, cortisol, epinephrine, GH). This makes sense from a survival perspective -- hypoglycemia is more immediately dangerous than hyperglycemia.

**Diabetes mellitus:**
- **Type 1:** Autoimmune destruction of beta cells -> no insulin production. Requires exogenous insulin. Onset typically in youth.
- **Type 2:** Insulin resistance -> cells don't respond to insulin. Beta cells compensate by producing more insulin initially, but eventually burn out. Associated with obesity and lifestyle. Most common form.

**MCAT integration:** After a meal, blood glucose rises -> beta cells release insulin -> insulin binds receptor tyrosine kinase on target cells -> GLUT4 transporters are inserted into the membrane of muscle and adipose tissue -> glucose enters cells -> blood glucose falls. In the fasted state, glucagon dominates -> liver breaks down glycogen and makes new glucose -> blood glucose is maintained for the brain (which uses GLUT1 and GLUT3, insulin-independent transporters).

### Gonads

- **Testes:** Produce **testosterone** (Leydig cells, stimulated by LH) and support spermatogenesis (Sertoli cells, stimulated by FSH). Testosterone: male secondary sex characteristics, anabolic effects (muscle, bone growth), sex drive, spermatogenesis. Inhibin B (from Sertoli cells) feeds back to inhibit FSH.
- **Ovaries:** Produce **estrogen** (granulosa cells, stimulated by FSH) and **progesterone** (corpus luteum, stimulated by LH). Estrogen: female secondary sex characteristics, endometrial proliferation, bone density. Progesterone: endometrial maintenance, pregnancy support.

### Pineal Gland

- Produces **melatonin** (derived from serotonin, which is derived from tryptophan)
- Regulated by light/dark cycles via the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus
- Melatonin increases in darkness -> promotes sleep
- Key for **circadian rhythm** regulation

## 4. Feedback Mechanisms

**Negative feedback (most hormones):**
The product of a hormonal cascade inhibits upstream components. Example: T3/T4 inhibit TRH and TSH release. Cortisol inhibits CRH and ACTH. This maintains homeostasis.

**Positive feedback (rare but tested):**
The product amplifies the stimulus. Examples:
- **Oxytocin during labor:** Uterine contractions -> cervical stretch -> more oxytocin -> stronger contractions -> more stretch -> cycle continues until delivery
- **LH surge at ovulation:** Rising estrogen from the mature follicle eventually triggers a POSITIVE feedback response (only at high sustained levels) -> LH surge -> ovulation
- **Blood clotting cascade:** Activated clotting factors activate more clotting factors

**MCAT rule:** If a question asks "what happens when you administer exogenous hormone X," think negative feedback. Exogenous cortisol -> ACTH drops. Exogenous testosterone -> LH and FSH drop (basis of male hormonal contraception). Exogenous T4 -> TSH drops.

## 5. Nervous-Endocrine Integration: The Big Picture

The MCAT rewards students who can connect these two systems. Here are the key integration points:

1. **Hypothalamus is the bridge:** It's a neural structure that controls the pituitary gland (endocrine). It receives neural input (temperature, stress, circadian signals) and converts it to hormonal output.

2. **Sympathetic -> adrenal medulla:** Preganglionic sympathetic neurons (neural) stimulate chromaffin cells to release epinephrine and NE (hormonal) into the blood. This amplifies and prolongs the fight-or-flight response beyond what direct sympathetic innervation alone can achieve.

3. **Stress response integration:** Acute stress activates BOTH the sympathetic nervous system (immediate: increased HR, vasoconstriction, pupil dilation) AND the HPA axis (minutes: CRH -> ACTH -> cortisol for sustained metabolic support). The nervous system handles the first seconds; the endocrine system sustains the response.

4. **Blood glucose regulation:** Sympathetic activation stimulates glucagon release and inhibits insulin release (alpha-adrenergic effect on beta cells). Epinephrine directly promotes glycogenolysis in muscle and liver. Cortisol promotes gluconeogenesis. All systems converge to ensure glucose is available during stress.

5. **Circadian rhythms:** The SCN (hypothalamic nucleus) receives light input from retinal ganglion cells (neural) and controls melatonin release from the pineal gland (endocrine) and cortisol rhythm via the HPA axis.

6. **ADH and blood pressure:** Baroreceptors in the carotid sinus and aortic arch (neural) detect low blood pressure -> signal to the hypothalamus -> ADH release from posterior pituitary (endocrine) -> water retention -> blood volume and pressure increase.

## High-Yield Summary: What to Memorize (Endocrine)

- **Anterior pituitary:** GH, TSH, ACTH, FSH, LH, Prolactin (portal system from hypothalamus)
- **Posterior pituitary:** ADH and oxytocin (made in hypothalamus, stored/released from posterior pit)
- **Peptide hormones:** hydrophilic, surface receptors, second messengers, fast
- **Steroid hormones:** lipophilic, carrier proteins in blood, intracellular receptors, gene expression, slow
- **PTH raises Ca2+** (bone resorption, kidney reabsorption, vitamin D activation); **calcitonin lowers Ca2+**
- **Insulin lowers glucose** (only one that does); glucagon, cortisol, epinephrine, GH all raise it
- **Adrenal cortex:** GFR = salt (aldosterone), sugar (cortisol), sex (DHEA)
- **Adrenal medulla:** epi and NE into blood (sympathetic nervous -> endocrine bridge)
- **Prolactin:** only anterior pituitary hormone under tonic inhibition (by dopamine); stalk cut -> prolactin goes UP, everything else goes DOWN
- **Negative feedback:** most hormones. Positive feedback: oxytocin in labor, LH surge at ovulation.
