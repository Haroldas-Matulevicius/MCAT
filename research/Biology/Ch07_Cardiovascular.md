## 1. Heart Anatomy and Blood Flow

**Chambers and flow path (follow the blood):**

Deoxygenated blood returns via **superior and inferior vena cava** --> **right atrium** --> through **tricuspid valve** (right AV valve) --> **right ventricle** --> through **pulmonary valve** (semilunar) --> **pulmonary arteries** --> lungs (gas exchange) --> **pulmonary veins** --> **left atrium** --> through **mitral (bicuspid) valve** (left AV valve) --> **left ventricle** --> through **aortic valve** (semilunar) --> **aorta** --> systemic circulation.

Key points:
- **Arteries** carry blood AWAY from the heart (not always oxygenated -- pulmonary arteries carry deoxygenated blood).
- **Veins** carry blood TOWARD the heart (pulmonary veins carry oxygenated blood).
- The left ventricle has the thickest wall because it pumps against the highest pressure (systemic circulation).
- **Chordae tendineae** and **papillary muscles** anchor the AV valve leaflets, preventing prolapse (inversion) during ventricular systole.

## 2. Cardiac Cycle

The cardiac cycle consists of **systole** (contraction) and **diastole** (relaxation).

**Phases in detail:**

1. **Atrial systole** -- Atria contract, pushing the last ~20% of blood into ventricles (ventricles are already ~80% filled passively). This corresponds to the P wave on the ECG.
2. **Isovolumetric contraction** -- Ventricles begin contracting. AV valves snap shut (both AV and semilunar valves are closed). Volume stays constant while pressure rises rapidly. AV valve closure produces the **first heart sound (S1 = "lub")**.
3. **Ventricular ejection** -- Ventricular pressure exceeds arterial pressure. Semilunar valves open. Blood is ejected into the aorta and pulmonary artery. This corresponds to the QRS complex and early T wave.
4. **Isovolumetric relaxation** -- Ventricles relax. Semilunar valves snap shut (both sets of valves again closed). Volume stays constant while pressure drops. Semilunar valve closure produces the **second heart sound (S2 = "dub")**. The dicrotic notch on the aortic pressure tracing marks this moment.
5. **Passive ventricular filling** -- AV valves open. Blood flows passively from atria into ventricles. This accounts for ~80% of ventricular filling.

**Heart sounds summary:**
- **S1** = AV valves close (beginning of systole, "lub")
- **S2** = semilunar valves close (beginning of diastole, "dub")
- Murmurs indicate turbulent flow through abnormal valves (stenosis = narrowed, regurgitation = leaky).

## 3. Cardiac Output

**CO = HR x SV** (Cardiac Output = Heart Rate x Stroke Volume)

- **Heart rate** is modulated by the autonomic nervous system. Sympathetic (norepinephrine on beta-1 receptors) increases HR. Parasympathetic (ACh on muscarinic receptors via the vagus nerve) decreases HR.
- **Stroke volume** depends on three factors:
  - **Preload** -- Degree of ventricular stretch at end of diastole (related to venous return). **Frank-Starling mechanism**: increased preload --> increased stretch --> increased force of contraction --> increased SV. Up to a point -- overstretching leads to decreased function (heart failure).
  - **Contractility** (inotropy) -- Intrinsic strength of contraction independent of preload. Sympathetic stimulation and circulating epinephrine increase contractility. Digitalis increases contractility by raising intracellular Ca2+.
  - **Afterload** -- Pressure the ventricle must overcome to eject blood (essentially systemic vascular resistance for the left ventricle). Increased afterload (e.g., hypertension, aortic stenosis) decreases SV because the heart must work harder to push blood out.

## 4. Conduction System

The heart generates its own electrical impulses (**autorhythmicity / automaticity**). No external nerve input is needed for the heart to beat -- the nervous system only modulates rate.

**Pathway:**
1. **SA node** (sinoatrial node) -- The pacemaker. Located in the right atrial wall. Fires at ~60-100 bpm. Depolarization spreads across the atria, causing atrial contraction (P wave on ECG).
2. **AV node** (atrioventricular node) -- Located at the junction of atria and ventricles. Introduces a ~0.1 sec **delay** to allow atria to finish emptying before ventricles contract (PR interval on ECG).
3. **Bundle of His** -- Conducts impulse from AV node into the interventricular septum.
4. **Bundle branches** -- Left and right branches carry the signal down either side of the septum.
5. **Purkinje fibers** -- Rapidly distribute the signal throughout the ventricular myocardium, causing coordinated ventricular contraction from apex to base (QRS complex on ECG).

MCAT connection: Pacemaker cells have **funny channels** (HCN channels) that allow slow Na+ leak during diastole, causing gradual depolarization to threshold. This is different from the stable resting potential of contractile myocytes.

## 5. Blood Pressure Regulation

**Mean Arterial Pressure (MAP)** = CO x Total Peripheral Resistance (TPR). MAP is approximately diastolic + 1/3(systolic - diastolic).

**Short-term regulation: Baroreceptors**
- Located in the carotid sinus and aortic arch.
- Detect stretch (increased BP --> more stretch --> increased firing).
- High BP: baroreceptors fire more --> brainstem increases parasympathetic output and decreases sympathetic --> HR and TPR decrease --> BP drops.
- Low BP: baroreceptors fire less --> opposite response --> BP increases.

- **Vasovagal syncope (clinical):** Sudden parasympathetic surge (triggered by emotional shock, sight of blood, prolonged standing) -> vagally mediated bradycardia + vasodilation -> BP drop -> fainting. Classic baroreflex-related clinical scenario; often used as MCAT passage hook.

**Long-term regulation: RAAS (Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System)**
1. Low blood pressure/volume detected by **juxtaglomerular (JG) cells** in the kidney.
2. JG cells release **renin** into the blood.
3. Renin cleaves **angiotensinogen** (from liver) to **angiotensin I**.
4. **ACE** (angiotensin-converting enzyme, in lung capillaries) converts angiotensin I to **angiotensin II**.
5. Angiotensin II effects:
   - Potent **vasoconstriction** (raises TPR immediately)
   - Stimulates **aldosterone** release from the adrenal cortex --> Na+ and water reabsorption in the kidney --> increases blood volume
   - Stimulates **ADH** release from the posterior pituitary --> water reabsorption
   - Stimulates thirst

**ANP (Atrial Natriuretic Peptide):**
- Released by atrial cardiomyocytes when stretched by high blood volume.
- Promotes Na+ and water excretion (natriuresis and diuresis).
- Opposes RAAS -- reduces blood volume and BP.

## 6. Vessel Types

| Vessel | Key Features | Function |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| **Arteries** | Thick walls, elastic tissue | Conduct blood under high pressure; elastic recoil maintains pressure during diastole |
| **Arterioles** | Thick smooth muscle layer | **Major site of resistance regulation** (TPR). Vasodilation/constriction controls blood flow to capillary beds |
| **Capillaries** | Single endothelial cell layer, very thin | Gas, nutrient, and waste exchange. **Slowest flow velocity** (largest total cross-sectional area) |
| **Venules** | Thin walls | Collect blood from capillaries |
| **Veins** | Thin walls, contain **valves** | Return blood to heart. Low pressure system. Valves prevent backflow. Skeletal muscle pump and respiratory pump assist venous return |

**Starling forces at capillaries:** Fluid movement across capillary walls depends on the balance of hydrostatic pressure (pushes fluid out) and oncotic/osmotic pressure (pulls fluid in, due to plasma proteins like albumin). At the arteriolar end, hydrostatic > oncotic --> fluid filters out. At the venular end, oncotic > hydrostatic --> fluid reabsorbs. Net slight excess of filtration is collected by lymphatics.

## 7. Blood Composition and Coagulation

**Blood composition:**
- **Plasma** (~55%) -- Water, proteins (albumin, globulins, fibrinogen), electrolytes, dissolved gases, nutrients, wastes.
- **Formed elements** (~45%) -- RBCs (99%), WBCs, platelets. The percentage of blood volume occupied by RBCs is the **hematocrit**.

**Red blood cells (erythrocytes):**
- **Biconcave disc shape** -- Maximizes surface area for gas exchange.
- **No nucleus, no mitochondria, no organelles** -- More room for hemoglobin; rely entirely on anaerobic glycolysis for ATP (cannot consume the O2 they carry).
- Lifespan: ~120 days. Produced in bone marrow (**erythropoiesis**), regulated by **erythropoietin (EPO)** from the kidneys.
- Old RBCs are recycled in the **spleen** (and liver). Heme --> biliverdin --> bilirubin (processed by liver, excreted in bile).

**Coagulation cascade (simplified, know the big picture):**
1. **Vascular spasm** -- Injured vessel constricts to reduce blood flow.
2. **Platelet plug formation** -- Platelets adhere to exposed collagen (via von Willebrand factor), activate, release ADP and thromboxane A2, recruit more platelets. This is the **primary hemostatic plug**.
3. **Coagulation cascade** -- Series of serine protease activations (intrinsic and extrinsic pathways converge on the **common pathway**). End result: **prothrombin --> thrombin** (via factor Xa). Thrombin converts **fibrinogen --> fibrin**. Fibrin forms a mesh that stabilizes the platelet plug (secondary hemostasis). Factor XIII cross-links fibrin strands.
4. **Fibrinolysis** -- **Plasmin** (from plasminogen) eventually dissolves the clot after tissue repair.

## 8. Blood Types

**ABO system:**
- Determined by glycoprotein antigens on the RBC surface.
- **Type A**: A antigen on RBCs, anti-B antibodies in plasma.
- **Type B**: B antigen on RBCs, anti-A antibodies in plasma.
- **Type AB**: Both antigens, no antibodies. **Universal recipient** (can receive from anyone because no antibodies to attack donor cells).
- **Type O**: No antigens, both anti-A and anti-B antibodies. **Universal donor** (no antigens to be attacked by recipient).

**Genetics:** The ABO gene has three alleles -- I^A, I^B, i. I^A and I^B are **codominant** to each other. Both are **dominant** over i (recessive). This is a classic MCAT genetics problem.

**Rh factor:**
- Rh+ = has D antigen. Rh- = lacks D antigen.
- Rh- mother carrying Rh+ fetus can develop anti-Rh antibodies after first exposure (usually at delivery). Subsequent Rh+ pregnancies risk **erythroblastosis fetalis** (hemolytic disease of the newborn). Prevented by **RhoGAM** (anti-D immunoglobulin) given to Rh- mothers.

## 9. Fetal Circulation (Reference)

The fetus does not use its lungs for gas exchange -- the **placenta** serves that function. Several shunts bypass the non-functional pulmonary circuit:

- **Umbilical vein** -- Carries oxygenated blood FROM the placenta TO the fetus. (Counterintuitive: this vein carries oxygenated blood.)
- **Ductus venosus** -- Shunts blood from the umbilical vein past the liver directly to the IVC.
- **Foramen ovale** -- Opening in the interatrial septum. Shunts blood from the right atrium to the left atrium, bypassing the lungs.
- **Ductus arteriosus** -- Connects the pulmonary artery to the aorta, diverting blood away from the lungs.
- **Umbilical arteries (2)** -- Carry deoxygenated blood FROM the fetus TO the placenta.

At birth: First breath --> lungs expand --> pulmonary resistance drops --> more blood flows through lungs --> left atrial pressure increases, closing the **foramen ovale** (becomes fossa ovalis). Rising O2 levels and declining prostaglandins cause the **ductus arteriosus** to close (becomes ligamentum arteriosum).

## High-Yield Summary: What to Memorize (Cardiovascular)

- **Flow path:** SVC/IVC → RA → tricuspid → RV → pulmonary valve → pulm artery → lungs → pulm veins → LA → mitral → LV → aortic valve → aorta.
- **Right side = deoxy, left side = oxy.** Pulmonary artery is the exception (deoxy artery); pulmonary vein is exception (oxy vein).
- **S1** = AV valves close (start systole). **S2** = semilunar valves close (start diastole).
- **CO = HR × SV.** SV depends on preload, contractility, afterload.
- **Frank-Starling:** more stretch (preload) → more force.
- **Conduction:** SA → AV (delay) → bundle of His → bundle branches → Purkinje.
- **Baroreceptors** = fast/short-term. **RAAS** = slow/long-term. **ANP** opposes RAAS.
- **Capillaries** = slowest flow (largest total cross-sectional area).
- **AB = universal recipient. O = universal donor.** I^A and I^B codominant; both dominant over i.
- **Three fetal shunts:** ductus venosus (bypass liver), foramen ovale (RA→LA), ductus arteriosus (pulm artery → aorta).
