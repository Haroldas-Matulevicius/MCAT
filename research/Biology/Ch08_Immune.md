## 1. Innate Immunity

The innate immune system provides **immediate, nonspecific** defense. No memory is formed. It is the first line of defense.

**Physical and chemical barriers:**
- Skin (keratinized epithelium), mucous membranes, stomach acid (pH ~2), lysozyme (in tears, saliva), defensins (antimicrobial peptides), normal flora (competitive exclusion).

**Cellular components:**
- **Neutrophils** -- Most abundant WBC. **First responders** to infection. Phagocytes. Short-lived. Release reactive oxygen species and form neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs).
- **Macrophages** -- Derived from monocytes. Phagocytose pathogens and dead cells. **Antigen-presenting cells (APCs)** -- process and present antigens on **MHC II** to helper T cells. Release cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-1, IL-6) to amplify inflammation.
- **Dendritic cells** -- The most effective APCs. Found in tissues exposed to the environment (skin, mucosal lining). Capture antigens, migrate to lymph nodes, present on MHC II to activate T cells. Bridge between innate and adaptive immunity.
- **Natural killer (NK) cells** -- Lymphocytes of the innate system (no antigen-specific receptors). Kill virus-infected cells and tumor cells by detecting the absence of MHC I ("missing self" hypothesis). Release **perforin** (pore-forming) and **granzymes** (trigger apoptosis).
- **Mast cells** -- Found in connective tissues. Release **histamine** (vasodilation, increased permeability) and other mediators. Key players in allergic responses.
- **Eosinophils** -- Combat parasitic infections. Also involved in allergic inflammation.
- **Basophils** -- Release histamine and heparin. Rare in blood.

**Complement system:**
A cascade of plasma proteins (C1-C9) activated by three pathways (classical, lectin, alternative). All converge on C3 --> C3a + C3b.
- **C3b** -- Opsonization (tags pathogens for phagocytosis).
- **C3a, C5a** -- Anaphylatoxins (trigger inflammation, recruit immune cells).
- **C5b-C9** -- Form the **membrane attack complex (MAC)**, which punches holes in pathogen membranes causing lysis.

**Inflammation cascade:**
1. Tissue damage/infection --> mast cells release histamine --> **vasodilation** + **increased capillary permeability**.
2. Fluid, proteins, and immune cells move from blood to tissue (edema = swelling).
3. Neutrophils arrive first (attracted by chemokines), then macrophages.
4. Classic signs: **redness** (rubor), **heat** (calor), **swelling** (tumor), **pain** (dolor), loss of function.

**Interferons:**
- Released by virus-infected cells. Alert neighboring cells to upregulate antiviral defenses (degrade viral RNA, inhibit protein synthesis). They do NOT directly kill the virus -- they signal danger.
- Type I interferons (IFN-alpha, IFN-beta) -- Antiviral.
- Type II interferon (IFN-gamma) -- Activates macrophages and NK cells.

**Fever:**
- Pyrogens (exogenous from pathogens or endogenous like IL-1, IL-6, TNF) act on the hypothalamus to raise the body temperature set point.
- Benefit: many pathogens grow optimally at 37 degrees C; elevated temperature enhances immune cell activity and inhibits pathogen replication.

## 2. Adaptive Immunity -- Humoral (B Cell-Mediated)

**Humoral immunity** targets **extracellular** pathogens (bacteria in blood, toxins, free viruses before they enter cells). The effectors are **antibodies**.

**B cell activation and clonal selection:**
1. Each B cell expresses a unique **B cell receptor (BCR)**, which is a membrane-bound antibody.
2. When a B cell encounters its matching antigen, it binds and internalizes it.
3. The B cell presents processed antigen on **MHC II** to a **CD4+ helper T cell** (T_H2 specifically). The helper T cell provides a second signal (cytokines like IL-4, IL-5).
4. The activated B cell undergoes **clonal expansion** (rapid proliferation) and differentiates into:
   - **Plasma cells** -- Antibody-secreting factories. Short-lived but produce massive amounts of antibodies.
   - **Memory B cells** -- Long-lived. Enable faster, stronger secondary response upon re-exposure.
5. This is **clonal selection theory**: only B cells with receptors matching the antigen are selected and expanded.

**Antibody (immunoglobulin) structure:**
- Y-shaped protein with 2 **heavy chains** and 2 **light chains**, held together by disulfide bonds.
- **Variable region (Fab)** -- The tips of the Y. Antigen-binding site. Unique to each antibody -- determines specificity.
- **Constant region (Fc)** -- The stem of the Y. Determines antibody class/isotype. Recognized by immune cell receptors and complement.

**Five antibody classes:**

| Class | Features | Function |
|-------|----------|----------|
| **IgG** | Most abundant in blood. Monomer. **Only class that crosses the placenta** | Opsonization, complement activation, neutralization. Provides passive immunity to newborns |
| **IgM** | Pentamer (5 units joined by J chain). First produced in a primary response | Excellent at agglutination and complement activation. Used for blood typing |
| **IgA** | Dimer in secretions (joined by J chain and secretory component). Found in mucus, saliva, breast milk, tears | Mucosal immunity. Prevents pathogen attachment to epithelial surfaces. Protects newborns via breast milk |
| **IgE** | Monomer. Very low serum concentration | Binds mast cells and basophils. Triggers histamine release. Involved in **allergic reactions** and defense against **parasites** |
| **IgD** | Monomer. Found on surface of mature naive B cells | Functions as a B cell receptor. Role in B cell activation (not well understood) |

Mnemonic for class switching order: **MADGE** (IgM first, then IgA, IgD, IgG, IgE -- though this is simplified; class switching is driven by cytokines from helper T cells).

## 3. Adaptive Immunity -- Cell-Mediated (T Cell-Mediated)

**Cell-mediated immunity** targets **intracellular** pathogens (viruses inside cells, intracellular bacteria) and abnormal self-cells (cancer, transplant).

**T cell maturation:**
- T cell precursors originate in the **bone marrow** but mature in the **thymus**.
- In the thymus, T cells undergo **positive selection** (must recognize self-MHC) and **negative selection** (must NOT strongly react to self-antigens). T cells that fail either are eliminated. This establishes **self-tolerance**.

**Major T cell types:**

**CD4+ Helper T cells (T_H):**
- Recognize antigens presented on **MHC II** (found on **APCs**: macrophages, dendritic cells, B cells).
- Do NOT directly kill anything. They coordinate the immune response by releasing **cytokines**.
  - T_H1 --> activate macrophages and CD8+ cells (cell-mediated)
  - T_H2 --> activate B cells (humoral response)
  - T_H17 --> recruit neutrophils, fight extracellular bacteria/fungi

**CD8+ Cytotoxic T cells (T_C):**
- Recognize antigens presented on **MHC I** (found on **all nucleated cells**).
- Directly kill infected or abnormal cells by releasing **perforin** (creates pores) and **granzymes** (activate apoptosis).
- This is why virus-infected cells and cancer cells are targeted: they display abnormal peptides on MHC I.

## 4. MHC I vs MHC II

This distinction is extremely high-yield and commonly tested.

| Feature | MHC I | MHC II |
|---------|-------|--------|
| **Found on** | ALL nucleated cells (+ platelets). NOT on RBCs | Only on **APCs** (macrophages, dendritic cells, B cells) |
| **Presents** | Endogenous antigens (proteins made INSIDE the cell -- viral proteins, tumor antigens, self-peptides) | Exogenous antigens (proteins taken in from OUTSIDE via phagocytosis or endocytosis) |
| **Recognized by** | **CD8+** cytotoxic T cells | **CD4+** helper T cells |
| **Function** | Allows immune system to detect and kill infected/abnormal self-cells | Allows APCs to "show" what they have engulfed and activate the adaptive response |

Think of it this way: MHC I is a "window" into what every cell is making. If a cell is making viral proteins, MHC I displays them, and CD8+ T cells come kill that cell. MHC II is a "report" from professional immune cells about what they have found in the environment.

## 5. Active vs Passive Immunity

- **Active immunity** -- The individual's own immune system generates the response. Memory cells are formed.
  - **Natural active**: Infection.
  - **Artificial active**: Vaccination.
  - Slow onset, long-lasting.
- **Passive immunity** -- Preformed antibodies are transferred. No memory cells formed.
  - **Natural passive**: Maternal IgG across placenta, IgA in breast milk.
  - **Artificial passive**: Injection of antiserum/antitoxin (e.g., rabies immunoglobulin).
  - Immediate protection, short-lived (antibodies degrade).

## 6. Hypersensitivity Reactions

| Type | Name | Mechanism | Timing | Examples |
|------|------|-----------|--------|----------|
| **I** | Immediate/Anaphylactic | **IgE** binds mast cells/basophils --> histamine release | Seconds to minutes | Allergies, anaphylaxis, asthma (allergic), hay fever |
| **II** | Cytotoxic | **IgG/IgM** antibodies bind antigens on cell surfaces --> complement or NK cell destruction | Hours | Hemolytic transfusion reactions, hemolytic disease of newborn, autoimmune hemolytic anemia |
| **III** | Immune complex | **Antigen-antibody complexes** deposit in tissues --> complement activation --> inflammation | Hours | Serum sickness, lupus (SLE), rheumatoid arthritis |
| **IV** | Delayed-type | **T cell** mediated (no antibodies) | 24-72 hours | TB skin test (PPD), contact dermatitis (poison ivy), transplant rejection |

Key: Types I-III are antibody-mediated. Type IV is cell-mediated. Type IV is the only one that takes days.

## 7. Lymphatic System

**Functions:**
1. **Fluid return** -- Collects excess interstitial fluid (lymph) and returns it to the blood via the thoracic duct (drains into left subclavian vein). Prevents edema.
2. **Fat absorption** -- Lacteals in intestinal villi absorb dietary fats packaged as chylomicrons.
3. **Immune surveillance** -- Lymph passes through lymph nodes, where immune cells (B and T cells, macrophages) can detect and respond to pathogens.

**Key lymphoid organs:**
- **Lymph nodes** -- Filter lymph. Contain B cells (in follicles/germinal centers) and T cells (paracortex). Swollen nodes = active immune response.
- **Spleen** -- Filters blood (not lymph). **Red pulp**: destroys old/damaged RBCs, acts as blood reservoir. **White pulp**: contains lymphoid tissue for immune response against blood-borne pathogens.
- **Thymus** -- Site of T cell maturation (positive and negative selection). Most active in childhood; involutes (shrinks) with age.
- **MALT** (mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue) -- Includes tonsils, Peyer's patches (in ileum), and appendix. Immune surveillance at mucosal surfaces.

MCAT connection: The lymphatic system lacks a pump. Lymph is moved by skeletal muscle contraction, respiratory movements, and smooth muscle in lymph vessel walls. One-way valves prevent backflow.

## High-Yield Summary: What to Memorize (Immune)

- **Innate** = fast, nonspecific, no memory. **Adaptive** = slow first time, specific, memory.
- **Neutrophils first**, then macrophages. Dendritic cells = best APCs.
- **NK cells** kill cells lacking MHC I ("missing self").
- **Complement:** C3b opsonizes; C3a/C5a recruit; C5b-C9 = MAC (lyses).
- **MHC I** on all nucleated cells → presents endogenous → activates **CD8+** (kills).
- **MHC II** only on APCs → presents exogenous → activates **CD4+** (helps).
- **IgG** = most abundant, crosses placenta. **IgM** = first/pentamer. **IgA** = secretions. **IgE** = allergy/parasites. **IgD** = BCR.
- **Active** = own immune response, memory. **Passive** = transferred antibodies, no memory.
- **Hypersensitivity:** I (IgE/anaphylaxis), II (IgG-cytotoxic), III (immune complex), IV (T-cell, delayed).
- **Spleen** filters blood; **lymph nodes** filter lymph; **thymus** matures T cells.
