# BB Bio Chapter 9 — The Digestive System

Scope: Anatomy of the GI tract; mechanical and chemical digestion by location; autonomic regulation; digestive hormones; accessory organs (liver, pancreas, gallbladder); absorption mechanisms; defecation.

## 1. Anatomy & Mechanical/Chemical Digestion by Location

### Oral cavity
- Mechanical: **Mastication** (chewing) breaks food into smaller pieces, increasing surface area.
- Chemical: **Salivary amylase** (ptyalin) begins **starch digestion** (hydrolyzes alpha-1,4 glycosidic bonds). Optimal pH ~7. Inactivated by stomach acid.
- **Lingual lipase** (from serous glands at the back of the tongue) begins fat digestion. Works in the stomach at low pH.

### Esophagus
- Conducts food via **peristalsis** (coordinated smooth muscle contractions). No significant digestion occurs here.
- **Lower esophageal sphincter (LES)** prevents reflux. Dysfunction --> GERD.

### Autonomic regulation of GI motility
- **Parasympathetic** (mainly via the **vagus nerve**) -> increased peristalsis, increased glandular secretions, increased overall digestive activity. "Rest and digest."
- **Sympathetic** (via **splanchnic nerves** from thoracolumbar spinal cord) -> decreased peristalsis, decreased secretions, blood shunted away from gut to skeletal muscle and heart. "Fight or flight" -- digestion is a low priority during stress.

### Stomach
- Mechanical: **Churning** by muscular walls (three muscle layers: oblique, circular, longitudinal) mixes food with gastric juices, producing **chyme**.
- Chemical:
  - **Chief cells** secrete **pepsinogen** (inactive zymogen). Activated to **pepsin** by HCl (acid) in the stomach lumen. Pepsin is an endopeptidase that begins **protein digestion**. Optimal pH ~2.
  - **Parietal cells** secrete **HCl** (creates acidic environment for pepsin, kills bacteria) and **intrinsic factor** (required for vitamin B12 absorption in the ileum). H+/K+ ATPase (proton pump) on apical surface.
  - **G cells** secrete **gastrin** (hormone) into blood.
  - **Mucous cells** secrete mucus and bicarbonate to protect the stomach lining from acid autodigestion.
  - **D cells** secrete somatostatin (inhibits gastrin release).
- The stomach does NOT absorb much (some water, alcohol, aspirin).

### Small intestine (major site of digestion AND absorption)

Three regions: **duodenum** (most digestion), **jejunum** (most absorption), **ileum** (B12 and bile salt absorption).

- **Pancreatic enzymes** (released into the duodenum):
  - **Trypsinogen** --> activated to **trypsin** by **enterokinase/enteropeptidase** (brush border enzyme). Trypsin then activates other zymogens (chymotrypsinogen --> chymotrypsin, procarboxypeptidase --> carboxypeptidase, proelastase --> elastase). This cascade prevents premature activation (autodigestion of the pancreas = pancreatitis).
  - **Pancreatic lipase** -- Digests triglycerides into 2 fatty acids + monoglyceride. Requires **colipase** (anchors lipase to lipid droplet) and **bile salts** (emulsify large fat globules into smaller droplets, increasing surface area).
  - **Pancreatic amylase** -- Continues starch digestion.
  - **Nucleases** -- Digest nucleic acids.
  - **Sodium bicarbonate** -- Secreted by pancreatic duct cells. Neutralizes acidic chyme entering the duodenum, raising pH to ~7-8 (optimal for pancreatic enzymes).

- **Brush border enzymes** (on microvilli of enterocytes):
  - Disaccharidases: maltase, sucrase, lactase (break disaccharides into monosaccharides).
  - Peptidases: aminopeptidase, dipeptidase (final protein digestion).
  - Enterokinase/enteropeptidase (activates trypsinogen).

### Large intestine (colon)
- Absorbs **water, electrolytes (especially Na+), and vitamins** (K and some B vitamins produced by gut bacteria).
- Bacteria ferment undigested material.
- Forms and stores feces. No significant enzymatic digestion.

## 2. Digestive Hormones

| Hormone | Source | Trigger | Actions |
|---------|--------|---------|---------|
| **Gastrin** | G cells (stomach antrum) | Peptides/amino acids in stomach, stomach distension, vagal stimulation | Stimulates HCl secretion by parietal cells, stimulates gastric motility, promotes mucosal growth |
| **Secretin** | S cells (duodenum) | Acidic chyme entering duodenum (low pH) | Stimulates pancreatic **bicarbonate** secretion, inhibits gastric acid secretion, stimulates bile secretion from liver |
| **CCK** (cholecystokinin) | I cells (duodenum/jejunum) | Fatty acids and amino acids in duodenum | Stimulates pancreatic **enzyme** secretion, stimulates **gallbladder contraction** (bile release), stimulates bile duct relaxation (sphincter of Oddi), inhibits gastric emptying, promotes satiety |

Mnemonic: **S**ecretin handles the **S**odium bicarbonate (acid neutralization). **C**CK handles **C**ontraction of gallbladder and enzyme release.

**GIP** (gastric inhibitory peptide/glucose-dependent insulinotropic peptide): Released by K cells of the duodenum in response to glucose and fat. Decreases gastric motility and stimulates insulin release.

## 3. Liver Functions (Accessory Organ)

The liver is a metabolic powerhouse with diverse functions:

- **Bile production** -- Bile salts (made from cholesterol) emulsify fats. Bile is stored and concentrated in the **gallbladder** between meals, then released into the duodenum via the common bile duct.
- **Detoxification** -- Metabolizes drugs, alcohol, and toxins (cytochrome P450 enzymes). Converts ammonia (NH3, toxic) to **urea** via the **urea cycle** for excretion by kidneys.
- **Gluconeogenesis** -- Synthesizes glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors (amino acids, lactate, glycerol) during fasting.
- **Glycogen storage and glycogenolysis** -- Stores glucose as glycogen after meals; breaks it down between meals to maintain blood glucose.
- **Plasma protein synthesis** -- Albumin (oncotic pressure), clotting factors (fibrinogen, prothrombin), complement proteins.
- **Cholesterol and lipoprotein synthesis** -- Produces VLDL; converts cholesterol to bile salts.
- **First-pass metabolism** -- Blood from the GI tract goes to the liver via the **hepatic portal vein** before reaching systemic circulation. Drugs taken orally are partially metabolized by the liver before reaching target tissues (reducing bioavailability).
- **Bilirubin processing** -- Conjugates bilirubin (from heme breakdown) for excretion in bile.
- **Storage** -- Vitamins (A, D, B12, iron).

**Pancreas (exocrine portion)** and **gallbladder** are described above (under small intestine and liver, respectively). The pancreas is also endocrine -- see `BB_Bio_Ch05_Endocrine.md` for insulin/glucagon detail.

## 4. Absorption

Surface area maximization is critical: **circular folds** (plicae circulares) > **villi** > **microvilli** (brush border).

- **Carbohydrates**: Absorbed as monosaccharides (glucose, fructose, galactose). Glucose and galactose via Na+-dependent cotransport (SGLT1). Fructose via facilitated diffusion (GLUT5). All exit basolateral side via GLUT2.
- **Proteins**: Absorbed as amino acids, dipeptides, and tripeptides. Amino acids via Na+-dependent cotransport and facilitated diffusion.
- **Fats**: Fatty acids and monoglycerides enter enterocytes by simple diffusion. Inside, they are re-esterified to triglycerides and packaged with cholesterol, phospholipids, and apolipoproteins into **chylomicrons**. Chylomicrons are too large for blood capillaries, so they enter the **lacteals** (lymphatic capillaries in villi) and eventually reach the blood via the thoracic duct.
- **Water**: Absorbed throughout the small and large intestines by osmosis.
- **Iron**: Absorbed in the duodenum. Stored as ferritin. Transported in blood bound to transferrin.
- **Vitamin B12**: Requires intrinsic factor (from parietal cells) for absorption in the terminal ileum.

---

## 5. Defecation

Undigested material, water, and electrolytes that pass into the colon are processed for elimination. The colon absorbs most of the remaining water, forming solid feces. The rectum stores feces; defecation occurs via coordinated relaxation of the internal anal sphincter (smooth muscle, involuntary) and external anal sphincter (skeletal muscle, voluntary). The defecation reflex is parasympathetically mediated.

## High-Yield Summary: What to Memorize (Digestive)

- **Mouth:** salivary amylase (starch). **Stomach:** pepsin (protein, low pH).
- **Stomach cells:** chief = pepsinogen; parietal = HCl + intrinsic factor; G = gastrin; D = somatostatin; mucous = mucus/HCO3-.
- **Pancreas (exocrine):** trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen, lipase + colipase, amylase, nucleases, HCO3-. Activated by enterokinase → trypsin → activates the rest.
- **Bile** (from liver, stored in gallbladder) emulsifies fats — does NOT digest them enzymatically.
- **Hormones:** Gastrin (HCl up). Secretin (HCO3- up). CCK (enzymes + gallbladder contract).
- **Absorption:** carbs/protein → blood (hepatic portal). Fats → chylomicrons → lacteals → thoracic duct → blood.
- **B12** absorbed in **terminal ileum** with intrinsic factor.
- **Large intestine** = water + electrolyte absorption + bacterial vitamin synthesis. No enzymatic digestion.
- **Parasympathetic (vagus)** stimulates digestion; **sympathetic** suppresses it.
