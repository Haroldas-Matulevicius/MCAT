# CARS -- Critical Analysis and Reasoning Skills

## Deep-Dive Strategy Guide

CARS is the only MCAT section where outside knowledge is not just unnecessary -- it's a liability. Every answer lives inside the passage. This section tests whether you can read a dense, unfamiliar text and reason about it under pressure. You cannot cram for CARS. You build this skill over months of consistent, deliberate practice.

This guide covers the structure of the section, how to read passages efficiently, how to approach every question type, and how to build the skill over time.

---

## 1. Section Overview

**Format:**
- 9 passages, each 500--600 words
- 5--7 questions per passage
- 53 questions total
- 90 minutes

**Time budget:** ~10 minutes per passage (reading + questions). This is tight. Every second counts, and pacing is a skill you train separately from comprehension.

**Content split:**
- Roughly 50% humanities: philosophy, ethics, art criticism, literature, religion, cultural studies, music theory
- Roughly 50% social sciences: history, economics, political science, anthropology, psychology, sociology

**The cardinal rule:** NO outside knowledge. If you know something about the topic that isn't stated or implied in the passage, it is irrelevant. Answers that sound right because of your background knowledge but aren't grounded in the passage are traps.

**Why CARS matters for your score:**
- It is the hardest section to improve quickly, which means starting now (months before the exam) gives you maximum runway
- Medical schools weigh CARS heavily -- some Canadian schools use it as a hard cutoff
- The reasoning skills transfer directly to the science sections: passage interpretation, experimental reasoning, and answer elimination all use the same muscles

**Scoring reality:** CARS is scaled 118--132. The curve is unforgiving. Missing 5--7 questions can drop you from a 130 to a 127. Precision matters more here than in any other section.

## 2. Passage Types and How to Approach Each

Not all CARS passages are built the same. Recognizing the type within the first paragraph helps you set expectations for what you need to extract.

### Humanities Passages

**Characteristics:**
- Abstract, conceptual, argument-driven
- Often revolve around a thinker's position or a debate between schools of thought
- Heavy use of qualifiers ("perhaps," "it might be argued," "one could contend")
- Tone ranges from detached academic to passionate advocacy
- May include references to specific works of art, literature, or philosophical texts

**Approach:**
- Focus on the **argument structure** above all else. Who claims what? What supports it?
- Track the author's **opinion** carefully. In philosophy and ethics passages, the author often evaluates competing positions. Know which one they favor.
- Don't panic if the content feels alien. You don't need to understand Heidegger's ontology -- you need to understand what the passage *says* about it.
- Watch for **shifts in perspective**. The author may present a view sympathetically in one paragraph and dismantle it in the next.

### Social Science Passages

**Characteristics:**
- More concrete and evidence-based than humanities
- May reference studies, data, historical events, or policy outcomes
- Arguments are often built on evidence chains rather than pure logic
- Tone tends to be more neutral/analytical, though persuasion still appears

**Approach:**
- Map the **evidence structure**. What claims are made, and what evidence supports each?
- Pay attention to **cause-and-effect relationships**. Social science passages love to argue that X caused Y, and questions will test whether you can distinguish correlation from causation within the passage.
- Note any **concessions** the author makes. If the author acknowledges a limitation or counterargument, that often becomes a question.
- Track **scope**. Social science authors often make claims about specific populations, time periods, or contexts. Questions will test whether you over-generalize.

### Cross-Cutting Passage Styles

Regardless of subject, passages tend to follow one of three rhetorical modes:

**Argumentative:** The author takes a clear position and defends it. Your job: identify the thesis, the supporting evidence, the counterarguments addressed, and any assumptions. These passages generate the most "reasoning beyond the text" questions.

**Informative:** The author presents information, theories, or perspectives without a strong personal stance. Your job: map the organizational structure -- what's being compared, contrasted, or categorized. These passages generate more comprehension and "reasoning within the text" questions.

**Persuasive:** The author is trying to convince you of something using rhetorical tools -- emotional appeals, strategic framing, selective evidence. Your job: identify the persuasive techniques and the author's underlying values. These passages often generate questions about what the author would say in a new situation.

## 3. Reading Strategy -- Passage Mapping

How you read a CARS passage determines everything. Reading passively -- letting your eyes move over the words without active processing -- is the single most common reason students underperform on CARS.

### Active Reading: What It Actually Means

Active reading means you are **interrogating** the text as you go. After every paragraph, you should be able to answer:

1. **What did the author just say?** (Main point of the paragraph)
2. **Why is this here?** (Its role in the larger argument)
3. **What's the author's attitude?** (Neutral? Critical? Supportive? Skeptical?)

If you can't answer these three questions, you didn't read the paragraph -- you scanned it.

### Paragraph-by-Paragraph Mapping

Build a mental map as you read. You don't need to write anything down (and shouldn't, given time constraints), but you should hold a rough structural outline in your head:

- **P1:** Introduces the topic and the central question or debate
- **P2:** Presents position A with supporting evidence
- **P3:** Challenges position A or introduces position B
- **P4:** Author weighs in, synthesizes, or offers a resolution

This is an example template. Real passages vary. The point is that you know **what each paragraph does**, not just what it says.

**Key elements to identify during your read:**
- **Main idea:** Not the topic -- the *claim*. "This passage is about art" is a topic. "This passage argues that modern art criticism has abandoned aesthetic judgment in favor of political posturing" is a main idea.
- **Author's tone:** Positive, negative, neutral, ambivalent, skeptical, cautious, enthusiastic. Look for **loaded words**, **qualifiers**, and **rhetorical questions** as tone indicators.
- **Argument structure:** Thesis, evidence, counterarguments, concessions, conclusion.
- **Key terms:** Words that carry specific meaning within the passage. The author may define a term differently than you'd expect -- use their definition, not yours.

### Time Allocation

- **Reading the passage:** 4--5 minutes. This feels slow, but a thorough first read saves time on questions. Rushing through the passage and then re-reading it three times per question is slower.
- **Answering questions:** 5--6 minutes. Roughly 1 minute per question, with some buffer for harder ones.

### What NOT to Do on First Read

- **Don't memorize details.** You can always go back. Know *where* things are, not the exact wording.
- **Don't get stuck on a confusing sentence.** If one sentence is impenetrable, move on. The surrounding context usually clarifies it, and the sentence may not even be tested.
- **Don't form opinions about the topic.** Your personal views on the subject are irrelevant and dangerous. They bias your answer choices.

## 4. Skill 1 -- Foundations of Comprehension (~30% of Questions)

These questions test whether you understood what the passage actually said. They feel like the "easy" ones, but they're where careless errors cost points.

### Main Idea Questions

**What they look like:**
- "The central thesis of the passage is..."
- "Which of the following best summarizes the author's argument?"
- "The passage is primarily concerned with..."

**Strategy:**
- The correct answer must capture the **scope** and **direction** of the entire passage, not just one paragraph.
- Eliminate answers that are **too narrow** (they describe only one part of the passage) or **too broad** (they generalize beyond what the passage covers).
- Eliminate answers that get the **direction wrong** (e.g., saying the author supports something they actually criticize).
- The main idea is usually established or foreshadowed in the first and last paragraphs. If you're unsure, re-read those.

**Common trap:** An answer that accurately describes a detail from the passage but doesn't capture the overall argument. It's *true* but it's not the *main idea*.

### Detail Questions

**What they look like:**
- "According to the passage, the author states that..."
- "The passage mentions which of the following as a characteristic of..."

**Strategy:**
- These are **look-up** questions. Go back to the passage and find the relevant sentence.
- Don't rely on memory. Even if you think you remember, confirm it. Passage details are deliberately dense, and the wrong answers are designed to exploit misremembered details.
- Match the passage wording closely. The correct answer is often a paraphrase, not a direct quote, but it preserves the meaning exactly.

### Inference Questions

**What they look like:**
- "It can be inferred from the passage that..."
- "The author would most likely agree that..."
- "The passage implies that..."

**Strategy:**
- The answer must be **necessarily** true based on the passage -- not just plausibly true.
- A valid inference is one small logical step from what's stated. If you need two or three leaps of logic, you've gone too far.
- Test each answer: "Does the passage provide direct evidence for this, or am I making an assumption?" If you're assuming, it's probably wrong.

**Common trap:** An answer that *could* be true and seems reasonable but isn't actually supported by the passage text. "Could be true" is not the same as "must be true."

### Vocabulary in Context

**What they look like:**
- "As used in the passage, the word 'radical' most nearly means..."
- "The author's use of the term 'economy' refers to..."

**Strategy:**
- Ignore your default definition. The MCAT specifically tests whether you can determine meaning from context.
- Go to the sentence. Read the sentence before it and after it. The meaning is embedded in the surrounding text.
- Substitute each answer choice into the sentence. The one that preserves the sentence's meaning is correct.

### Author's Tone

**What they look like:**
- "The author's attitude toward the theory described in paragraph 2 is best described as..."
- "The tone of the passage is most accurately characterized as..."

**Strategy:**
- Look for **signal words**: "unfortunately," "remarkably," "merely," "so-called" -- these reveal attitude.
- Look for **qualifiers**: "seems to," "arguably," "to some extent" -- these indicate caution or ambivalence.
- Look for **rhetorical questions**: These often signal skepticism or are used to introduce a point the author disagrees with.
- Eliminate extremes. CARS authors are usually nuanced. "Outraged condemnation" is almost never right. "Cautious skepticism" or "qualified approval" are far more common.

## 5. Skill 2 -- Reasoning Within the Text (~30% of Questions)

These questions test whether you can analyze the passage's internal logic -- not just what it says, but how the argument works.

### Evaluating Arguments

**What they look like:**
- "Which of the following, if true, would most weaken the author's argument?"
- "The author's reasoning is most vulnerable to which criticism?"
- "The evidence presented in paragraph 3 is best described as..."

**Strategy:**
- Break the argument into components: **claim**, **evidence**, **reasoning** (the logical connection between evidence and claim), and **assumptions** (unstated beliefs the argument depends on).
- Weak arguments often have: insufficient evidence, overgeneralization, false analogies, or unacknowledged counterevidence.
- When asked what weakens an argument, look for an answer that **breaks the link** between the evidence and the conclusion. The most effective weakener attacks an assumption, not the evidence itself.

### Identifying Relationships

**What they look like:**
- "The relationship between the ideas in paragraph 2 and paragraph 4 is best described as..."
- "The author discusses X primarily in order to..."

**Strategy:**
- Common relationships: **cause and effect**, **comparison**, **contrast**, **analogy**, **example and generalization**, **concession and rebuttal**.
- Ask: "Why did the author put this here?" Everything in a well-constructed passage serves a purpose. A historical example illustrates a point. A counterargument is raised to be addressed. A concession strengthens the author's credibility before they reassert their thesis.
- The function of a paragraph or sentence is usually more important than its content for these questions.

### Recognizing Assumptions

**What they look like:**
- "The author's argument assumes which of the following?"
- "Which of the following is an underlying assumption of the theory described?"

**Strategy:**
- An assumption is something that **must be true** for the argument to work but is **not explicitly stated**.
- To find assumptions: identify the conclusion, identify the evidence, and ask "what connects these?" The unstated connector is the assumption.
- Example: If an author argues "standardized testing should be abolished because it doesn't measure creativity," the assumption is that measuring creativity is an important function of testing. That's never stated -- it's assumed.

### Distinguishing Supported vs. Unsupported Claims

**Strategy:**
- A **supported claim** has evidence or reasoning provided in the passage.
- An **unsupported claim** is asserted without backing -- it may be presented as obvious, common knowledge, or simply stated as fact.
- The MCAT tests whether you can tell the difference. When a question asks about the strength of an argument, unsupported claims are weaknesses.

### The Master Question for Skill 2

For every piece of evidence, example, or digression in the passage, ask: **"WHY does the author include this?"**

This single question unlocks most Skill 2 questions. If you know the *purpose* of every component, you can answer questions about argument structure, rhetorical strategy, and logical relationships.

## 6. Skill 3 -- Reasoning Beyond the Text (~40% of Questions)

This is the largest category and the hardest. These questions ask you to take the author's reasoning and **apply it to new situations**. You need to understand the author's logic deeply enough to predict what they'd say about something they never actually discussed.

### Apply and Extrapolate

**What they look like:**
- "Based on the passage, the author would most likely support which of the following policies?"
- "The author's argument, if applied to [new domain], would suggest that..."

**Strategy:**
- You must understand not just the author's conclusion but their **values and reasoning principles**.
- If an author argues against standardized testing because it privileges rote memorization over critical thinking, they would likely oppose other systems that privilege rote memorization -- even ones never mentioned in the passage.
- The key is identifying the **underlying principle**, not the specific position. The specific position is about testing; the underlying principle is about valuing critical thinking over memorization.

### New Information Questions

**What they look like:**
- "Suppose a new study found [X]. How would this affect the author's argument?"
- "If the following were discovered to be true, which of the author's claims would be most undermined?"

**Strategy:**
- Read the new information carefully. Determine whether it **supports**, **undermines**, or is **irrelevant to** the author's argument.
- To assess impact: identify which specific claim or assumption the new information targets.
- If the new info contradicts a key piece of evidence, the argument is weakened.
- If the new info supports an assumption the author made, the argument is strengthened.
- If the new info is tangential, the answer is usually "it would have no significant effect" or the closest equivalent.

**Common trap:** Assuming new information is devastating when it only affects a minor point, or assuming it's irrelevant when it actually undermines a core assumption.

### Strengthen and Weaken

**What they look like:**
- "Which of the following, if true, would most strengthen the author's position?"
- "Which finding would most undermine the author's conclusion?"

**Strategy:**
- To **strengthen**: find an answer that provides additional evidence for the conclusion, supports an assumption, or eliminates a potential counterargument.
- To **weaken**: find an answer that provides a counterexample, undermines a key assumption, or shows the evidence doesn't actually support the conclusion.
- The most powerful strengthener/weakener targets the **assumption** connecting evidence to conclusion -- not the evidence itself.
- Rank the answer choices by **impact**. "Most strengthen" means you need the one with the greatest effect, not just any answer that somewhat supports the argument.

### Analogous Situations

**What they look like:**
- "Which of the following situations is most analogous to the one described in the passage?"
- "The author's reasoning about X is most similar to..."

**Strategy:**
- Strip the passage argument down to its **abstract structure**. Ignore the specific content and look at the logical pattern.
- Example: If the passage argues "X was once dismissed by experts but later proved revolutionary," the analogous structure is "something initially rejected by authorities that turned out to be important." The content doesn't matter -- a scientific discovery, an artistic movement, a political reform could all fit the structure.
- Match the **relationship** between elements, not the surface features. Two situations about art are not analogous just because they're both about art. Two situations with completely different subjects ARE analogous if they share the same logical structure.

### The Master Strategy for Skill 3

**Get inside the author's head.** Before answering any Beyond the Text question, ask yourself:

1. What does the author believe?
2. What does the author value?
3. What reasoning pattern does the author use?
4. Given those beliefs, values, and reasoning patterns, what would they say about this new situation?

If you can answer those four questions, you can predict the author's response to almost anything.

## 7. Question Type Strategies

Beyond the three skill categories, certain question formats require specific tactical approaches.

### EXCEPT/NOT/LEAST Questions

**Example:** "All of the following are mentioned in the passage EXCEPT..."

**Strategy:**
- These are time-consuming. You need to verify three answer choices ARE in the passage and identify the one that ISN'T.
- Go through each answer choice systematically. Check it against the passage. Mark it as "yes, this is in the passage" or "no, I can't find this."
- The answer is the one you can't find support for.
- **Don't rush these.** The MCAT designs the wrong answers to sound plausible. Verify each one.

### Roman Numeral Questions

**Example:** "Which of the following claims are supported by the passage? I. [claim] II. [claim] III. [claim]"

**Strategy:**
- Evaluate each Roman numeral **independently**. Don't let your judgment of one affect the others.
- Start with the numeral you're most confident about. If you're sure II is correct, eliminate all answer choices that don't include II. This often narrows four options to two.
- Then evaluate the remaining numerals to distinguish between the surviving answer choices.

### Two-Part Answer Choices

**Example:** Answer choices have two components, like "A is true BECAUSE B" or "A, not B."

**Strategy:**
- **Both parts must be correct.** If one part is wrong, the whole answer is wrong, even if the other part is perfect.
- Evaluate each part separately, then check the relationship between them (usually a causal or explanatory link).
- This is where half-right answers are most dangerous. Don't get seduced by a correct first half.

### "Most Likely" vs. "Must Be True"

**"Must be true"** requires certainty. The answer is a direct logical consequence of the passage. There is no room for ambiguity.

**"Most likely"** allows for judgment. The answer is the best-supported option, even if it's not 100% certain. You're choosing the most reasonable inference among the four options.

Knowing which standard applies changes how aggressively you eliminate. For "must be true," anything uncertain is out. For "most likely," you're comparing relative strength of support.

## 8. Process of Elimination (POE)

On the MCAT, POE is not a backup strategy -- it's the primary strategy. You will rarely read an answer choice and think "that's obviously right." More often, you'll eliminate three wrong answers and the one that's left is correct.

### The Four Eliminations

**1. Eliminate Extremes**

Words like **"always," "never," "all," "none," "entirely," "completely"** are red flags. CARS passages are nuanced. Authors rarely make absolute claims, and the correct answer usually reflects that nuance.

- "The author completely rejects..." -- Probably wrong. The author likely acknowledges some merit.
- "The author is somewhat skeptical of..." -- Much more likely to be correct.

Exception: If the passage itself uses extreme language ("this theory is entirely without merit"), then an extreme answer may be justified. But this is rare.

**2. Eliminate Out-of-Scope**

Any answer that requires knowledge **not found in the passage** is wrong. This includes:

- Scientific facts you know from studying other MCAT sections
- Historical details not mentioned in the passage
- Common sense reasoning that goes beyond what the author stated
- Information from other passages you've read

If you catch yourself thinking "well, I know from my biology class that..." -- stop. That's outside knowledge, and the answer is wrong.

**3. Eliminate Half-Right Answers**

These are the most dangerous wrong answers. One part of the answer is supported by the passage, and the other part is not (or is contradicted). The supported part catches your eye and you pick it without checking the rest.

**Always read the entire answer choice.** If any part of it is wrong, the whole thing is wrong.

**4. Eliminate Opposites**

Some answers say the **opposite** of what the passage states. These are designed for students who misread or misremember. They can be especially tricky when the passage uses double negatives or complex syntax.

If an answer contradicts something you're confident the passage says, it's wrong. But **confirm** by going back to the passage -- don't rely on memory for this.

### When Stuck Between Two

This will happen regularly. You've eliminated two answers and are stuck between the remaining two. Here's the protocol:

1. **Go back to the passage.** Find the specific sentences relevant to the question.
2. **Re-read the question stem.** Make sure you're answering what's actually being asked. Often, one of the two answers responds to a slightly different question.
3. **Compare the two answers directly.** What's the specific difference between them? Usually it's a matter of scope, tone, or one key word.
4. **Pick the one with direct textual support.** If one requires an assumption and the other doesn't, choose the one that doesn't.
5. **If still stuck after 30 seconds, pick your best guess, flag it, and move on.** Spending 3 minutes on one question costs you time on 2--3 other questions.

## 9. Pacing and Timing

CARS timing is brutal. 90 minutes for 9 passages means exactly 10 minutes per passage. There is virtually no buffer. You must train pacing as deliberately as you train comprehension.

### The 10-Minute Framework

| Phase | Time | What You're Doing |
|-------|------|-------------------|
| Read passage | 4:00--5:00 | Active reading, building passage map |
| Answer questions | 5:00--6:00 | ~1 min per question, using POE |
| **Total** | **~10:00** | **Per passage** |

### Pacing Rules

**Rule 1: Don't spend more than 90 seconds on any single question.**
If you've been staring at a question for over a minute and are no closer to an answer, flag it and move on. Come back with fresh eyes if time permits.

**Rule 2: Flag and move.**
The flag function exists for a reason. Use it for:
- Questions where you're torn between two answers
- Questions where you picked an answer but aren't confident
- Questions you skipped entirely

**Rule 3: Keep a rough time check.**
- After passage 3: ~30 minutes elapsed
- After passage 6: ~60 minutes elapsed
- After passage 9: ~90 minutes elapsed

If you're falling behind, identify why. Are you spending too long reading? Speed up your first read (but don't sacrifice comprehension). Are you spending too long on questions? Be more aggressive with POE and flagging.

**Rule 4: All 53 questions must have an answer.**
There is no guessing penalty. Never leave a question blank. If you're running out of time, make your best guess on flagged questions and move on.

### The Two-Pass Approach

**First pass (primary):** Read the passage, answer each question. If a question is hard, spend up to 90 seconds, then make your best guess, flag it, and continue.

**Second pass (cleanup):** After completing all 9 passages, return to flagged questions with any remaining time. You now have the benefit of having seen all passages, which sometimes provides fresh perspective. Re-read the relevant passage section and reconsider.

Most students don't have much time for the second pass. That's fine -- the first pass is where your score is made. The second pass is a bonus.

### Practice Timing from Day 1

**This is non-negotiable.** Untimed CARS practice teaches you to be a slow, careful reader -- which is exactly the wrong skill for the actual exam. You need to be a *fast*, careful reader.

- When you first start practicing, you might take 15 minutes per passage. That's okay for the first week.
- By week 2--3, start timing yourself. Set a 10-minute timer per passage.
- If you can't finish in 10 minutes, don't extend the time -- stop, guess on remaining questions, and review what slowed you down.
- Over time, your speed will increase without sacrificing accuracy. But only if you practice under time pressure.

## 10. Building CARS Skills Over Time

CARS is a skill, not a knowledge base. You build it through practice, review, and consistency -- not through memorization or content review.

### Daily Practice

**Minimum:** 1 passage per day, every day, timed.

This is the single most important habit for CARS improvement. One passage takes 10 minutes. There is no excuse to skip it.

As you get closer to the exam, increase to 2--3 passages per day. But consistency matters more than volume. One passage every day for 6 months beats 5 passages a day for 2 weeks.

**Where to get passages:**
- AAMC CARS QPacks (save some for later, but start with these to calibrate to AAMC style)
- AAMC Section Banks (harder, save for later phases)
- Third-party resources: Jack Westin (free daily passage), UWorld, Khan Academy
- Note: Third-party passages differ in style from AAMC. They're useful for building stamina but don't perfectly replicate AAMC logic. Always calibrate with AAMC materials.

### Reviewing Wrong Answers: The Most Important Step

Getting a question wrong is only useful if you understand **why** you got it wrong. After every practice passage, go through every question -- right and wrong -- and categorize your performance.

**For wrong answers, identify the root cause:**

| Error Type | What Happened | Fix |
|------------|---------------|-----|
| **Comprehension error** | You misunderstood what the passage said | Slow down your reading; practice passage mapping |
| **Misread the question** | You answered a different question than what was asked | Read the question stem twice before looking at answers |
| **Fell for a trap answer** | You picked a half-right or out-of-scope answer | Practice POE more deliberately; check every part of the answer |
| **Reasoning error** | You understood the passage but drew the wrong conclusion | Practice inference and extrapolation; study argument structure |
| **Timing pressure** | You rushed and picked without thinking | Practice pacing; flag and return instead of rushing |
| **Outside knowledge** | You used what you know instead of what the passage says | Catch yourself when you think "I know that..." -- stop and go back to the passage |

**Keep a running log of your errors.** After 2--3 weeks, patterns will emerge. Maybe you consistently miss inference questions. Maybe you lose points on "reasoning beyond the text." Whatever the pattern, that's where you focus your improvement.

### Building Reading Stamina

CARS rewards people who read challenging material regularly. If the only dense text you read is MCAT passages, you'll struggle with stamina and engagement.

**Read challenging non-science material for 20--30 minutes a day:**
- Philosophy: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy articles
- Literary criticism: New York Review of Books, London Review of Books
- Opinion/analysis: The Atlantic, Aeon, The Economist (opinion pieces)
- History: Academic book reviews, historiographical essays
- Ethics: Bioethics journals, applied ethics articles

**As you read, practice the same skills:**
- What's the author's main argument?
- What evidence do they use?
- What are they assuming?
- Do I agree or disagree -- and why? (This last one is just for engagement; on the real exam, your opinion is irrelevant.)

### Error Tracking Template

Use this framework to track your CARS practice over time:

```
Date: [date]
Source: [AAMC QPack 1, Jack Westin, etc.]
Passage topic: [brief description]
Time: [how long the passage took]
Score: [X/Y correct]
Errors:
  - Q[#]: [error type] -- [brief explanation of what went wrong]
Pattern notes: [any recurring issues you notice]
```

After every 10 passages, review your error log and identify your top 2--3 weaknesses. Those become your focus areas for the next 10 passages.

### Progression Plan

**Weeks 1--4: Foundation**
- 1 passage per day, timed at 12 minutes (slightly generous)
- Focus on passage mapping and identifying main ideas
- Review every question thoroughly
- Start your error log
- Begin reading challenging non-science material daily

**Weeks 5--8: Tighten Timing**
- 1 passage per day, timed at 10 minutes (exam pace)
- Focus on POE technique and Skill 2 questions
- Start identifying your weakest question types
- Aim for 70%+ accuracy

**Weeks 9--12: Build Volume**
- 2 passages per day at exam pace
- Focus on Skill 3 questions (reasoning beyond the text)
- Do timed sets of 3 passages (30 minutes) to build stamina
- Aim for 75%+ accuracy

**Weeks 13--16: Simulate**
- Full 9-passage timed sections (90 minutes)
- Review every question, update error log
- Target weak areas identified by your error log
- Aim for 80%+ accuracy

**Weeks 17+: Peak Performance**
- Continue full timed sections
- Shift to AAMC materials exclusively
- Fine-tune pacing and POE
- Aim for 85%+ accuracy on AAMC materials

## Quick Reference: CARS Decision Framework

When you sit down with a passage, run this mental checklist:

**While reading:**
1. What type of passage is this? (Argumentative, informative, persuasive)
2. What is the main idea? (Not the topic -- the claim)
3. What is the author's tone? (Look for signal words)
4. What is the structure? (Passage map: P1 does X, P2 does Y...)

**For each question:**
1. Read the question stem carefully. What is it actually asking?
2. Predict an answer before looking at choices (when possible).
3. Eliminate wrong answers using POE (extremes, out-of-scope, half-right, opposites).
4. Pick the best remaining answer. If torn between two, go back to the passage.
5. If stuck for more than 90 seconds: pick, flag, move on.

**After the section:**
1. Review every question (right and wrong).
2. Log errors with root cause.
3. Update your pattern tracker.
4. Adjust practice focus based on patterns.

## Key Takeaways

1. **CARS cannot be crammed.** Start practicing now and practice every single day.
2. **No outside knowledge.** Every answer is in the passage. If it's not in the passage, it's wrong.
3. **Active reading is the foundation.** If you don't understand the passage structure, you can't answer the questions.
4. **POE is your primary tool.** You find the right answer by eliminating wrong ones.
5. **Review is where learning happens.** Doing passages without reviewing is wasted time.
6. **Pacing is a skill.** Train it from the beginning. Untimed practice creates bad habits.
7. **Track your errors.** Patterns reveal your weaknesses. Fix weaknesses systematically.
8. **Read challenging material daily.** Build the reading muscles outside of MCAT practice.
9. **Skill 3 is 40% of your score.** Master "reasoning beyond the text" to maximize your CARS score.
10. **The author's reasoning is everything.** Understand their values, logic, and assumptions, and you can predict their response to anything.
