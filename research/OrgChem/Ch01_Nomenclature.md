## 1. IUPAC Naming Rules (General)

The MCAT rarely makes you generate a name from scratch, but you MUST recognize the structure from a name and vice versa. The systematic approach:

1. **Find the longest continuous carbon chain** containing the highest-priority functional group → this is the parent chain.
2. **Number the parent chain** so the highest-priority functional group gets the lowest locant.
3. **Identify substituents** (alkyl groups, halogens, etc.) and number their position.
4. **Assemble the name:** prefixes (substituents in alphabetical order) + parent chain + suffix (functional group).

### Parent Chain Length Roots

| Carbons | Root |
|---------|------|
| 1 | meth- |
| 2 | eth- |
| 3 | prop- |
| 4 | but- |
| 5 | pent- |
| 6 | hex- |
| 7 | hept- |
| 8 | oct- |
| 9 | non- |
| 10 | dec- |

### Functional Group Priority (highest to lowest, for suffix)

| Group | Suffix | Prefix (when not principal) |
|-------|--------|----------------------------|
| Carboxylic acid | -oic acid | carboxy- |
| Ester | -oate | (alkoxy)carbonyl- |
| Amide | -amide | carbamoyl- |
| Nitrile | -nitrile | cyano- |
| Aldehyde | -al | oxo- |
| Ketone | -one | oxo- |
| Alcohol | -ol | hydroxy- |
| Amine | -amine | amino- |
| Alkene | -ene | -- |
| Alkyne | -yne | -- |
| Alkane | -ane | -- |
| Halide | -- | fluoro/chloro/bromo/iodo- |

The highest-priority group gets the suffix; everything else becomes a prefix.

## 2. Hydrocarbons

### Alkanes (-ane)
Saturated; only single bonds. Formula: CnH₂n+₂. Examples: methane (CH₄), ethane, propane, butane.

### Alkenes (-ene)
Contain at least one C=C double bond. Formula: CnH₂n (one double bond). Number to give the double bond the lowest locant. Cis/trans (or E/Z) for stereochemistry.

### Alkynes (-yne)
Contain at least one C≡C triple bond. Formula: CnH₂n-₂ (one triple bond).

### Alkyl Substituents
- Methyl (-CH₃), ethyl (-C₂H₅), propyl (-C₃H₇), isopropyl, butyl, sec-butyl, isobutyl, tert-butyl.
- Common ring nomenclature: cyclopropane, cyclohexane, etc.

### Aromatic Compounds
- **Benzene** (C₆H₆) — the parent. Substituted benzenes use ortho (1,2-), meta (1,3-), para (1,4-).
- Common names: toluene (methylbenzene), phenol (hydroxybenzene), aniline (aminobenzene), benzoic acid.

## 3. Alcohols (-ol)

R-OH where R is an alkyl group.

- **Primary (1°):** -OH on a carbon bonded to one other C
- **Secondary (2°):** -OH on a carbon bonded to two other C
- **Tertiary (3°):** -OH on a carbon bonded to three other C

Examples: methanol, ethanol, 2-propanol (isopropyl alcohol), 1-butanol vs 2-butanol.

**Diols (two -OH):** ethylene glycol (1,2-ethanediol), glycerol (1,2,3-propanetriol).

**Phenol:** -OH directly attached to an aromatic ring. More acidic than typical alcohols (pKa ~10 vs ~17) due to resonance stabilization of the phenolate anion.

---

## 4. Aldehydes (-al) and Ketones (-one)

Both have a **carbonyl group** (C=O), but the position and substitution pattern differ.

### Aldehydes
- C=O with at least one H on the carbonyl carbon (terminal)
- Suffix: -al (e.g., methanal/formaldehyde, ethanal/acetaldehyde, propanal, benzaldehyde)
- The carbonyl carbon is always C1 in the chain (no number needed).

### Ketones
- C=O with two R groups (carbonyl is internal)
- Suffix: -one. Number to give carbonyl lowest locant.
- Examples: propanone (acetone), 2-butanone, cyclohexanone.

---

## 5. Carboxylic Acids (-oic acid)

R-COOH. Highest priority on the standard MCAT functional group list.

- Suffix: -oic acid (e.g., methanoic acid/formic acid, ethanoic acid/acetic acid, propanoic acid)
- Carbonyl C is always C1.
- Aromatic: benzoic acid.
- Diacids: malonic (3C), succinic (4C), glutaric (5C), adipic (6C) — all dicarboxylic.

**Important biological examples:** lactic acid, pyruvic acid, citric acid, fatty acids (long-chain RCOOH).

---

## 6. Carboxylic Acid Derivatives

All have C=O bonded to a heteroatom-containing leaving group.

### Esters (-oate)
- R-COO-R' (carbonyl + O-alkyl)
- Named as "alkyl alkanoate" — alkyl from the OR' side, alkanoate from the carbonyl side.
- Example: methyl ethanoate (methyl acetate, CH₃COOCH₃).
- Common in biology: triglycerides, phospholipids, biological membranes.

### Amides (-amide)
- R-CO-N(H/R')₂
- Suffix: -amide (e.g., ethanamide/acetamide).
- N-substituents prefixed with N-: e.g., N-methylacetamide.
- The peptide bond is an amide bond (between AA carboxyl + amino group).

### Anhydrides (-oic anhydride)
- R-CO-O-CO-R' (two carbonyls bridged by O)
- Symmetric: ethanoic anhydride (acetic anhydride). Mixed: name both acids, "ethanoic propanoic anhydride."

### Acyl Halides / Acid Halides (-oyl halide)
- R-CO-X (X = halogen)
- Most reactive of the carboxylic acid derivatives.
- Example: ethanoyl chloride (acetyl chloride, CH₃COCl).

### Nitriles (-nitrile)
- R-C≡N
- Suffix: -nitrile (the carbon of CN is counted as C1).
- Example: ethanenitrile (acetonitrile, CH₃CN).

---

## 7. Amines

R-NH₂, R₂NH, R₃N — primary, secondary, tertiary amines respectively.

- Named with -amine suffix: methylamine, dimethylamine, trimethylamine.
- Aromatic: aniline (aminobenzene), pyridine (N in 6-membered ring).
- Important biological examples: amino acids, neurotransmitters (serotonin, dopamine, epinephrine).

---

## 8. Ethers

R-O-R'

- Named as "alkyl alkyl ether" (common) or substituent-alkoxy- (IUPAC, e.g., methoxyethane).
- Examples: dimethyl ether (methoxymethane), diethyl ether (ethoxyethane).
- Cyclic ethers: tetrahydrofuran (THF), 1,4-dioxane, epoxides (3-membered ring with O — highly reactive).

## High-Yield Takeaways

1. **Functional group priority** (carboxylic acid > ester > amide > nitrile > aldehyde > ketone > alcohol > amine > alkene > alkyne > alkane > halide) — the highest gets the suffix.
2. **Aldehyde C is always C1**; carboxylic acid C is always C1.
3. **1°/2°/3° classification** for alcohols and amines counts adjacent C atoms.
4. **Phenol is more acidic than alcohols** (resonance-stabilized phenolate).
5. **Acyl halide > anhydride > ester > amide** in reactivity (Ch09 will detail).
6. **Common-name → IUPAC translation** table above is high-yield for recognizing test passages.
