## CHAPTER 1: Biology of Behavior

**Maps to AAMC FC:** 6A (parts of nervous system) + 7A (biological bases of behavior) + parts of FC 3 (nervous system biology, cross-section with BB).
**MCAT weight:** Foundational chapter — touches every other PS topic. ~5-8% of questions directly, but underlies everything else.
**Overview:** Nervous system anatomy and function, endocrine system, brain regions, neurons and neurotransmitters, development of the nervous system, and the methods used to study the brain.

### Brief History of Neuropsychology

- Phrenology (Gall, early 1800s): the discredited idea that personality could be read from skull bumps. Important for one reason: it introduced **localization of function** — different brain regions do different things.
- Pierre Flourens (mid-1800s): used **extirpation/ablation** in animals (removing brain parts and observing behavior). First to **experimentally** prove localization of function.
- **Wilhelm Wundt (1879):** Established the **first psychology laboratory** at the **University of Leipzig** — often cited as the birth of **experimental psychology**. Founded **structuralism**, which used **introspection** (trained self-report of conscious experience) to break consciousness into basic components. **MCAT pattern:** "first psych lab" / "1879" / "Leipzig" / "structuralism" / "introspection" all = Wundt. Contrast with William James (functionalism).
- William James: founder of American **functionalism** — focus on how mental processes help organisms **adapt** to the environment (Darwinian influence). Wrote *Principles of Psychology* (1890); coined "stream of consciousness."
- John Dewey: extended functionalism to education and reflexes as whole units. His 1896 paper *The Reflex Arc Concept in Psychology* is considered the formal start of American functionalism.
- Paul Broca: studied "Tan" (a patient who could only say "tan") — localized speech production to the left frontal lobe.
- Hermann von Helmholtz: measured the speed of a nerve impulse — first to treat psychology as quantifiable physiology.
- Sir Charles Sherrington: inferred the existence of synapses (later confirmed to be chemical, not electrical as he assumed).

### Organization of the Human Nervous System

**Two main divisions:**
- **Central Nervous System (CNS):** Brain + spinal cord.
- **Peripheral Nervous System (PNS):** All neurons outside the CNS.

**PNS subdivides into:**
- **Somatic NS:** Voluntary motor control of skeletal muscle, plus sensory input.
- **Autonomic NS (ANS):** Involuntary control of smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands. Splits into:
  - **Sympathetic ("fight or flight"):** ↑ HR, ↑ BP, dilated pupils, bronchodilation, ↓ digestion, ↑ sweating, glycogenolysis. Releases **norepinephrine** at most target organs (acetylcholine at preganglionic synapses).
  - **Parasympathetic ("rest and digest"):** ↓ HR, constricted pupils, bronchoconstriction, ↑ digestion, ↑ salivation. Releases **acetylcholine** at both pre- and postganglionic synapses.
  - **Vagus nerve (CN X):** Major parasympathetic nerve, carries ~75% of all parasympathetic fibers from brain to thoracic and abdominal organs.

**MCAT mnemonic:** Sympathetic = "Stress." Parasympathetic = "Peaceful." Sympathetic uses NE peripherally, parasympathetic uses ACh.

### Organization of the Brain

**Hindbrain (rhombencephalon)** — keeps you alive:
- **Medulla oblongata:** Vital reflexes — breathing, heart rate, blood pressure, vomiting.
- **Pons:** Sensory and motor pathway between cortex and medulla; sleep/breathing role.
- **Cerebellum:** Coordination, balance, motor learning, posture.
- **Reticular formation:** Arousal, alertness, sleep-wake cycle (extends into midbrain).

**Midbrain (mesencephalon)** — sensorimotor reflexes:
- **Superior colliculi:** Visual reflexes (e.g., reflexive eye movements).
- **Inferior colliculi:** Auditory reflexes (e.g., turning toward sound).
- **Substantia nigra:** Produces dopamine; degeneration causes Parkinson's.

**Forebrain (prosencephalon)** — complex perception, emotion, cognition:
- Thalamus, hypothalamus, limbic system, basal ganglia, cerebrum (see next section).

### Parts of the Forebrain

**Thalamus:** The "sensory relay station." Every sensory modality except **olfaction** routes through here before going to cortex.

**Hypothalamus** (controls homeostasis via the "Four F's" — Feeding, Fighting, Fleeing, Sex):
- **Lateral hypothalamus (LH):** Hunger center. "Lateral = Lunch." Lesion = aphagia.
- **Ventromedial hypothalamus (VMH):** Satiety center. "Ventromedial = Very full." Lesion = hyperphagia.
- **Anterior hypothalamus:** Sexual behavior, sleep, body temperature.
- **Suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN):** Circadian rhythm pacemaker; receives retinal light input, controls melatonin release from pineal.

**Limbic system** (emotion + memory):
- **Amygdala:** Fear, aggression, emotional memory. Damage → reduced fear (Klüver-Bucy).
- **Hippocampus:** Forming new long-term explicit memories. Damage → anterograde amnesia (HM case).
- **Fornix:** White matter tract connecting hippocampus to other limbic structures.
- **Septal nuclei:** Pleasure-seeking, addiction circuits.
- **Cingulate cortex:** Emotion regulation, attention.

**Basal ganglia** (motor coordination, smooth voluntary movement):
- **Caudate, putamen, globus pallidus.**
- Disorders: Parkinson's (loss of dopamine input from substantia nigra), Huntington's (caudate/putamen degeneration → chorea).

**Cerebrum** — outer cortex divided into 4 lobes per hemisphere:

| Lobe | Primary Cortex | Functions |
|---|---|---|
| **Frontal** | Primary motor cortex (precentral gyrus) | Executive function, planning, motor control, **Broca's area** (left, speech production), personality |
| **Parietal** | Primary somatosensory cortex (postcentral gyrus) | Touch, spatial reasoning, body awareness |
| **Temporal** | Primary auditory cortex | Hearing, language comprehension (**Wernicke's area** in left), memory |
| **Occipital** | Primary visual cortex (V1) | Vision, visual processing |

**Aphasias:**
- **Broca's aphasia (expressive/non-fluent):** Damage to Broca's area. Patient understands speech but produces slow, halting, telegraphic speech. "Broca = Broken speech."
- **Wernicke's aphasia (receptive/fluent):** Damage to Wernicke's area. Patient produces fluent but meaningless "word salad" and cannot understand speech. "Wernicke = Wordy but Wrong."
- **Conduction aphasia:** Damage to **arcuate fasciculus** (connecting Broca's and Wernicke's). Comprehension and production intact, but cannot repeat what is said.

**Lateralization:** The two hemispheres specialize.
- **Dominant (usually left):** Language, analysis, logic, math.
- **Non-dominant (usually right):** Spatial reasoning, music, intuition, face recognition, emotion.

**Corpus callosum:** Massive white matter bundle connecting the two hemispheres.

**Split-brain studies (Sperry):** Patients with severed corpus callosum (treatment for severe epilepsy). Each hemisphere processes information independently. Object shown to right visual field (left hemisphere) → patient can name it. Same object to left visual field (right hemisphere) → patient cannot verbally name it but can pick it out by hand.

### Influences on Behavior — Genetics and Endocrine

**Heritability:** The proportion of variation in a trait within a population that is due to genetic differences.
- **Twin studies:** Compare monozygotic (MZ, identical) vs dizygotic (DZ, fraternal) concordance rates. Higher MZ concordance vs DZ → greater genetic contribution.
- **Adoption studies:** Compare adopted children to biological vs adoptive parents to disentangle genes from environment.
- **Family studies:** Look for trait clustering within families.
- **Gene-environment interaction:** Genes don't act in a vacuum. Environment can switch genes on/off (epigenetics) and modify the expression of genetic predispositions.

**Endocrine system — hypothalamus-pituitary axis:**
The hypothalamus controls the pituitary, which controls most other endocrine glands.

**Anterior pituitary** (mnemonic **FLAT PEG**):
- **F**SH — follicle-stimulating hormone (gonads)
- **L**H — luteinizing hormone (gonads)
- **A**CTH — adrenocorticotropic hormone (adrenal cortex → cortisol)
- **T**SH — thyroid-stimulating hormone (thyroid)
- **P**rolactin — milk production
- **E**ndorphins — pain reduction
- **G**H — growth hormone

**Posterior pituitary** (does not synthesize hormones — releases them from hypothalamus):
- **Oxytocin:** Bonding, uterine contraction, milk letdown, social trust.
- **ADH (vasopressin):** Water retention by kidneys.

**HPA axis** (stress pathway): Hypothalamus → CRH → anterior pituitary → ACTH → adrenal cortex → **cortisol**.

**Adrenal glands:**
- **Adrenal cortex:** Steroid hormones — cortisol (stress), aldosterone (Na+ retention), small amounts of sex hormones.
- **Adrenal medulla:** Epinephrine and norepinephrine (sympathetic NS, fight-or-flight).

**Thyroid:** T3 and T4 — metabolism, growth. Calcitonin (lowers blood calcium).

**Gonads:** Testes (testosterone — sex drive, aggression, secondary sex characteristics in males); Ovaries (estrogen, progesterone — menstrual cycle, pregnancy).

**Pineal gland:** Melatonin — circadian rhythm and sleep regulation.

### Development of the Nervous System

**Neurulation:** The neural tube forms from ectoderm in week 3-4 of embryogenesis.
- Neural tube → **3 primary brain vesicles** (prosencephalon, mesencephalon, rhombencephalon) → **5 secondary vesicles** (telencephalon + diencephalon, mesencephalon, metencephalon + myelencephalon) → adult brain structures.
- **Neural tube defects:**
  - **Spina bifida:** Failure of caudal neural tube to close → exposed spinal cord.
  - **Anencephaly:** Failure of rostral neural tube to close → absent forebrain. Lethal.
  - Folic acid supplementation reduces risk.

**Infant reflexes** (primitive, present at birth, disappear with CNS maturation):
- **Rooting reflex:** Infant turns head toward stimulus on cheek (feeding).
- **Sucking reflex:** Sucks anything that touches the roof of mouth.
- **Moro reflex:** Sudden loss of support → arms flung out, then drawn in (like an embrace).
- **Babinski reflex:** Stroking sole of foot → toes fan out. Normal in infants, abnormal in adults (suggests upper motor neuron damage).
- **Grasping (palmar) reflex:** Object placed in palm → infant grasps.

**Fixed action patterns:** Innate, stereotyped behaviors triggered by a specific stimulus (sign stimulus). Once initiated, they run to completion. Example: greylag goose retrieving an egg.

**Sensitive periods vs critical periods:**
- **Critical period:** A narrow window during which a specific experience MUST occur for normal development (e.g., visual cortex development).
- **Sensitive period:** A broader window during which the organism is more responsive to environmental input but development can still occur outside of it.

**Imprinting (Lorenz):** Newly hatched birds form attachment to the first moving object they see (usually the mother). Demonstrates a critical period — once the window closes, imprinting cannot occur.

### Methods to Study the Brain

| Method | What it measures | Spatial resolution | Temporal resolution |
|---|---|---|---|
| **EEG** | Electrical activity at scalp | Poor | Excellent (ms) |
| **CT** | X-ray cross-sections (structure) | Good | None (snapshot) |
| **MRI** | Magnetic field, soft tissue (structure) | Excellent | None (snapshot) |
| **fMRI** | Blood oxygenation (function) | Good | Moderate (seconds) |
| **PET** | Radioactive tracer uptake (function/metabolism) | Moderate | Poor (minutes) |
| **Lesion studies** | Behavior after damage | Variable | N/A |

**Trade-off:** Methods with high temporal resolution (EEG) sacrifice spatial detail. Methods with high spatial resolution (MRI/fMRI) sacrifice timing.

### Neurons and Glia

**Neuron anatomy:**
- **Dendrites:** Receive signals from other neurons.
- **Cell body (soma):** Contains nucleus, integrates inputs.
- **Axon hillock:** Where action potentials are initiated.
- **Axon:** Carries action potential away from cell body.
- **Myelin sheath:** Insulates axon, speeds conduction (saltatory conduction at nodes of Ranvier).
- **Axon terminals (synaptic boutons):** Release neurotransmitters into synaptic cleft.

**Glial cell types:**
- **Astrocytes:** Most abundant glia. Support, blood-brain barrier, K+ buffering, nutrient transport.
- **Oligodendrocytes (CNS):** Myelinate multiple axons in CNS.
- **Schwann cells (PNS):** Myelinate one segment of one axon in PNS.
- **Microglia:** Immune cells of CNS. Remove debris, phagocytose pathogens.
- **Ependymal cells:** Line ventricles, produce cerebrospinal fluid.

### Action Potentials

- **Resting potential:** ~ -70 mV. Maintained by **Na+/K+ ATPase pump** (3 Na+ out, 2 K+ in per ATP) and K+ leak channels.
- **Depolarization:** Stimulus opens voltage-gated Na+ channels → Na+ rushes in → membrane depolarizes. If threshold (~-55 mV) is reached → all-or-none action potential.
- **Repolarization:** Voltage-gated K+ channels open → K+ flows out → membrane returns to negative.
- **Hyperpolarization:** K+ channels stay open briefly → membrane briefly more negative than resting.
- **Refractory periods:** Absolute (no AP possible — Na+ channels inactivated) and relative (AP possible but harder).

### Synaptic Transmission

1. AP arrives at axon terminal.
2. Voltage-gated **Ca²⁺ channels** open → Ca²⁺ influx.
3. Ca²⁺ triggers vesicles to fuse with membrane → **neurotransmitter release** into synaptic cleft.
4. NT binds to receptors on postsynaptic membrane.
5. Postsynaptic effect:
   - **EPSP (excitatory postsynaptic potential):** Depolarization, makes AP more likely.
   - **IPSP (inhibitory postsynaptic potential):** Hyperpolarization, makes AP less likely.
6. **Summation** at axon hillock determines whether postsynaptic neuron fires:
   - **Temporal summation:** Multiple signals from same source over time.
   - **Spatial summation:** Multiple signals from different sources at the same time.
7. NT removed from cleft by: **reuptake** (transporters), **enzymatic degradation**, or **diffusion**.

### Neurotransmitters Table (high-yield)

| Neurotransmitter | Key Functions | Associated Conditions |
|---|---|---|
| **Acetylcholine (ACh)** | Memory, muscle contraction, parasympathetic | Alzheimer's (low ACh) |
| **Dopamine (DA)** | Reward, motivation, movement | Schizophrenia (↑ in mesolimbic), Parkinson's (↓ in nigrostriatal), addiction |
| **Norepinephrine (NE)** | Alertness, arousal, fight-or-flight | Depression (low), anxiety (high) |
| **Serotonin (5-HT)** | Mood, sleep, appetite, aggression | Depression (low), OCD |
| **GABA** | Major inhibitory NT | Anxiety (low), enhanced by alcohol/benzos |
| **Glutamate** | Major excitatory NT | Schizophrenia (NMDA hypofunction), learning (LTP) |
| **Endorphins** | Pain reduction, pleasure | Opioids mimic |

## CHAPTER 2: Sensation and Perception

**Maps to AAMC FC:** 6A (sensing the environment).
**MCAT weight:** ~6-8 questions per exam. Vision, hearing, and perception principles dominate.
**Overview:** How sensory receptors transduce stimuli into neural signals (sensation) and how the brain interprets them (perception).

### Sensation vs. Perception

These are two different processes, and the MCAT loves testing the distinction.

- **Sensation** = detection. Your sensory receptors pick up a stimulus and convert it to neural signals (**transduction**). This is bottom-level, raw data.
- **Perception** = interpretation. Your brain takes those signals and makes meaning out of them. This is where context, expectations, and experience come in.

Example: Your retina detects wavelengths of 700 nm (sensation). Your brain interprets that as "red apple" (perception).

### Thresholds

- **Absolute threshold:** the minimum stimulus intensity you can detect 50% of the time.
- **Difference threshold (JND — just noticeable difference):** the smallest change in stimulus intensity that you can detect 50% of the time.
- **Weber's law:** ΔI / I = k. The JND is proportional to the original stimulus. If you're holding a 10 lb weight, you might notice an extra 1 lb (10%). If you're holding 100 lbs, you'd need 10 lbs added to notice. The ratio stays constant. MCAT loves calculation questions with this.
- **Subliminal stimuli:** below absolute threshold. You can't consciously detect them, but they may still influence behavior (priming effects).

### Signal Detection Theory (SDT)

This replaces the simple "can you detect it or not" model with a framework that accounts for **response bias** — your willingness to say "yes, I detected it."

| | Stimulus Present | Stimulus Absent |
|---|---|---|
| **Say "Yes"** | **Hit** | **False Alarm** |
| **Say "No"** | **Miss** | **Correct Rejection** |

Detection depends on both **sensitivity** (how good your sensory system is) and **criterion** (how willing you are to say "yes"). A radiologist looking for tumors might lower their criterion (say "yes" more) to avoid misses, but that increases false alarms. This is a cost-benefit decision, not just a sensory one.

**MCAT application:** SDT shows up in passages about medical screening, security, or any detection scenario. Know the 2x2 cold.

### Sensory Adaptation

When a stimulus is constant, your receptors decrease their firing rate over time. You stop noticing it. Examples: the feel of clothes on your skin, background noise, the smell of your own home.

**Important exception:** Pain receptors do NOT fully adapt. Pain signals danger.

### Vision

**Eye anatomy pathway:** Light enters through the **cornea** (most refraction happens here) → **aqueous humor** → **pupil** (controlled by **iris**) → **lens** (fine-tunes focus via **accommodation**, controlled by ciliary muscles) → **vitreous humor** → **retina**.

**Photoreceptors:**
- **Rods:** ~120 million. Sensitive to dim light. Concentrated in the periphery. Contain **rhodopsin**. Scotopic (night) vision. One type only — no color.
- **Cones:** ~6 million. Require bright light. Concentrated in the **fovea** (center of retina, highest acuity). Three types (S, M, L for short, medium, long wavelengths). Photopic (daylight) and color vision.

**MCAT trap:** The fovea has the highest visual acuity because cones there have a 1:1 ratio with ganglion cells. In the periphery, many rods converge on one ganglion cell — great for sensitivity, terrible for detail.

**Color vision theories** (both correct, at different levels):
- **Trichromatic theory (Young-Helmholtz):** Three types of cones (R, G, B). Color = relative activation of these three. Explains color blindness (missing a cone type). Operates at **retinal level**.
- **Opponent-process theory (Hering):** Color is processed in antagonistic pairs: red-green, blue-yellow, black-white. Cells excited by one color, inhibited by the other. Explains afterimages. Operates at **ganglion cell and LGN level**.

**Visual pathway:** Retina → **optic nerve** → **optic chiasm** (nasal fibers cross) → **lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN)** of thalamus → **primary visual cortex (V1)** in occipital lobe.

- **Feature detectors (Hubel & Wiesel):** V1 neurons respond to specific features — edges, angles, movement.
- **Parallel processing:** color, motion, form, and depth processed simultaneously in different areas, then integrated.

### Hearing and Vestibular Sense

**Ear anatomy:**
- **Outer ear:** pinna (collects sound) → auditory canal → **tympanic membrane** (eardrum, vibrates).
- **Middle ear:** **ossicles** — malleus, incus, stapes (smallest bones in body). Amplify vibrations ~22x. Stapes pushes on **oval window**.
- **Inner ear:** **cochlea** (fluid-filled, coiled tube). Vibrations create waves in cochlear fluid → push on **basilar membrane** → bend **hair cells** (mechanoreceptors) → neural signals → **auditory nerve** → auditory cortex (temporal lobe), with relay through **medial geniculate nucleus (MGN)** of thalamus.

**Pitch perception theories:**
- **Place theory:** Different frequencies vibrate different locations on basilar membrane. High-frequency → **base** (near oval window, stiff/narrow). Low-frequency → **apex** (far end, flexible/wide). Best explains high-frequency hearing.
- **Frequency theory (temporal theory):** Rate of firing of auditory nerve matches frequency. Works for low frequencies (<1000 Hz). For intermediate frequencies, **volley principle** — groups of neurons fire in alternation to collectively match the frequency.

**MCAT loves:** "Base = high pitch, apex = low pitch." Counterintuitive and frequently tested.

**Hearing loss:**
- **Conductive:** Problem in outer or middle ear (blocked canal, damaged ossicles, perforated eardrum). Often treatable.
- **Sensorineural:** Damage to cochlear hair cells or auditory nerve. Usually permanent. From aging, loud noise.

**Vestibular sense:** Detects head position and acceleration.
- **Semicircular canals:** Three orthogonal canals → rotational movement (3 planes).
- **Otolith organs:** Utricle and saccule → linear acceleration and gravity. Fluid movement bends hair cells.

### Other Senses

**Somatosensation:** Touch, pressure, temperature, pain. Receptors with different specializations:
- **Meissner's corpuscles** — light touch.
- **Pacinian corpuscles** — deep pressure/vibration.
- **Merkel's discs** — sustained pressure.
- **Free nerve endings** — pain/temperature.

**Pain and gate control theory (Melzack & Wall):** Pain signals travel via small nerve fibers to spinal cord. A "gate" in the spinal cord can be closed by activation of **large nerve fibers** (non-pain touch/pressure) — this is why rubbing a stubbed toe helps. Also modulated by descending signals from the brain (distraction, mood, expectations alter pain). **Endorphins** also close the gate.

**Taste (gustation):** Five basic tastes — **sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami**. Taste receptor cells on taste buds, clustered in papillae on tongue. Cranial nerves **VII (facial), IX (glossopharyngeal), X (vagus)** carry signals to the brain.

**Smell (olfaction):** Olfactory receptor neurons in nasal cavity → **olfactory bulb** → olfactory cortex. Unique among senses because it does NOT relay through the thalamus first (goes directly to cortex and limbic system). This is why smells trigger strong emotional memories.

### Object Recognition / Perception

**Bottom-up processing (data-driven):** Building perception from raw sensory input. Start with individual features and assemble into a whole. Example: reading an unfamiliar character by character.

**Top-down processing (knowledge-driven):** Using prior knowledge, expectations, and context to interpret sensory input. Example: reading sloppy handwriting because you know what word to expect. Why "Th3 c4t s4t on th3 m4t" is easy.

**MCAT distinction:** If context/expectation matters → top-down. If you're analyzing raw features without prior expectation → bottom-up.

**Gestalt principles** (your brain organizes visual input into coherent wholes):
- **Proximity:** Objects near each other are grouped together.
- **Similarity:** Similar objects are grouped together.
- **Closure:** We fill in gaps to see complete shapes.
- **Continuity:** We follow smooth, continuous lines rather than choppy ones.
- **Figure-ground:** We separate a main figure from the background.
- **Common fate:** Objects moving in the same direction are grouped.

**Depth cues:**
- **Binocular** (require both eyes): retinal disparity (slightly different images from each eye), convergence (eyes turn inward for close objects).
- **Monocular** (one eye is enough): relative size (smaller = farther), interposition/overlap (blocking = closer), linear perspective (parallel lines converge), texture gradient, motion parallax (closer things move faster across visual field), aerial perspective (farther objects are hazier).

## CHAPTER 3: Learning, Memory, and Behavior

**Maps to AAMC FC:** 7C (learning) + 6B (memory).
**MCAT weight:** ~5-7 questions per exam. Conditioning, memory systems, and biological basis are guaranteed.
**Overview:** How experience changes behavior (learning) and how information is encoded, stored, and retrieved (memory).

### Habituation, Dishabituation, Sensitization (Non-Associative Learning)

- **Habituation:** Decreased response to a repeated, non-threatening stimulus (you stop noticing the AC humming).
- **Dishabituation:** Sudden recovery of response when a new stimulus is introduced.
- **Sensitization:** Increased response to a stimulus, often after a threatening or intense event. Can generalize to other stimuli.

### Classical Conditioning (Pavlov)

This is **learning through association**. A neutral stimulus becomes associated with a stimulus that naturally produces a response.

**Core terms:**
- **Unconditioned stimulus (UCS):** Naturally produces a response without learning (food).
- **Unconditioned response (UCR):** Natural, unlearned reaction to UCS (salivation to food).
- **Conditioned stimulus (CS):** Previously neutral stimulus that, after pairing with UCS, now produces a response (bell).
- **Conditioned response (CR):** Learned reaction to CS (salivation to bell).

**Key processes:**
- **Acquisition:** CS and UCS repeatedly paired. CS must come **before** UCS (forward conditioning is most effective).
- **Extinction:** CS presented repeatedly without UCS → CR gradually disappears.
- **Spontaneous recovery:** After extinction, CR reappears after a rest period (weaker than before).
- **Generalization:** Similar stimuli to CS also produce CR (Little Albert feared white rat, then also feared white rabbit).
- **Discrimination:** Ability to distinguish between similar stimuli (responding to CS but not to similar stimuli).
- **Higher-order conditioning:** A new neutral stimulus paired with an established CS (rather than the original UCS).

**Biological preparedness and the Garcia effect:**
- Some associations learned more easily due to evolutionary preparedness.
- **Taste aversion (Garcia effect):** If you eat something and get sick hours later, you develop a strong aversion to that food — even though the CS-UCS gap is very long. Violates the usual requirement for CS-UCS close timing.
- **One-trial learning:** Taste aversion can form after a single pairing. Evolutionary advantage: avoiding poisonous food.

### Operant Conditioning (Skinner)

Learning through **consequences**. Behavior is shaped by what happens after it.

**The 2x2 matrix** (know this cold):

| | Adding stimulus (+) | Removing stimulus (-) |
|---|---|---|
| **Increase behavior (reinforcement)** | **Positive reinforcement** (give reward) | **Negative reinforcement** (remove something unpleasant) |
| **Decrease behavior (punishment)** | **Positive punishment** (add something unpleasant) | **Negative punishment** (take away something desired) |

Examples:
- Positive reinforcement: Dog sits → gets a treat.
- Negative reinforcement: Put on seatbelt → annoying buzzer stops. Take aspirin → headache goes away.
- Positive punishment: Child hits sibling → gets spanked.
- Negative punishment: Teenager breaks curfew → loses phone privileges.

**MCAT trap:** "Positive" and "negative" do NOT mean good and bad. They mean adding (+) and removing (-) a stimulus. "Reinforcement" always increases behavior; "punishment" always decreases it.

**Schedules of reinforcement:**

| Schedule | Pattern | Response Rate | Resistance to Extinction |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Fixed-ratio (FR)** | Reward after a set number of responses | High, with brief **post-reinforcement pause** | Moderate |
| **Variable-ratio (VR)** | Reward after an unpredictable number of responses | **Highest**, no pauses | **Highest** (slot machines, social media) |
| **Fixed-interval (FI)** | Reward after a set time period | **Scallop pattern** (ramps up near end) | Low |
| **Variable-interval (VI)** | Reward after unpredictable time periods | Slow and steady | High |

**Mnemonic:** Variable-ratio is the "gambling schedule" — hardest to extinguish.

**Other operant concepts:**
- **Shaping:** Reinforcing successive approximations to a target behavior. Used to train novel behaviors.
- **Escape learning:** Behavior that terminates an ongoing aversive stimulus (leaving a loud room).
- **Avoidance learning:** Behavior that prevents an aversive stimulus (taking an alternate route to avoid traffic). Harder to extinguish — you never experience the aversive stimulus to learn it's gone.
- **Instinctive drift:** Tendency for learned behavior to revert to innate (instinctive) behavior patterns. An animal trained to deposit coins may start "washing" them instead — resembling natural foraging.

### Observational Learning (Bandura)

Learning by watching others — no direct reinforcement required.

**Bobo doll experiment:** Children watched an adult model behave aggressively toward a Bobo doll (hitting, kicking, verbal aggression). Children who observed the aggressive model were significantly more likely to imitate the aggressive behavior, especially when the model was **rewarded** or faced no consequences.

**Vicarious reinforcement/punishment:** You're more likely to imitate behavior you've seen rewarded in others, less likely to imitate behavior you've seen punished.

**Mirror neurons:** Neurons that fire both when you perform an action AND when you observe someone else performing that action. Thought to be the neural basis for observational learning and empathy. (Their exact role is still debated.)

### Memory — Encoding

- **Automatic processing:** Encoding without effort (where you ate lunch, sequence of events).
- **Effortful processing:** Requires conscious attention (studying for the MCAT).
- **Levels of processing (Craik & Lockhart):** Deeper processing = better retention.
  - **Shallow** = structural (how does it look?).
  - **Intermediate** = phonemic (how does it sound?).
  - **Deep** = semantic (what does it mean?).
- **Elaborative rehearsal:** Connecting new information to existing knowledge. Far better than maintenance rehearsal (rote repetition).
- **Self-referencing effect:** Relating information to yourself enhances encoding.
- **Spacing effect:** Distributed practice > massed practice (cramming). This is literally why Anki works.

### Memory — Storage (three-stage model)

1. **Sensory memory:** Brief, large-capacity buffer.
   - **Iconic** (visual): ~0.5 seconds.
   - **Echoic** (auditory): ~3-4 seconds (longer, why you can "replay" what someone just said).

2. **Short-term memory (STM) / Working memory:** Capacity **7 ± 2 items** (Miller's magic number). Duration ~20-30 seconds without rehearsal. **Chunking** increases effective capacity.
   - **Working memory (Baddeley's model):** Active manipulation of information. Components:
     - **Phonological loop** (verbal information).
     - **Visuospatial sketchpad** (visual/spatial information).
     - **Central executive** (directs attention).
     - **Episodic buffer** (integrates information).

3. **Long-term memory (LTM):** Essentially unlimited capacity and duration.
   - **Explicit (declarative)** — conscious recall, requires hippocampus for consolidation:
     - **Episodic:** Personal experiences ("I had pasta last Tuesday").
     - **Semantic:** General facts ("Rome is the capital of Italy").
   - **Implicit (non-declarative)** — unconscious, does NOT require hippocampus:
     - **Procedural:** Motor skills, habits (riding a bike). Cerebellum + basal ganglia.
     - **Priming:** Prior exposure influences later responses.
     - **Classical conditioning:** Conditioned emotional responses.

### Memory — Retrieval

- **Recall:** Generating information from memory (fill-in-the-blank). Hardest.
- **Recognition:** Identifying previously learned information (multiple choice). Easier.
- **Relearning:** Learning material again faster than the first time (savings method).
- **Retrieval cues:** Stimuli that help access stored memories.
- **Context-dependent memory:** Recall is better when the retrieval environment matches the encoding environment.
- **State-dependent memory:** Recall is better when your internal state (mood, intoxication) matches encoding state.
- **Mood-congruent memory:** You preferentially recall memories that match your current mood.
- **Primacy effect:** Better recall of items at the start of a list (LTM).
- **Recency effect:** Better recall of items at the end of a list (STM).

### Forgetting

- **Encoding failure:** Information never entered LTM in the first place.
- **Decay:** Memory trace fades over time (mainly affects sensory and STM).
- **Forgetting curve (Ebbinghaus):** Most forgetting happens rapidly after learning, then levels off. Spaced review counteracts this.
- **Proactive interference:** OLD memories block NEW learning. "Pro = forward" — old interferes going forward. Example: You moved and keep driving to your old house.
- **Retroactive interference:** NEW memories block OLD recall. "Retro = backward" — new interferes going backward. Example: Learning your new phone number makes it hard to remember the old one.
- **Source monitoring error:** Remembering information but misattributing its source.
- **Misinformation effect (Loftus):** Post-event information alters memory of the original event. Loftus's car crash study: "How fast were the cars going when they *smashed* into each other?" → higher speed estimates than "hit."
- **Repressed/false memories:** Highly contested in psychology. False memories can be implanted via suggestion.

**MCAT tip for interference:** "ProOld, RetroNew." Proactive = old interferes with new. Retroactive = new interferes with old.

### Memory Disorders

- **Alzheimer's disease:** Progressive loss of memory and cognition. Associated with **acetylcholine** deficiency, **amyloid plaques**, and **neurofibrillary tangles** (tau protein). Hippocampus affected early. Treatment: **cholinesterase inhibitors** (e.g., donepezil) raise ACh levels. (See Chapter 7 for biological detail.)
- **Korsakoff's syndrome:** Caused by **thiamine (B1) deficiency**, usually from chronic alcoholism. Severe anterograde amnesia, **confabulation** (making up memories to fill gaps).

### Neurobiology of Learning and Memory

- **Long-term potentiation (LTP):** Repeated stimulation of a synapse strengthens the connection. The cellular mechanism of learning and memory. Involves **NMDA receptors** (a glutamate receptor) and AMPA receptors.
- **Synaptic plasticity:** Synapses change in strength with use; underlies all learning.
- **Hippocampus:** Critical for forming new explicit memories and consolidation. The famous **HM case** (Henry Molaison) had bilateral medial temporal lobe removal (including hippocampi) — could not form new explicit memories, but procedural learning was intact.
- **Amygdala:** Emotional memory, especially fear.
- **Cerebellum:** Procedural memory, classical conditioning.
- **Neural plasticity:** The brain's ability to reorganize itself through new connections, throughout life. Greater in childhood; decreases (but does not disappear) with age.

## CHAPTER 4: Cognition, Consciousness, and Language

**Maps to AAMC FC:** 6B (cognition, consciousness, language) — heaviest single chapter in PS.
**MCAT weight:** ~8-10 questions per exam. Sleep, biases, and language are guaranteed; cognitive development and intelligence frequent.
**Overview:** Thinking, problem-solving, intelligence, attention, levels of consciousness (sleep, drugs, hypnosis), and language.

### Cognition — Information Processing

The mind operates somewhat like a computer:
- **Input** (perception) → **encoding** → **storage** → **retrieval** → **output** (behavior).
- Limits on each stage shape what we can think about.

**Types of thought:**
- **Concept formation:** Categorizing objects/events.
- **Prototypes:** The "best" example of a concept (a robin is a more prototypical bird than a penguin).
- **Exemplars:** Specific examples stored in memory and used for comparison.
- **Mental representations:** Mental images, schemas, and scripts.

### Cognitive Development (Piaget)

Piaget proposed four stages of cognitive development. Children must pass through them **in order**, but the ages are approximate.

| Stage | Age | Key Acquisition | What They Can't Do Yet |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sensorimotor** | 0-2 | **Object permanence** (~8 mo) — objects exist even when you can't see them | Symbolic thought |
| **Preoperational** | 2-7 | Symbolic thought, language, pretend play | **Conservation**, logical reasoning. **Egocentrism** (can't take another's perspective), **centration** (focus on one aspect) |
| **Concrete operational** | 7-11 | **Conservation** (quantity stays same despite shape change), logical thought about concrete objects | Abstract/hypothetical reasoning |
| **Formal operational** | 12+ | **Abstract reasoning**, hypothetical thinking, systematic problem-solving | — |

**Vygotsky** (contrast with Piaget): Emphasized **social/cultural** role in development. Key concepts:
- **Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD):** The gap between what a child can do alone and what they can do with guidance.
- **Scaffolding:** Temporary support from a more skilled person.
- **More knowledgeable other (MKO):** Anyone (parent, teacher, peer) with greater ability who supports learning.

**MCAT distinction:** Piaget = child as independent explorer discovering the world. Vygotsky = child learns through social interaction and cultural tools (especially language).

### Problem-Solving and Decision Making

**Problem-solving strategies:**
- **Algorithms:** Step-by-step procedures that guarantee a solution. Slow but reliable.
- **Heuristics:** Mental shortcuts. Fast but error-prone.

**Key heuristics and biases** (very high yield — show up nearly every exam):
- **Availability heuristic:** Judging the likelihood by how easily examples come to mind. After a plane crash story, you overestimate flying risk.
- **Representativeness heuristic:** Judging probability by how much something resembles the prototype. "Linda is 31, outspoken, philosophy major" → people say she's more likely a "feminist bank teller" than a "bank teller" (conjunction fallacy).
- **Anchoring bias:** Over-relying on the first piece of information. If a car is listed at $30,000, then offered at $25,000, it feels like a deal — even if $25,000 is overpriced.
- **Framing effect:** How information is presented changes decisions. "90% survival rate" sounds better than "10% mortality rate" even though identical.
- **Confirmation bias:** Seeking information that confirms existing beliefs, ignoring contradictory evidence.
- **Belief perseverance:** Maintaining beliefs even after they've been disproven.
- **Overconfidence bias:** Overestimating the accuracy of your own judgments.
- **Hindsight bias:** "I knew it all along" after learning the outcome.
- **Functional fixedness:** Inability to see an object as useful for anything other than its typical function.
- **Mental set:** Tendency to approach problems the same way that worked before, even when a better method exists.

**Dual-process theory (Kahneman):**
- **System 1:** Fast, automatic, intuitive, emotional. Where heuristics live.
- **System 2:** Slow, effortful, deliberate, logical. Where algorithms and careful reasoning live.
- Most everyday thinking is System 1. Errors often arise when we rely on System 1 in situations that require System 2.

**Intuition:** Pattern-matching against past experience without conscious reasoning. Often surprisingly accurate (in domains with extensive experience), often very wrong (in novel situations).

### Intelligence

- **Spearman's g:** General intelligence — one underlying factor that predicts performance across domains.
- **Fluid vs crystallized intelligence (Cattell):**
  - **Fluid:** Reasoning, novel problem-solving. Peaks in early adulthood, declines with age.
  - **Crystallized:** Accumulated knowledge, vocabulary. Stable or increases throughout adulthood.
- **Gardner's multiple intelligences:** 8+ types (linguistic, logical-mathematical, spatial, musical, bodily-kinesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalistic). Not all universally accepted.
- **Sternberg's triarchic theory:** Three components — **analytical** (academic problem-solving), **creative** (novel situations), **practical** (street smarts, adapting to environment).
- **Emotional intelligence:** Ability to perceive, understand, manage, and use emotions. Salovey & Mayer; popularized by Goleman.
- **IQ tests:** Standardized to mean = 100, SD = 15. Originally developed by Binet for identifying children needing extra help.
- **Flynn effect:** Average IQ scores have been rising about 3 points per decade for the past century. Reasons disputed (better nutrition, education, test familiarity).

### Consciousness — States

States of consciousness range from full alertness to deep sleep to coma. Other altered states: dreaming, hypnosis, meditation, drug-induced.

### Sleep Stages (use EEG to measure)

| Stage | EEG Pattern | Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Awake/alert** | Beta waves (high frequency, low amplitude) | Active thinking |
| **Awake/relaxed** | Alpha waves | Eyes closed, calm |
| **N1 (NREM 1)** | Theta waves | Light sleep, **hypnagogic hallucinations** (vivid sensory experiences while falling asleep), easily awakened |
| **N2 (NREM 2)** | Theta waves + **sleep spindles** + **K-complexes** | Deeper sleep, body temp drops, heart rate slows. Most time spent here (~50%) |
| **N3 (NREM 3)** | **Delta waves** (low frequency, high amplitude) | Deepest sleep, **slow-wave sleep**. Hardest to wake. **Growth hormone** released. **Parasomnias** (sleepwalking, night terrors) occur here |
| **REM** | Beta waves (like being awake!) | Vivid dreaming, **muscle atonia** (paralysis), rapid eye movements, memory consolidation. **Paradoxical sleep** because EEG looks awake but person is deeply asleep |

**Key patterns:**
- Sleep cycles ~90 minutes each.
- **Early in the night:** More N3 (deep sleep).
- **Later in the night:** More REM (dreaming).
- Newborns spend ~50% in REM; adults ~20-25%.

### Circadian Rhythms

~24-hour biological clock. Controlled by the **suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN)** in the hypothalamus, which receives light input from the retina. The SCN regulates the **pineal gland**, which releases **melatonin** (promotes sleepiness, peaks at night). Disrupted by jet lag, shift work, blue light exposure.

### Sleep Disorders

- **Insomnia:** Difficulty falling or staying asleep.
- **Narcolepsy:** Sudden, uncontrollable episodes of REM sleep during the day. Linked to **orexin/hypocretin deficiency**.
- **Sleep apnea:** Repeated breathing interruptions. **Obstructive** (airway collapses) vs **central** (brain fails to signal breathing muscles).
- **Parasomnias:** Abnormal behaviors during sleep. **Sleepwalking** (somnambulism) and **night terrors** occur during **N3**. Not during REM — no muscle movement possible during REM due to atonia.

### Dreaming Theories

- **Freud:** Wish fulfillment — dreams express unconscious desires. **Manifest content** (literal storyline) vs **latent content** (hidden symbolic meaning).
- **Activation-synthesis (Hobson & McCarley):** Brain stem sends random signals during REM; cortex tries to make sense of them.
- **Information processing:** Dreams help consolidate memories and process the day's events.

### Hypnosis and Meditation

- **Social influence theory (Spanos):** Hypnotized people are playing a social role, not in an altered state.
- **Dissociation theory (Hilgard):** Hypnosis splits consciousness — a "hidden observer" remains aware while the hypnotized part responds to suggestions.
- **Meditation:** Practice that focuses attention and quiets cognition. Associated with reduced cortisol, lower BP, increased gray matter in attention/emotion regulation areas.

### Consciousness-Altering Drugs

| Class | Examples | Mechanism | Effects |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Stimulants** | Cocaine, amphetamines, caffeine, nicotine | Increase dopamine, NE (cocaine blocks reuptake; amphetamines increase release) | Increased alertness, euphoria, heart rate, BP |
| **Depressants** | Alcohol, barbiturates, benzodiazepines | Enhance **GABA** (inhibitory) | Decreased CNS activity, sedation, disinhibition |
| **Opioids** | Heroin, morphine, oxycodone, fentanyl | Mimic **endorphins**, bind opioid receptors | Pain relief, euphoria, respiratory depression |
| **Hallucinogens** | LSD, psilocybin, mescaline | Primarily affect **serotonin** (5-HT2A receptors) | Altered perception, hallucinations |
| **Cannabis** | THC | Binds **cannabinoid receptors** (CB1 in brain) | Mild euphoria, altered time perception, pain relief |

**Reward pathway (mesolimbic dopamine pathway):** **VTA (ventral tegmental area)** → **nucleus accumbens**. All drugs of abuse (and natural rewards like food and sex) increase dopamine in this pathway. The neural basis of reinforcement and addiction.

**Key terms:**
- **Tolerance:** Needing more drug for the same effect (neuroadaptation).
- **Physical dependence:** Body adapts; **withdrawal** symptoms appear when drug is removed (opposite of drug's effects).
- **Psychological dependence:** Craving, compulsive use despite negative consequences.

### Attention

- **Selective attention:** Focusing on one stimulus while ignoring others. The **cocktail party effect** — you can pick out your name from background noise at a party.
- **Divided attention:** Trying to attend to multiple things simultaneously. Worse with complexity, better with practice (**automaticity**).
- **Inattentional blindness:** Failing to see something in plain sight because attention is elsewhere (gorilla basketball experiment, Simons & Chabris).
- **Change blindness:** Failing to notice changes in a visual scene (person behind a counter swaps mid-conversation and you don't notice).

### Language — Components

- **Phonology:** Sound system (phonemes — smallest units of sound).
- **Morphology:** Smallest units of meaning (morphemes — words and meaningful word parts like prefixes/suffixes).
- **Semantics:** Meaning of words and sentences.
- **Syntax:** Rules for combining words into sentences.
- **Pragmatics:** Use of language in social context (tone, context, sarcasm).

### Brain Areas for Language

- **Broca's area** (left frontal lobe): Speech **production**. Damage causes **Broca's aphasia** (expressive/non-fluent aphasia) — patient understands language but speaks in slow, halting, telegraphic speech. "Broca = Broken speech."
- **Wernicke's area** (left temporal lobe): Speech **comprehension**. Damage causes **Wernicke's aphasia** (receptive/fluent aphasia) — patient speaks fluently but makes no sense (word salad). They don't understand speech either. "Wernicke = Wordy but Wrong."
- **Arcuate fasciculus:** Connects Broca's and Wernicke's. Damage causes **conduction aphasia** — can understand and produce speech, but can't repeat what's said.

### Language Acquisition Stages

| Age (approx) | Stage | Features |
|---|---|---|
| Birth-6 mo | Cooing | Vowel sounds, all phonemes possible |
| 6-12 mo | Babbling | Consonant-vowel combinations; phonemes narrow to native language |
| 12-18 mo | One-word stage | Single words ("milk") |
| 18-24 mo | Telegraphic speech | Two-word phrases ("want milk") |
| 24+ mo | Full sentences | Grammatical complexity grows rapidly |

### Language Acquisition Theories

- **Nativist (Chomsky):** Language ability is innate. **Language Acquisition Device (LAD)** — a built-in biological capacity for language. Supported by **universal grammar** (all languages share deep structure) and rapid acquisition without formal instruction.
- **Behaviorist (Skinner):** Language is learned through operant conditioning — reinforcement, imitation, shaping.
- **Social interactionist:** Both biology and environment contribute; social interaction is critical.

### Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis (Linguistic Relativity)

Language shapes the way you think.
- **Strong version:** Language determines thought (largely rejected).
- **Weak version:** Language influences thought and perception (better supported).
- Example: cultures with more words for snow may perceive snow differently.

### Bilingualism

- Children learning two languages from birth become equally fluent in both.
- Bilingualism associated with cognitive advantages (executive function, delayed dementia onset) but possibly slightly smaller vocabulary in each individual language.

## CHAPTER 5: Motivation, Emotion, and Stress

**Maps to AAMC FC:** 7A (motivation) + 6C (emotion, stress).
**MCAT weight:** ~5-7 questions per exam. Theories of emotion are nearly guaranteed; stress + HPA axis very high yield.
**Overview:** Why we do what we do (motivation), what we feel (emotion), and how we react to threats (stress).

### Motivation — Theories

**Instinct theory:** Behavior is driven by innate, fixed action patterns. Largely outdated for human behavior; better suited to animal behavior.

**Drive reduction theory:** Biological needs create internal tension (drives) that motivate behavior to reduce the tension and restore **homeostasis**. Thirsty → drink water → tension reduced.
- **Primary drives:** Biological (hunger, thirst, sleep).
- **Secondary drives:** Learned (money, achievement).
- Limitation: doesn't explain thrill-seeking behavior or behavior in absence of need.

**Arousal theory:** People are motivated to maintain an **optimal level of arousal**. Too low = boredom-seeking behavior. Too high = avoidance behavior.
- **Yerkes-Dodson law:** Performance is best at **moderate arousal** (inverted-U curve). Optimal level depends on task difficulty: simple tasks → higher arousal is better; complex tasks → lower arousal is better. **MCAT loves this relationship.**

**Incentive theory:** External stimuli (**incentives**) pull behavior. Money, food, grades. Contrasts with drive reduction (internal push).

**Expectancy-value theory:** Motivation = expectancy of success × value of the outcome.

**Opponent-process theory (motivation):** Every emotional/motivational state triggers an opposing process. Initial use of a drug = pleasure; opposing process = discomfort. With repeated use, the opposing process strengthens → tolerance and withdrawal.

**Cognitive theories:** Motivation arises from how you think about goals, attributions, and self-efficacy.

**Need theories:**

**Maslow's hierarchy of needs** (bottom to top):
1. **Physiological:** Food, water, sleep, homeostasis.
2. **Safety:** Security, stability, health.
3. **Love/belonging:** Friendship, family, intimacy.
4. **Esteem:** Respect, recognition, self-esteem.
5. **Self-actualization:** Achieving full potential, creativity.

Lower needs must be at least partially met before higher needs become motivating. Criticism: overly rigid, doesn't account for cultural variation.

**Self-determination theory (Deci & Ryan):** Three innate psychological needs:
1. **Autonomy:** Feeling in control of your own behavior.
2. **Competence:** Feeling effective and capable.
3. **Relatedness:** Feeling connected to others.

When met → **intrinsic motivation** flourishes. When undermined → motivation shifts to extrinsic or disappears.

**Intrinsic vs extrinsic motivation:**
- **Intrinsic:** Doing something for its own sake (curiosity, enjoyment).
- **Extrinsic:** Doing something for an external reward.
- **Overjustification effect:** Giving external rewards for something someone already enjoys can *decrease* intrinsic motivation. Classic example: kids who loved drawing got rewarded for drawing → drew less when rewards stopped.

### Hunger Regulation

- **Lateral hypothalamus (LH):** "Hunger center." Stimulation → eating. Destruction → no eating (aphagia). Mnemonic: "Lateral = Lunch."
- **Ventromedial hypothalamus (VMH):** "Satiety center." Stimulation → stop eating. Destruction → overeating (hyperphagia). Mnemonic: "Ventromedial = Very full."
- **Ghrelin:** Hormone from **stomach**. Rises before meals. Signals hunger. "Ghrelin = Growling stomach."
- **Leptin:** Hormone from **adipose (fat) tissue**. Signals satiety. More fat → more leptin → less hunger (in theory; obesity involves leptin resistance).
- **Insulin:** From pancreas. Promotes glucose uptake, involved in hunger signaling.

### Sexual Motivation and Social Motivation

- **Sexual motivation:** Driven by hormones (testosterone in both sexes), social/cultural context, and learned associations.
- **Social motivation:** Need for affiliation, achievement, power. McClelland identified these as three core social motives.

### Emotion — Three Components

Every emotion has three intertwined parts:
1. **Physiological** (heart rate, sweating).
2. **Behavioral** (facial expression, body language).
3. **Cognitive** (subjective experience, interpretation).

### Theories of Emotion (nearly guaranteed on every exam)

This is one of the highest-yield topics in all of PS. Know all three theories cold and apply them to novel scenarios.

**James-Lange theory:** Stimulus → **physiological arousal first** → then conscious emotion. "I am trembling, therefore I must be afraid." The body reacts; you interpret the reaction as an emotion. Prediction: different emotions should have distinct physiological signatures.

**Cannon-Bard theory:** Stimulus → physiological arousal AND emotional experience occur **simultaneously and independently**. Your heart races and you feel fear at the same time. Critique of James-Lange: many emotions produce similar physiological responses.

**Schachter-Singer two-factor theory:** Stimulus → physiological arousal + **cognitive label** → emotion. You feel aroused, then look to environment to figure out *why*. Same arousal can become different emotions depending on context. Classic experiment: epinephrine injection → angry confederate room = anger; happy confederate room = happiness.

**Lazarus cognitive appraisal theory:** Cognitive appraisal of the stimulus comes first, then both arousal and emotion arise from that appraisal.

**Quick decision framework:**
- Body first, then emotion? → James-Lange
- Body and emotion at the same time? → Cannon-Bard
- Body + cognitive interpretation? → Schachter-Singer
- Cognition before everything? → Lazarus

### Universal Emotions (Ekman)

Six emotions recognized across cultures through facial expressions:
- **Happiness, sadness, fear, anger, surprise, disgust.**
(Some now include contempt as a seventh.)

### Brain and Emotion

- **Amygdala:** Central to fear processing and emotional memory. The brain's threat detector.
- **Prefrontal cortex:** Emotional regulation, impulse control. Damage → poor decision-making, impulsivity (Phineas Gage).
- **Autonomic nervous system:** Sympathetic branch activates during emotional arousal (fight or flight); parasympathetic calms you down (rest and digest).
- **Limbic system:** Generally underlies emotion (amygdala, hippocampus, cingulate, hypothalamus).

### Stress — Appraisal Model (Lazarus)

- **Primary appraisal:** "Is this situation a threat, a challenge, or irrelevant?"
- **Secondary appraisal:** "Do I have the resources to cope with this?"
- If you appraise a situation as threatening AND you lack resources → high stress. Same event can be stressful for one person and exciting for another depending on appraisal.

### Stressor Types

- **Cataclysmic events:** Large-scale disasters affecting many people.
- **Personal stressors:** Major life changes (divorce, job loss, illness).
- **Daily hassles/background stressors:** Ongoing, low-level irritants (traffic, work deadlines). Research shows these may have the most cumulative impact on health.
- **Ambient stressors:** Chronic environmental factors (noise, pollution).

### General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS) — Selye

Three stages of the body's stress response:
1. **Alarm:** Initial "fight or flight." Sympathetic activation, adrenaline (epinephrine) release, cortisol release. Resources mobilized.
2. **Resistance:** Body adapts to the stressor. Cortisol remains elevated. Appears to be coping, but resources depleting.
3. **Exhaustion:** Prolonged stress depletes resources. Immune system weakens, vulnerability to illness increases, burnout, potentially organ damage or death.

### HPA Axis (Stress Hormone Pathway)

**Hypothalamus** → releases **CRH (corticotropin-releasing hormone)** → **anterior pituitary** → releases **ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone)** → **adrenal cortex** → releases **cortisol**.

Cortisol effects: increases blood sugar, suppresses immune system, promotes fat storage. **Negative feedback:** cortisol inhibits CRH and ACTH release. Chronic elevation is terrible for health (impairs hippocampal function, weakens immune response, ↑ CV disease risk).

### Chronic Stress Effects

- **Immune:** Suppressed → ↑ infection, slower wound healing.
- **Cardiovascular:** ↑ BP, ↑ atherosclerosis, ↑ heart disease.
- **Cognitive:** Cortisol damages hippocampus → memory impairment.
- **Mental health:** Predisposes to depression, anxiety, PTSD.

### Coping

- **Problem-focused coping:** Directly addressing the stressor (studying more when struggling in a class). More effective when you have control over the situation.
- **Emotion-focused coping:** Managing the emotional response (meditation, venting, reframing). More effective when you can't control the situation.
- **Social support:** Buffer against stress. Includes emotional, instrumental, informational, appraisal support.
- **Learned helplessness (Seligman):** Repeated exposure to uncontrollable aversive events → person stops trying to escape, even when escape becomes possible. Model of depression.

## CHAPTER 6: Identity and Personality

**Maps to AAMC FC:** 8A (self-identity) + 7A (personality).
**MCAT weight:** ~5-7 questions per exam. Erikson, Freud, Big Five, and self-concept are all heavily tested.
**Overview:** How identity forms across the lifespan, theories of personality, and the social/cognitive influences on the self.

### Self-Concept and Identity

- **Self-concept:** Your overall understanding of who you are (beliefs, attributes, values). Develops through social interaction.
- **Self-schema:** Organized cognitive structure containing beliefs about a particular aspect of yourself ("I'm an introvert").
- **Self-esteem:** Your evaluation of your own self-worth. Can be global or domain-specific.
- **Self-efficacy (Bandura):** Your belief in your ability to succeed at a **specific task**. Different from self-esteem. You can have high self-esteem but low self-efficacy for calculus.
- **Locus of control (Rotter):**
  - **Internal:** "I control what happens to me." Correlates with higher achievement, less depression.
  - **External:** "Outside forces control what happens to me."

### Types of Identity

- **Social identity:** The part of self-concept derived from group membership (nationality, gender, religion, profession).
- **Ethnic identity:** Sense of belonging to an ethnic group; can be salient or background depending on context.
- **National identity:** Identification with one's country.
- **Gender/sex identity:** Internal sense of being male, female, both, neither, or other. Develops alongside cultural exposure to gender roles.

### Formation of Identity — Symbolic Interactionism

**Looking-glass self (Cooley):** Our self-concept is shaped by how we *think* others see us. Three steps:
1. We imagine how we appear to others.
2. We imagine their judgment of our appearance.
3. We develop feelings about ourselves based on that imagined judgment.

This is a subjective process — about *perceived* judgments, which may not match reality.

**George Herbert Mead — I and Me:**
- **"I"**: The spontaneous, unpredictable, creative self. Your impulses and unique responses.
- **"Me"**: The socialized self. How you've internalized the attitudes and expectations of others.
- **Role-taking:** Mead's idea that children develop self-concept by learning to take the perspective of others.
  - **Preparatory stage:** Imitation (no understanding of meaning).
  - **Play stage:** Take on roles of specific others (playing "doctor" or "teacher").
  - **Game stage:** Understanding multiple roles simultaneously, concept of the **"generalized other"** (society's expectations as a whole).

**Reference groups:** Groups you compare yourself to when evaluating your own status, attitudes, and behavior. Can be groups you belong to or aspire to join.

**Total institutions (Goffman):** Settings where individuals are isolated from broader society and life is tightly regulated (prisons, military, monasteries). Often involve **resocialization** — stripping previous identity and constructing a new one.

### Erikson's 8 Psychosocial Stages

| Stage | Age | Crisis | Key Event |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 0-1 | **Trust vs. Mistrust** | Consistent caregiving → trust |
| 2 | 1-3 | **Autonomy vs. Shame/Doubt** | Toilet training, exploration → independence |
| 3 | 3-6 | **Initiative vs. Guilt** | Taking initiative in play → purpose |
| 4 | 6-12 | **Industry vs. Inferiority** | School, competence in tasks → confidence |
| 5 | 12-18 | **Identity vs. Role Confusion** | Exploring identity (who am I?) → fidelity |
| 6 | Young adult | **Intimacy vs. Isolation** | Forming committed relationships → love |
| 7 | Middle adult | **Generativity vs. Stagnation** | Contributing to next generation → care |
| 8 | Late adult | **Integrity vs. Despair** | Reflecting on life → wisdom |

**MCAT tip:** Stage 5 (identity vs. role confusion) is the most tested because it connects to adolescent identity formation, which overlaps with Marcia's identity statuses (identity achievement, moratorium, foreclosure, diffusion).

### Kohlberg's Moral Development

| Level | Stage | Basis for Moral Judgment |
|---|---|---|
| **Preconventional** | 1. Obedience & punishment | "I'll get punished" |
| | 2. Self-interest/reward | "What's in it for me?" |
| **Conventional** | 3. Good boy/girl | "I want to be liked" |
| | 4. Law and order | "It's the law" |
| **Postconventional** | 5. Social contract | "Laws are agreements that can be changed for the greater good" |
| | 6. Universal ethics | "I follow my conscience, even if it breaks the law" |

**Heinz dilemma:** Kohlberg's classic test scenario — should a man steal expensive medicine to save his dying wife? It's the *reasoning*, not the answer, that classifies moral level.

**MCAT application:** You'll get a scenario and need to identify the Kohlberg level. Key distinction: Preconventional = consequences to self. Conventional = social rules. Postconventional = abstract principles.

**Criticism of Kohlberg:** Gilligan argued the model is biased toward male justice-oriented morality and doesn't capture female care-oriented morality.

### Freud's Psychosexual Stages

| Stage | Age | Focus | Fixation If Unresolved |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Oral** | 0-1 | Mouth (feeding) | Oral fixation (smoking, overeating, nail-biting) |
| **Anal** | 1-3 | Bowel control | Anal-retentive (obsessive orderliness) or anal-expulsive (messy, reckless) |
| **Phallic** | 3-6 | Genitals, **Oedipus/Electra complex** | Issues with sexuality, authority |
| **Latency** | 6-12 | Sexual impulses dormant; social/intellectual growth | — |
| **Genital** | 12+ | Mature sexual interests | — |

### Vygotsky and Sociocultural Identity

- **Vygotsky:** Identity, like cognition, is shaped by social interaction within a cultural context.
- **ZPD, scaffolding, more knowledgeable other** (see Ch 4) all apply to identity development as well.

### Personality — Psychoanalytic (Freud)

- **Id:** Operates on the **pleasure principle**. Unconscious, primitive drives. Wants immediate gratification. Present from birth.
- **Ego:** Operates on the **reality principle**. Mediates between id and superego. "The executive." Develops in early childhood.
- **Superego:** The moral conscience. Internalized societal and parental standards. The "angel on your shoulder."
- Personality = the balance and conflict among these three.

**Defense mechanisms** (ego's tools for managing anxiety from id-superego conflict):

| Mechanism | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Repression** | Pushing threatening thoughts into the unconscious | Forgetting a traumatic event |
| **Denial** | Refusing to accept reality | "I don't have a drinking problem" |
| **Projection** | Attributing your own unacceptable feelings to others | An unfaithful spouse accusing their partner of cheating |
| **Displacement** | Redirecting emotions to a safer target | Yelling at your dog after a bad day at work |
| **Sublimation** | Channeling unacceptable impulses into socially acceptable activities (most mature) | Aggressive person takes up boxing |
| **Rationalization** | Creating logical excuses for unacceptable behavior | "I didn't want that job anyway" |
| **Reaction formation** | Acting opposite to your true feelings | Being overly nice to someone you dislike |
| **Regression** | Reverting to childlike behaviors under stress | Adult throwing a tantrum |

**MCAT loves defense mechanisms.** You'll get a scenario and have to identify which one is operating. **Sublimation is considered the most "mature."**

### Personality — Neo-Freudians

- **Carl Jung:** **Collective unconscious** — shared inherited memories and archetypes (the hero, the mother, the shadow).
- **Alfred Adler:** **Inferiority complex** — striving to overcome perceived inadequacies drives behavior.
- **Karen Horney:** **Basic anxiety** — feeling helpless in a hostile world. Critiqued Freud's male-centered theory.

### Personality — Humanistic

- **Carl Rogers:** People have an innate drive toward **self-actualization**. Healthy development requires **unconditional positive regard** (acceptance without conditions). **Conditions of worth** = strings attached to acceptance → **incongruence** between real self and ideal self → psychological distress.
- **Maslow:** **Hierarchy of needs** culminating in self-actualization (see Ch 5).

### Personality — Trait Theories

- **Allport:** Identified cardinal traits (dominate personality), central traits (general characteristics), and secondary traits (situational).
- **Eysenck — PEN model:** Three superfactors: **Psychoticism, Extraversion, Neuroticism.**
- **Big Five (OCEAN):** Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism. Stable, measurable dimensions of personality. Based on **factor analysis**. Most empirically supported trait model.

### Personality — Social Cognitive (Bandura)

- **Reciprocal determinism:** Behavior, personal factors (cognition, personality), and environment all influence each other bidirectionally. A triangle, not a one-way street.
- **Self-efficacy:** Belief in your ability to succeed in a specific situation. High self-efficacy → more effort, more persistence.

### Personality — Biological

- **Temperament:** Innate behavioral style (activity level, emotionality, sociability). Studied in infants. Strong genetic component.
- **Twin studies:** Monozygotic (identical) vs dizygotic (fraternal) twin concordance rates help estimate heritability of personality traits.

### Personality — Behaviorist

- **Skinner:** Personality is just patterns of behavior shaped by reinforcement history. No inner self required.

## CHAPTER 7: Psychological Disorders

**Maps to AAMC FC:** 7A (psychological disorders).
**MCAT weight:** ~6-8 questions per exam. Schizophrenia, depression, anxiety/OCD, and biological basis are all guaranteed.
**Overview:** DSM-5 classification, major disorder categories, biological basis of disorders, and treatments.

### Understanding Psychological Disorders

**Classification:** **DSM-5** (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual, 5th edition). Uses descriptive criteria (symptoms, duration, impairment). The MCAT doesn't expect you to diagnose, but you need to recognize key features.

**Models:**
- **Biomedical model:** Disorders are medical diseases with biological causes.
- **Biopsychosocial model:** Disorders result from the interaction of biological, psychological, and social factors. **This is the MCAT-preferred model.**

**Rates of disorders:** Over a lifetime, ~50% of US adults will meet criteria for a mental disorder. Anxiety and depression are most common.

**Deinstitutionalization:** Mid-20th century shift away from large psychiatric hospitals toward community-based care. Driven by new medications (antipsychotics) and concerns about institutional abuses. Side effects: increased homelessness, incarceration of mentally ill.

### Anxiety Disorders

- **Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD):** Excessive, uncontrollable worry about multiple things for **6+ months**.
- **Specific phobia:** Irrational fear of a specific object or situation.
- **Social anxiety disorder:** Fear of social situations and being judged.
- **Panic disorder:** Recurrent unexpected **panic attacks** (intense fear with physical symptoms — racing heart, shortness of breath, chest pain). Can develop **agoraphobia** (fear of situations where escape might be difficult).
- **Separation anxiety disorder:** Excessive distress when separated from attachment figures.

### Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

- **Obsessions:** Intrusive, unwanted, persistent thoughts (e.g., contamination fears).
- **Compulsions:** Repetitive behaviors performed to reduce anxiety (e.g., hand washing).
- Note: OCD is **no longer classified as an anxiety disorder** in DSM-5 — it has its own category.

### Trauma- and Stressor-Related Disorders

**PTSD (post-traumatic stress disorder):** Follows exposure to a traumatic event. Four symptom clusters:
1. **Intrusion:** Flashbacks, nightmares, intrusive memories.
2. **Avoidance:** Avoiding reminders of the trauma.
3. **Negative changes in cognition/mood:** Detachment, distorted blame, persistent negative emotional state.
4. **Arousal/reactivity:** Hyperarousal, exaggerated startle, hypervigilance, sleep problems.

### Somatic Symptom and Related Disorders

- **Somatic symptom disorder:** Distressing physical symptoms that disrupt life, with excessive thoughts/feelings about them.
- **Illness anxiety disorder:** Preoccupation with having or acquiring a serious illness, despite few or no symptoms.

### Depressive Disorders

- **Major Depressive Disorder (MDD):** **5+ symptoms for 2+ weeks.** Symptoms: depressed mood, **anhedonia** (loss of pleasure), weight/appetite changes, sleep changes, fatigue, worthlessness/guilt, concentration difficulties, **suicidal ideation**.
- **Persistent depressive disorder (dysthymia):** Depressed mood most days for **2+ years**, less severe than MDD.
- **Premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD):** Severe mood symptoms tied to menstrual cycle.
- **Biological basis:** **Serotonin** and **norepinephrine** deficiency hypothesis. SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors) are first-line treatment.

### Bipolar and Related Disorders

- **Bipolar I:** Full **manic episodes** (elevated mood, grandiosity, decreased need for sleep, rapid speech, risky behavior, possible psychotic features) + depressive episodes.
- **Bipolar II:** **Hypomanic** episodes (less severe than mania, no psychotic features) + depressive episodes.

### Schizophrenia

- **Positive symptoms** (added to normal functioning):
  - **Hallucinations** — usually **auditory** (hearing voices).
  - **Delusions** — paranoid (being persecuted), grandiose (special powers), referential (TV is talking to me).
  - **Disorganized speech** (word salad, loose associations).
  - **Disorganized behavior.**
- **Negative symptoms** (taken away from normal functioning):
  - **Flat affect, avolition** (lack of motivation), **alogia** (reduced speech), **anhedonia, social withdrawal**.
- **Cognitive symptoms:** Working memory deficits, impaired executive function, attention problems.
- **Dopamine hypothesis:** Excess dopamine (especially D2 receptors in **mesolimbic pathway**) → positive symptoms. Supported by: antipsychotics block D2; amphetamines (↑ dopamine) can cause psychosis-like symptoms. Negative symptoms may involve **hypodopaminergic** activity in **mesocortical pathway** (prefrontal cortex).
- **Glutamate hypothesis:** **NMDA receptor hypofunction**. PCP (NMDA antagonist) produces schizophrenia-like symptoms.

### Dissociative Disorders

- **Dissociative identity disorder (DID):** Two or more distinct personality states.
- **Dissociative amnesia:** Inability to recall important personal information, usually after trauma.
- **Depersonalization/derealization disorder:** Persistent feeling of being detached from one's own body or from reality.

### Personality Disorders (know the clusters)

- **Cluster A (odd/eccentric):** **Paranoid, Schizoid** (detached, flat), **Schizotypal** (eccentric beliefs, magical thinking).
- **Cluster B (dramatic/emotional):** **Antisocial** (disregard for others' rights, lack of remorse), **Borderline** (unstable relationships, self-image, impulsivity), **Histrionic** (attention-seeking, excessive emotion), **Narcissistic** (grandiosity, need for admiration).
- **Cluster C (anxious/fearful):** **Avoidant** (social inhibition, inadequacy), **Dependent** (need to be taken care of), **OCPD** (rigidity, perfectionism — distinct from OCD).

### Eating Disorders

- **Anorexia nervosa:** Restriction of food intake, intense fear of weight gain, distorted body image. Significantly low body weight.
- **Bulimia nervosa:** Recurrent episodes of binge eating followed by compensatory behaviors (vomiting, laxatives, excessive exercise). Usually normal-to-overweight.

### Neurodevelopmental Disorders

- **ADHD:** Inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity. Onset before age 12. Treated with stimulants (paradoxically calming).
- **Autism spectrum disorder (ASD):** Deficits in social communication and restricted/repetitive behaviors. Wide range of severity.

### Biological Basis of Nervous System Disorders

- **Alzheimer's disease:**
  - **Amyloid plaques** (beta-amyloid protein deposits).
  - **Neurofibrillary tangles** (hyperphosphorylated tau protein).
  - **Acetylcholine deficit** — neurons in basal forebrain die first.
  - Hippocampus affected early → memory loss.
  - Treatment: **cholinesterase inhibitors** (donepezil, rivastigmine) ↑ ACh; NMDA receptor antagonist (memantine) for moderate-severe.
- **Parkinson's disease:**
  - Loss of dopaminergic neurons in **substantia nigra** → ↓ dopamine in basal ganglia.
  - Symptoms: resting **tremor**, **rigidity**, **bradykinesia** (slow movement), postural instability.
  - Treatment: **L-DOPA** (dopamine precursor; crosses blood-brain barrier).
- **Huntington's disease:**
  - **Autosomal dominant** (CAG repeat expansion in HTT gene).
  - Degeneration of **caudate and putamen** → **chorea** (jerky involuntary movements), cognitive decline, psychiatric symptoms.
  - Onset typically in 30s-40s. Fatal.
- **ALS (amyotrophic lateral sclerosis / Lou Gehrig's):** Progressive degeneration of upper and lower **motor neurons**. Muscle weakness, atrophy, paralysis. Cognition usually intact.

### Treatment of Psychological Disorders

**Psychotherapy types:**
- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT):** Identify and change maladaptive thought patterns and behaviors. Most empirically supported for many disorders (depression, anxiety, OCD, PTSD).
- **Psychoanalytic / psychodynamic:** Explore unconscious conflicts, defense mechanisms, early experiences. Long-term, less empirical support.
- **Humanistic (Rogerian / person-centered):** Therapist provides unconditional positive regard; client explores their own self-actualization.
- **Behavioral therapy:** Apply learning principles directly — exposure therapy for phobias, systematic desensitization, token economies.
- **Group therapy:** Multiple clients with similar issues meet with a therapist. Lower cost, social support, normalization.
- **Family therapy:** Treats the family system rather than the individual; useful in eating disorders, conduct disorders, family conflict.

**Biomedical treatments:**
- **SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors):** Block serotonin reuptake → ↑ synaptic serotonin. First-line for depression and most anxiety disorders. Examples: fluoxetine, sertraline, escitalopram.
- **SNRIs (serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors):** Block both serotonin and NE reuptake. Examples: venlafaxine, duloxetine.
- **Tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs):** Older, more side effects (anticholinergic, cardiotoxicity in overdose). Examples: amitriptyline.
- **MAOIs (monoamine oxidase inhibitors):** Inhibit the enzyme that breaks down monoamines. Dietary restrictions (tyramine → hypertensive crisis). Rarely used now.
- **Antipsychotics:**
  - **Typical (first-generation):** Block D2 receptors. Strong on positive symptoms, weak on negative. Side effects: extrapyramidal symptoms (Parkinsonism, tardive dyskinesia). Examples: haloperidol, chlorpromazine.
  - **Atypical (second-generation):** Block D2 + serotonin (5-HT2A) receptors. Better on negative symptoms, fewer EPS. Risk of metabolic syndrome. Examples: risperidone, olanzapine, clozapine.
- **Mood stabilizers — lithium:** First-line for bipolar disorder. Narrow therapeutic window; needs blood monitoring.
- **Anxiolytics — benzodiazepines:** Enhance GABA → sedation, anxiety reduction. Risk of dependence. Examples: diazepam, lorazepam, alprazolam.
- **Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT):** Controlled electrical seizure under anesthesia. For treatment-resistant depression, severe mania, catatonia. More effective than meds in some cases, but stigmatized.

**Evidence base and combination therapy:** For most disorders, **combination of psychotherapy + medication** has the strongest evidence. CBT alone is gold standard for many anxiety disorders; medication alone is rarely first-line outside of acute psychosis or severe mood disorders.

## CHAPTER 8: Social Processes, Attitudes, and Behavior

**Maps to AAMC FC:** 7B (social processes) + 7D (attitudes and behavior).
**MCAT weight:** ~5-7 questions per exam. Group dynamics, conformity/obedience, and ELM/cognitive dissonance are heavily tested.
**Overview:** How groups influence individual behavior, how socialization shapes us, and how attitudes form and change.

### Group Behavior

- **Social facilitation (Zajonc):** The presence of others improves performance on **simple/well-practiced tasks** but impairs performance on **complex/novel tasks**. Why? Others increase arousal → enhances dominant response. If the dominant response is correct (easy task), you do better. If the dominant response is wrong (hard task), you do worse.

- **Social loafing:** Individuals exert less effort in group work (especially when individual contributions aren't identifiable). Reduced by: making contributions identifiable, increasing task importance, small groups.

- **Deindividuation:** Loss of self-awareness and individual identity in a group, leading to behavior you wouldn't normally do. Facilitated by anonymity, group size, arousal. Example: mob behavior, online trolling.

- **Bystander effect:** The more people present, the less likely any one person is to help. Driven by **diffusion of responsibility** (everyone assumes someone else will act) and **pluralistic ignorance** (everyone looks to others for cues about whether it's an emergency, and since no one else is reacting, nobody acts).

- **Group polarization:** Group discussion tends to push opinions toward the extreme of the direction they were already leaning. If a group mildly favors risk before discussion, they'll strongly favor risk afterward.

- **Groupthink (Janis):** Desire for group harmony/conformity overrides realistic evaluation of alternatives. Leads to poor decisions. More likely in highly cohesive groups with a strong leader. Symptoms: illusion of invulnerability, pressure on dissenters, self-censorship.

- **Peer pressure:** Influence from peers to conform in attitudes, behaviors, or values. Most powerful in adolescence.

### Conformity and Obedience

**Conformity (Asch line experiments):** Asch showed participants a line and asked which of three comparison lines matched. Confederate unanimously gave the wrong answer. About **75% conformed at least once**; overall conformity rate ~33%.

Two types:
- **Normative social influence:** Conforming to be liked/accepted (driven by desire to fit in). You go along even though you privately disagree.
- **Informational social influence:** Conforming because you genuinely believe the group knows better (driven by desire to be correct). You actually change your belief.

**Obedience (Milgram experiment):** Participants were told to deliver increasingly severe electric shocks to a "learner" (confederate) when they answered incorrectly. **65% went all the way to the maximum 450V** shock. Factors increasing obedience: proximity of authority figure, perceived legitimacy of authority, physical distance from victim, no role model for defiance.

### Reference Groups

Groups you compare yourself to when evaluating your own status, attitudes, and behavior. Can be groups you belong to or aspire to join.

### Socialization

**Norms** are unwritten social rules:
- **Folkways:** Informal customs (saying "bless you" after a sneeze). Violating them is awkward but not seriously punished.
- **Mores:** Norms with moral significance (honesty, sexual behavior). Violation carries stronger social sanctions.
- **Taboos:** Strongest prohibitions (incest, cannibalism). Violation triggers severe punishment and disgust.
- **Laws:** Formally codified norms enforced by the state.

**Sanctions:** Consequences for violating or following norms.
- **Positive vs negative:** Reward vs punishment.
- **Formal vs informal:** Official (a fine) vs unofficial (a frown).

**Agents of socialization:** Family (primary, earliest), peers (increasingly important in adolescence), media, education, workplace, religion.

**Types of socialization:**
- **Primary socialization:** Early childhood, learning basic norms and language.
- **Secondary socialization:** Later, learning the norms of a specific group or institution.
- **Anticipatory socialization:** Preparing for a future role (medical students learning to act like doctors).
- **Resocialization:** Discarding former behavior patterns and accepting new ones (boot camp, prison, religious conversion).

### Deviance Theories

- **Differential association (Sutherland):** Deviance is learned through interaction with others. You become deviant by associating with deviant people and learning their values and techniques.
- **Labeling theory (Becker):** Deviance is not inherent in an act but is the result of social labeling. **Primary deviance** = the initial act. **Secondary deviance** = the consequences of being labeled (the label becomes a **master status**, shaping how others treat you and how you see yourself). **Stigma** = a discrediting attribute that reduces a person from "whole" to "tainted."
- **Strain theory (Merton):** Deviance results from the gap between culturally valued goals and available legitimate means to achieve them. Five adaptations:

| Adaptation | Goals? | Means? | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Conformity** | Accept | Accept | Working a regular job |
| **Innovation** | Accept | Reject | Drug dealing to get rich |
| **Ritualism** | Reject | Accept | Going through the motions at a dead-end job |
| **Retreatism** | Reject | Reject | Dropping out of society entirely |
| **Rebellion** | Replace | Replace | Revolutionary movements |

- **Control theory:** Strong social bonds (attachment, commitment, involvement, belief) prevent deviance.
- **Conflict theory of deviance:** Powerful groups define what counts as deviant in ways that protect their interests.

### Attitudes and Behavior

**Three components (ABC):**
- **Affective:** Feelings about the attitude object.
- **Behavioral:** Tendency to act on the attitude.
- **Cognitive:** Beliefs about the attitude object.

**Attitude-behavior consistency:** Attitudes don't always predict behavior. **LaPiere study (1934):** Travelled the US with a Chinese couple, recorded that nearly all establishments served them. Later survey: most owners said they wouldn't serve Chinese customers. Big gap between attitude and behavior.

### Elaboration Likelihood Model (Petty & Cacioppo)

- **Central route:** Careful, thoughtful processing of the actual message content. Requires motivation and ability to think deeply. Produces **lasting** attitude change. Example: Reading a detailed policy paper and changing your political view.
- **Peripheral route:** Processing based on superficial cues (attractiveness of speaker, number of arguments, emotional appeal). Requires little cognitive effort. Produces **temporary** attitude change. Example: Buying a product because a celebrity endorses it.

### Cognitive Dissonance (Festinger)

When your **attitudes** and **behavior** conflict, you experience psychological discomfort (dissonance). To reduce it, you change either your attitude or your behavior (usually the attitude, since behavior already happened).

Classic study: Participants did a boring task, then were paid either $1 or $20 to tell the next participant it was fun. The **$1 group** rated the task as more enjoyable afterward. Why? They couldn't justify lying for just $1 (insufficient justification), so they changed their attitude to reduce dissonance ("it must have actually been fun"). The $20 group had sufficient external justification and didn't need to change their attitude.

### Persuasion / Compliance Techniques

- **Foot-in-the-door:** Start with a small request, then escalate to a larger one. Agreeing to the small request creates consistency pressure.
- **Door-in-the-face:** Start with an unreasonably large request (expected to be refused), then make the real, smaller request. Works through reciprocity — they conceded, so you should too.
- **Lowball technique:** Get commitment to a favorable deal, then reveal hidden costs after commitment is made.
- **That's-not-all:** Make an offer, then improve it before the person can respond ("but wait, there's more!"). Triggers reciprocity.

**Role of social media:** Modern attitudes shaped heavily by social media — echo chambers, viral persuasion, influencer endorsements (peripheral route on steroids), and algorithmic content selection.

## CHAPTER 9: Social Interaction

**Maps to AAMC FC:** 8C (social interaction).
**MCAT weight:** ~3-5 questions per exam. Goffman, status/roles, and bureaucracy are reliably tested.
**Overview:** Elements of social interaction (status, roles, groups, networks, organizations) and self-presentation.

### Status

- **Achieved status:** Earned through effort or choice (doctor, college graduate).
- **Ascribed status:** Assigned at birth or involuntarily (race, sex, birth order, royal lineage).
- **Master status:** The status that dominates how others see you, overriding all other statuses (e.g., being a convicted felon, being the President).

### Roles

- **Role conflict:** Tension between roles associated with **different** statuses. Example: A doctor who is also a parent — work demands conflict with family obligations.
- **Role strain:** Tension within **one** role. Example: A teacher who must be both a strict disciplinarian and a nurturing mentor.
- **Role exit (Ebaugh):** Disengaging from a role that has been central to your identity (retiring, divorcing, leaving the priesthood). Four stages: doubt, search for alternatives, action stage, creation of new identity.

### Groups

- **Primary group:** Small, close, long-lasting, emotionally significant (family, close friends).
- **Secondary group:** Larger, impersonal, task-oriented, temporary (coworkers, classmates).
- **In-group:** Group you identify with.
- **Out-group:** Group you don't identify with.
- **Reference group:** Group you compare yourself to for self-evaluation.

### Networks and Social Capital

- **Social network:** The web of relationships connecting individuals.
- **Weak ties (Granovetter):** Acquaintances rather than close friends. Often the most useful for information flow (job leads come from weak ties more than close friends, who already know what you know).
- **Social capital:** The resources available to you through your social networks and relationships. Who you know matters.

### Organizations and Bureaucracy

**Bureaucracy (Weber's ideal type):** Formal organization with:
- Division of labor.
- Hierarchy of authority.
- Written/formal rules.
- Impersonality (treat people by role, not relationship).
- Employment based on qualifications/merit.

Efficient but can become rigid, slow, alienating.

- **Iron law of oligarchy (Michels):** Even democratic organizations inevitably become ruled by a small elite.
- **McDonaldization (Ritzer):** Society adopts the characteristics of fast-food restaurants:
  - **Efficiency** (fastest way to fill a need).
  - **Calculability** (quantity over quality).
  - **Predictability** (same product everywhere).
  - **Control** (technology replaces human judgment).
  Critique of excessive rationalization.
- **Characteristics of formal organizations:** Explicit goals, formal structure, rules, division of labor, specialized roles.

### Self-Presentation and Interacting with Others

**Impression management:** The conscious or unconscious process of regulating how others perceive us. Includes self-presentation strategies (ingratiation, self-promotion, intimidation, exemplification, supplication).

**Goffman's dramaturgical approach:** Life is like a theater. We are all actors performing roles for an audience.
- **Front stage:** The self we present to others. We manage impressions — controlling how we appear.
- **Back stage:** Where we drop the performance and are our "true selves."

### Communication

- **Verbal communication:** Spoken or written language.
- **Nonverbal communication:** Body language, facial expressions, gestures, eye contact, posture, tone of voice. Often conveys more emotional information than words.

### Expressing and Detecting Emotion

- **Universal facial expressions (Ekman):** Cross-cultural recognition of happiness, sadness, fear, anger, surprise, disgust.
- **Eye contact:** Cultural variation — sustained eye contact is engagement in some cultures, aggression in others.
- **Body language:** Open vs closed posture, mirroring, proxemics (use of space).
- **Animal communication:** Signals (alarm calls), gestures (chimp behaviors), displays (peacock feathers), song (whales, birds). Generally simpler than human language but can be sophisticated.

## CHAPTER 10: Social Thinking and Behavior

**Maps to AAMC FC:** 8B (social thinking) + 8C (attraction, aggression, attachment, altruism).
**MCAT weight:** ~5-7 questions per exam. Attribution theory, attachment, and stereotype threat are guaranteed.
**Overview:** How we perceive and explain others' behavior, attraction and aggression, attachment, altruism, and prejudice/discrimination.

### Attraction

- **Proximity / mere exposure:** We like things and people we encounter often. The mere exposure effect (Zajonc): repeated exposure increases liking.
- **Similarity:** "Birds of a feather flock together." We like people similar to us in attitudes, values, background.
- **Reciprocity:** We like people who like us.
- **Physical attractiveness:** The "halo effect" — attractive people are assumed to have other positive traits.

### Aggression

- **Biological factors:** Testosterone, amygdala activation, serotonin deficiency, genetic predisposition.
- **Frustration-aggression hypothesis (Dollard):** Frustration (blocked goal) increases the likelihood of aggression. Aggression may be displaced toward a safer target.
- **Social learning theory (Bandura):** Aggression is learned through observation and imitation (Bobo doll again).

### Attachment

**Bowlby:** Attachment is an innate biological need. Infants form a primary attachment bond with a caregiver (**monotropy**), and this bond serves as a **secure base** for exploration. **Contact comfort** (Harlow's monkey experiments — infant monkeys preferred soft cloth "mothers" over wire ones that gave food) is more important than feeding for attachment formation. There is a **critical period** for attachment in early childhood.

**Ainsworth's Strange Situation** (observe infant's reaction to caregiver leaving and returning):

| Type | Response to Separation | Response to Return | Caregiver Style |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Secure** (~70%) | Distressed but explores | Easily comforted, seeks contact | Responsive, sensitive |
| **Avoidant** (~20%) | Little distress | Ignores/avoids caregiver | Emotionally unavailable |
| **Anxious-ambivalent/resistant** (~10%) | Extremely distressed | Not easily comforted, ambivalent (seeks contact but resists) | Inconsistent |
| **Disorganized** (~5%) | Confused, contradictory behavior | Fearful, disoriented | Frightening, abusive |

Secure attachment in infancy predicts better social relationships, emotional regulation, and mental health later in life.

### Altruism

- **Empathy-altruism hypothesis (Batson):** When we feel empathy for someone, we are motivated to help them for **their** sake (true altruism), not just to reduce our own distress.
- **Kin selection (Hamilton):** We're more likely to help genetic relatives (promotes our shared genes). The closer the genetic relationship, the more likely the help. Quantified by inclusive fitness.
- **Reciprocal altruism (Trivers):** We help non-relatives with the expectation of future reciprocation.
- **Inclusive fitness:** Total reproductive success including offspring AND relatives' reproductive success.
- **Social exchange theory:** Helping is a cost-benefit calculation (not truly selfless).
- **Social support types:** Emotional (caring), instrumental (tangible help), informational (advice), appraisal (feedback).

### Social Perception — Attribution Theory

Attribution is how we explain the causes of behavior — both our own and others'.

- **Dispositional (internal) attribution:** The cause is something about the person (personality, character, ability).
- **Situational (external) attribution:** The cause is something about the environment or circumstances.
- **Heider:** Founded attribution theory; called us "naive psychologists" who try to explain behavior.

**Key biases:**

- **Fundamental attribution error (FAE):** We overattribute others' behavior to **dispositional** (internal) causes and underestimate situational factors. You see someone trip and think "they're clumsy" (disposition) rather than "the floor is uneven" (situation). The single most tested attribution concept on the MCAT.

- **Actor-observer bias:** Extension of FAE. We explain **our own** behavior situationally ("I failed because the test was unfair") but explain **others'** behavior dispositionally ("They failed because they didn't study"). You're the actor for your behavior (you see the situation) and the observer for others' behavior (you see the person).

- **Self-serving bias:** We attribute our **successes** to internal factors ("I'm smart") and our **failures** to external factors ("The test was unfair"). This protects self-esteem.

**MCAT distinction:** FAE applies to judgments of **others**. Self-serving bias applies to judgments of **yourself**. Actor-observer bias is about the systematic difference between how you explain your own vs. others' behavior.

- **Halo effect:** Positive impression in one area (attractiveness) bleeds into other judgments (intelligence, kindness).

### Cultural Differences in Attribution

- **Individualistic cultures** (US, Western Europe) emphasize personal responsibility → more dispositional attributions, more FAE.
- **Collectivistic cultures** (East Asia, Latin America) emphasize context → more situational attributions, less FAE.

### Stereotypes, Prejudice, and Discrimination

Three components (cognitive, affective, behavioral):
- **Stereotype** (cognitive): Generalized belief about a group. Not necessarily negative, but often oversimplified.
- **Prejudice** (affective): Negative attitude/feeling toward a group.
- **Discrimination** (behavioral): Negative action toward a group member.

Types of discrimination:
- **Individual discrimination:** Personal acts of discrimination.
- **Institutional discrimination:** Discrimination embedded in social structures, policies, and practices (redlining, sentencing disparities).

**Stereotype threat (Steele & Aronson):** When members of a stereotyped group are aware of a negative stereotype about their group, they experience anxiety that can **impair performance** on tasks related to that stereotype. This is NOT about believing the stereotype — it's about the fear of confirming it.

**Self-fulfilling prophecy (Merton; Rosenthal/Pygmalion in classrooms):** Expectations about a person or group lead to behavior that causes those expectations to come true. Teacher expects student to fail → gives less attention → student performs poorly → "See, I knew they'd fail."

**Ethnocentrism:** Judging other cultures by the standards of your own (viewing your culture as superior).
**Cultural relativism:** Understanding other cultures on their own terms.

**In-group/out-group dynamics:**
- **In-group bias:** Favoring members of your own group.
- **Out-group homogeneity:** Perceiving members of other groups as more similar to each other than they actually are ("They're all the same").
- **Scapegoat theory:** Prejudice arises from frustration — people blame an out-group for their problems.

**Contact hypothesis (Allport):** Prejudice can be reduced through intergroup contact, but only under specific conditions: **equal status**, **common goals**, **intergroup cooperation**, and **support of authorities/norms**.

## CHAPTER 11: Social Structure and Demographics

**Maps to AAMC FC:** 9A (social structure) + 9B (demographics).
**MCAT weight:** ~6-8 questions per exam. **Major Kaplan gap** — supplement heavily with Khan Academy and the 300-page P/S document.
**Overview:** Sociological theories, social institutions, culture, and demographic change.

> **MAJOR KAPLAN GAP.** This chapter is heavily sociology-based and Kaplan devotes only about one chapter to it. You MUST supplement with Khan Academy P/S videos and the 300-page KA document. This content is tested every single exam.

### Core Sociological Theories

These are lenses for analyzing ANY social phenomenon. The MCAT will describe a scenario and ask which perspective best explains it.

**Functionalism (Structural Functionalism) — Durkheim, Parsons:**
- Society is like an organism: each part serves a function that contributes to the stability of the whole.
- **Manifest functions:** Intended and recognized consequences of a social institution. Example: Education → teaches knowledge and skills.
- **Latent functions (Merton):** Unintended and often unrecognized consequences. Example: Education → provides childcare, builds social networks, acts as a marriage market.
- **Dysfunctions:** Elements that disrupt social stability.
- **Social solidarity:**
  - **Mechanical solidarity:** Bonds based on shared beliefs, values, and activities. Found in small, traditional, homogeneous societies.
  - **Organic solidarity:** Bonds based on interdependence and specialization. Found in large, complex, heterogeneous societies (everyone has a different job and depends on others).
- **Anomie:** A breakdown of social norms and values, leading to feelings of disconnection and purposelessness. Occurs during rapid social change. Durkheim linked anomie to higher suicide rates.

**Conflict theory — Marx, Weber:**
- Society is defined by competition over limited resources. Power and inequality are central.
- Those with power (**bourgeoisie/ruling class**) use social institutions to maintain their dominance over those without (**proletariat/working class**).
- **Class consciousness:** The working class recognizing their exploitation and shared interests.
- **False consciousness:** The working class accepting the dominant ideology that justifies their exploitation ("If I work hard enough, I'll be rich too").
- **Weber's extension:** Stratification is not just about economics (class) but also **status** (prestige) and **power/party** (political influence). Weber also emphasized **rationalization** — the increasing role of calculation and efficiency in modern life (connected to bureaucracy).

**Symbolic Interactionism — Mead, Blumer:**
- Focuses on small-scale, face-to-face interactions rather than large social structures.
- Meaning is created through social interaction and the use of **symbols** (language, gestures, objects).
- People act based on the meaning they assign to things, and those meanings are constantly negotiated in social interaction.
- Connected to Mead's "I" and "Me," the looking-glass self, and labeling theory.

**Social Constructionism (Berger & Luckmann):**
- Reality is not objective but is socially constructed through human interaction and agreement.
- Concepts like race, gender, mental illness, and even "childhood" are social constructions — they exist because society collectively agrees they do, not because of inherent natural categories.
- **MCAT application:** When a passage describes how a concept's meaning varies across cultures or historical periods, social constructionism is the relevant framework.

**Feminist Theory:**
- Focuses on gender inequality as a pervasive feature of social structure.
- Examines how gender intersects with race, class, and sexuality (**intersectionality**).
- Critiques traditional sociology for being male-centered.
- Connected to concepts of patriarchy, gender roles, glass ceiling, wage gap.

**Exchange / Rational Choice Theory:**
- Social behavior is based on a cost-benefit analysis. People seek to maximize rewards and minimize costs.
- Relationships persist when rewards outweigh costs.
- Connected to reciprocity, social exchange in relationships, and economic models of behavior.

### Social Institutions

Social institutions are established patterns of behavior that address fundamental social needs.

**Education:**
- **Manifest functions:** Transmit knowledge, socialize children, confer credentials.
- **Latent functions:** Childcare, social networking, delaying labor force entry.
- **Hidden curriculum:** Unspoken, unofficial lessons and values taught in school (punctuality, obedience, competition, acceptance of hierarchy). Conflict theorists emphasize this reproduces class inequality.
- **Teacher expectancy effect / Pygmalion effect (Rosenthal & Jacobson):** Teachers' expectations of students influence student performance. Told some students were "late bloomers," those students actually improved — because teachers gave them more attention and encouragement.
- **Tracking:** Sorting students into different academic paths. Functionalists say it matches students to appropriate education; conflict theorists say it reproduces inequality (lower-income students disproportionately placed in lower tracks).
- **Educational stratification:** Unequal access to quality education based on SES, race, geography.

**Family:**
- Functions: socialization, emotional support, regulation of sexual behavior, social placement.
- **Kinship:** Social bonds based on common ancestry, marriage, or adoption.
- **Family forms:** Nuclear (two parents + children), extended (includes grandparents, aunts, etc.), single-parent, blended.
- **Endogamy:** Marriage within a social group (same religion, race, class).
- **Exogamy:** Marriage outside a social group.

**Religion:**
- Functions (functionalist view): social cohesion, social control, emotional comfort, meaning-making.
- **Types:** Church (large, established, mainstream), sect (small, separated from mainstream), cult/new religious movement (novel beliefs, charismatic leader).
- **Secularization:** Decline of religion's influence in society.
- **Fundamentalism:** Strict adherence to core religious principles, often in reaction to secularization.
- **Religiosity:** Degree of religious involvement and belief.

**Government / Politics:**
- **Weber's three types of authority:**
  - **Traditional:** Based on long-established customs and practices (monarchy, tribal chiefs).
  - **Charismatic:** Based on personal qualities of the leader (MLK, Gandhi, cult leaders). Inherently unstable — what happens when the leader dies?
  - **Rational-legal:** Based on established laws, rules, and procedures (modern democracies, bureaucracies). Most stable.
- **Power:** The ability to achieve goals even against resistance (Weber).

**Economy:**
- **Capitalism:** Private ownership, profit motive, market competition.
- **Socialism:** Collective or government ownership of means of production.
- **Division of labor (Durkheim):** Specialization of tasks in society. Greater division of labor → organic solidarity.

**Healthcare:**
- **Medicalization:** The process by which normal human conditions or behaviors become defined as medical problems requiring treatment. Examples: ADHD, childbirth (treated as a medical event rather than a natural process), aging. Conflict theorists see this as expanding medical authority and pharmaceutical profits.
- **Sick role (Parsons):** Society has expectations for how a sick person should behave:
  - **Rights:** (1) Exempt from normal social responsibilities. (2) Not held personally responsible for their illness.
  - **Obligations:** (1) Must try to get well. (2) Must seek competent medical help.
  - **Criticism:** Doesn't account for chronic illness (you can't "try to get well" from diabetes), stigmatized conditions (mental illness, HIV), or those who lack access to healthcare.
- **Illness experience:** How individuals and social groups perceive and respond to illness. Shaped by culture, social context, and access to information.
- **Social epidemiology:** The study of how social factors (class, race, gender, environment) influence the distribution of health and disease. (See Ch 12 for full treatment.)

### Culture

**Elements of culture:** Symbols, language, values, beliefs, norms, rituals, material objects.

**Material culture:** Physical objects and technology (tools, clothing, architecture).
**Symbolic / Non-material culture:** Ideas, beliefs, values, norms, language.

**Culture lag (Ogburn):** Material culture (technology) changes faster than non-material culture (values, laws, norms). Example: Technology for genetic cloning exists before society has established ethical guidelines for its use.

**Culture shock:** Disorientation when encountering a very different culture. Stages: honeymoon, frustration, adjustment, acceptance.

**Assimilation:** A minority group adopts the culture of the dominant group, losing their original cultural identity.
**Multiculturalism / pluralism:** Multiple cultures coexist and maintain their identities within a society.

**Subcultures:** Groups within a larger culture that have distinct values, norms, and lifestyles but do not fundamentally oppose the dominant culture (e.g., Amish communities, medical students).
**Countercultures:** Subcultures that actively oppose dominant cultural norms and values (e.g., hippies, punk movement).

**Cultural transmission:** Passing culture from one generation to the next via socialization.
**Evolution of culture:** Cultures change over time through invention, discovery, and diffusion.

### Demographic Transition Model

This model describes how populations change as societies industrialize. Four stages:

| Stage | Birth Rate | Death Rate | Population Growth | Society Type |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Pre-industrial** | High | High | Stable (low growth) | Agrarian, no modern medicine |
| **2. Transitional** | High | **Declining** | **Rapid growth** | Improved sanitation, medicine, nutrition |
| **3. Industrial** | **Declining** | Low | Slowing growth | Urbanization, women's education, access to contraception |
| **4. Post-industrial** | Low | Low | Stable or declining | Modern developed nations |

Some demographers propose a **Stage 5:** Birth rate falls *below* death rate → population decline (Japan, parts of Europe).

### Malthusian Theory

Population grows **exponentially** (1, 2, 4, 8, 16...) while food production grows **arithmetically** (1, 2, 3, 4, 5...). Eventually, population outstrips food supply → famine, disease, war ("positive checks"). Malthus advocated "preventive checks" (delayed marriage, abstinence).

**Criticism:** Malthus didn't predict technological advances in agriculture (Green Revolution), contraception, or demographic transition.

### Population Pyramids

- **Expansive:** Wide base (many young people), narrow top. High birth rate, rapid growth. Typical of developing countries.
- **Constrictive:** Narrow base. Low birth rate, aging population, potential population decline. Typical of developed countries.
- **Stationary:** Roughly equal proportions at each age. Stable population.

### Fertility and Mortality Measures

- **Crude birth rate:** Births per 1,000 population per year.
- **Total fertility rate (TFR):** Average number of children per woman. Replacement level ~2.1 in developed countries.
- **Age-specific fertility rate:** Births per 1,000 women in a specific age group.
- **Crude mortality rate:** Deaths per 1,000 population per year.
- **Infant mortality rate:** Deaths under age 1 per 1,000 live births. Key indicator of a population's health and development.
- **Age-specific mortality rate:** Deaths per 1,000 in a specific age group.
- **Life expectancy:** Average number of years a person can expect to live.

### Migration

**Push factors:** Conditions driving people away from a location (war, poverty, persecution, environmental disasters).
**Pull factors:** Conditions attracting people to a new location (economic opportunity, safety, freedom, family).

**Types:** Immigration (into a country), emigration (out of a country), internal migration (within a country, e.g., rural to urban).

### Social Movements

- **Relative deprivation:** Feeling deprived relative to a comparison group. Can fuel social movements — it's not absolute poverty that motivates revolution, but the perception that others have more.
- Types: reform movements (change specific aspects of society), revolutionary movements (replace existing system), reactionary movements (return to previous state), new social movements (identity/quality of life issues — environmentalism, LGBTQ+ rights).

### Globalization

Increasing interconnectedness of economies, cultures, and populations worldwide. Driven by trade, technology, migration, and communication.
- **Positive effects:** Economic growth, cultural exchange, access to goods and information.
- **Negative effects:** Cultural homogenization, exploitation of developing countries, widening inequality, environmental degradation.

### Urbanization

**Urbanization:** The movement of population from rural to urban areas. Associated with industrialization, economic development, and social change.

**Suburbanization:** Movement from cities to surrounding suburbs. Associated with post-WWII America (highways, GI Bill, white flight).

**Gentrification:** Wealthier people move into previously lower-income urban neighborhoods, renovate properties, raise property values, and displace original residents. **Conflict theory** lens: reflects and reinforces class inequality.

**Urban decline / urban blight:** Deterioration of inner-city areas — poverty, crime, infrastructure decay.

## CHAPTER 12: Social Inequality

**Maps to AAMC FC:** 10A (social inequality, health disparities).
**MCAT weight:** ~3-5 questions per exam, but **rising** — health disparities and social determinants of health are among the fastest-growing topic areas.
**Overview:** Stratification, social class, mobility, intersectionality, and the social determinants of health.

> **KAPLAN GAP.** Only 5% of PS, but questions on health disparities and social determinants of health are increasing. Kaplan coverage is thin — supplement.

### Social Stratification

**Social stratification** is the ranking of people in a hierarchy based on their access to valued resources (wealth, power, prestige). It is a structural feature of society, not just individual outcomes.

**Theoretical perspectives:**
- **Davis-Moore thesis (functionalist):** Stratification is necessary and inevitable. Important positions require more talent and training, so they must offer greater rewards to motivate qualified people to fill them. Criticism: doesn't explain why some high-value jobs (teaching, social work) are poorly compensated.
- **Conflict theory:** Stratification results from exploitation and perpetuates inequality. The wealthy maintain power through control of institutions, ideology, and resources.
- **Weber:** Stratification has three dimensions: **class** (economic position), **status** (social prestige), **party/power** (political influence). These can be independent — a pastor may have high status but low class.

### Social Class

- **Working class:** Manual labor, hourly wages, less education and autonomy.
- **Middle class:** White-collar work, college education, salaried positions.
- **Upper class:** Wealth (often inherited), control of capital, elite social networks.

### Socioeconomic Status (SES)

SES is a composite measure: **income + education + occupation**. The single best predictor of life outcomes on the MCAT — health, education, life expectancy, mental health, and more.

### Social Mobility

- **Intergenerational mobility:** Change in social position from one generation to the next (your parents were working class, you become middle class).
- **Intragenerational mobility:** Change in social position within a single lifetime (you start as a cashier and become a CEO).
- **Vertical mobility:** Movement up or down the stratification hierarchy.
- **Horizontal mobility:** Change in position at the same level (switching from one middle-class job to another).

**Meritocracy:** The idea that social position is determined by individual effort and ability. Conflict theorists argue it's a myth that legitimizes inequality — structural barriers (racism, lack of access to education) prevent equal opportunity.

### Poverty

- **Absolute poverty:** Inability to meet basic needs (food, shelter, clean water).
- **Relative poverty:** Having significantly fewer resources than others in your society, even if basic needs are met.
- **Social exclusion:** Being marginalized from participation in social, economic, and political life.

### Cultural and Social Capital

**Cultural capital (Bourdieu):** Non-financial assets that promote social mobility. Three forms:
1. **Embodied:** Knowledge, skills, dispositions, mannerisms acquired through socialization (knowing how to network at a professional event, speaking "properly").
2. **Objectified:** Physical objects that signify cultural knowledge (books, art, instruments).
3. **Institutionalized:** Formal credentials (degrees, certifications).

Cultural capital is unevenly distributed by class. Upper-class children are socialized with cultural capital that gives them advantages in education and careers. This is how class is **reproduced** across generations even without direct transfer of wealth.

**Social capital:** The resources available to you through your social networks and relationships. Who you know matters. A well-connected person can get job referrals, mentorship, financial help, and information that a socially isolated person cannot. Social capital is a major mechanism of inequality — wealthier people tend to have more valuable social networks.

**Class consciousness vs false consciousness (Marx):** See Chapter 11 for full treatment. Class consciousness drives collective action; false consciousness perpetuates inequality.

### Intersectionality

**Intersectionality (Crenshaw):** Multiple social identities (race, gender, class, sexuality, disability) intersect to create unique experiences of privilege and oppression. A Black woman's experience is not simply "Black experience + woman experience" — it is a qualitatively different experience produced by the intersection of both identities.

**MCAT application:** When a passage describes disparities that are worse for people at the intersection of multiple marginalized identities, intersectionality is the relevant concept.

### Epidemiology — Core Measures

These measures describe how disease is distributed and tracked in populations. The MCAT increasingly tests these as part of social epidemiology.

- **Incidence:** **Number of NEW cases** of a disease in a population over a specified time period. Tells you about the rate of acquiring the disease. Formula: (new cases) / (population at risk × time).
- **Prevalence:** **Total number of cases** (new + existing) in a population at a given time. Tells you the disease burden. Formula: (total cases) / (total population).
- **Relationship:** Prevalence = Incidence × average duration. A chronic disease (long duration) can have low incidence but high prevalence. An acute disease that kills quickly can have high incidence but low prevalence.
- **Mortality (death rate):** Number of deaths in a population per unit time.
- **Morbidity:** Disease state; the rate of disease in a population.

### Social Epidemiology

The study of how social factors (class, race, gender, environment) influence the distribution of health and disease. Connects directly to the social determinants of health below.

### Health Disparities

**Health disparities:** Differences in health outcomes between groups. Lower SES → higher rates of chronic disease, mental illness, infant mortality, lower life expectancy. Racial and ethnic minorities experience worse health outcomes even after controlling for SES (due to racism, discrimination, and cumulative stress).

Disparities exist by:
- **Class:** Lower SES → worse outcomes nearly across the board.
- **Gender:** Women have higher rates of depression, autoimmune disease; men have higher rates of CV disease, suicide.
- **Race:** Black Americans have higher rates of hypertension, infant mortality, maternal mortality. Disparities persist after controlling for SES.

### Healthcare Disparities

**Healthcare disparities:** Differences in access to and quality of healthcare. Includes:
- Lack of insurance.
- Geographic barriers (rural areas, "healthcare deserts").
- Language barriers.
- Implicit bias among providers.
- Lack of culturally competent care.
- Cost barriers (high deductibles, out-of-pocket).

### Social Determinants of Health

Health is not just about biology and individual behavior. It is shaped by the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age. Key social determinants:

- **SES:** The dominant determinant. Poverty → poor nutrition, housing instability, exposure to environmental toxins, chronic stress, limited access to healthcare.
- **Education:** Higher education → better health literacy, higher income, healthier behaviors.
- **Neighborhood / physical environment:** Food deserts, proximity to pollution, crime, access to parks and recreational facilities.
- **Discrimination and racism:** Chronic exposure to discrimination → elevated cortisol, cardiovascular disease, mental health disorders. **Weathering hypothesis (Geronimus):** Cumulative impact of social and economic adversity on health of marginalized populations causes accelerated biological aging.
- **Social support / social networks:** Strong social connections → better health outcomes, lower mortality.

### Environmental Justice

**Environmental justice:** The fair treatment of all people regardless of race, income, or national origin with respect to environmental policies and hazards. In practice, **environmental racism** means that pollution sources (factories, waste sites, highways) are disproportionately located in low-income communities and communities of color. This creates health disparities through exposure to toxins, polluted air and water, and lack of green space.

### Global Health

Patterns of disease and well-being across countries. Wealthy countries have higher rates of chronic, lifestyle-related disease (CV disease, cancer, diabetes); poorer countries have higher rates of infectious disease, maternal/child mortality, and undernutrition. The "double burden" affects middle-income countries dealing with both.

### Second Sickness

**Second sickness:** Illness that arises not from the primary disease itself, but from inadequate or harmful societal/healthcare response to it. Stigma, lack of treatment access, financial ruin from medical bills, social isolation due to illness, and discrimination by providers all constitute "second sickness." Particularly relevant to mental illness, HIV, addiction, and chronic illness in marginalized populations.
