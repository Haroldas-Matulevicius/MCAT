### Brief History of Neuropsychology

- Phrenology (Gall, early 1800s): the discredited idea that personality could be read from skull bumps. Important for one reason: it introduced **localization of function** — different brain regions do different things.
- Pierre Flourens (mid-1800s): used **extirpation/ablation** in animals (removing brain parts and observing behavior). First to **experimentally** prove localization of function.
- **Wilhelm Wundt (1879):** Established the **first psychology laboratory** at the **University of Leipzig** — often cited as the birth of **experimental psychology**. Founded **structuralism**, which used **introspection** (trained self-report of conscious experience) to break consciousness into basic components. **MCAT pattern:** "first psych lab" / "1879" / "Leipzig" / "structuralism" / "introspection" all = Wundt. Contrast with William James (functionalism).
- William James: founder of American **functionalism** — focus on how mental processes help organisms **adapt** to the environment (Darwinian influence). Wrote *Principles of Psychology* (1890); coined "stream of consciousness."
- John Dewey: extended functionalism to education and reflexes as whole units. His 1896 paper *The Reflex Arc Concept in Psychology* is considered the formal start of American functionalism.
- Paul Broca: studied "Tan" (a patient who could only say "tan") — localized speech production to the left frontal lobe.
- Hermann von Helmholtz: measured the speed of a nerve impulse — first to treat psychology as quantifiable physiology.
- Sir Charles Sherrington: inferred the existence of synapses (later confirmed to be chemical, not electrical as he assumed).

### Organization of the Human Nervous System

**Two main divisions:**
- **Central Nervous System (CNS):** Brain + spinal cord.
- **Peripheral Nervous System (PNS):** All neurons outside the CNS.

**PNS subdivides into:**
- **Somatic NS:** Voluntary motor control of skeletal muscle, plus sensory input.
- **Autonomic NS (ANS):** Involuntary control of smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands. Splits into:
  - **Sympathetic ("fight or flight"):** ↑ HR, ↑ BP, dilated pupils, bronchodilation, ↓ digestion, ↑ sweating, glycogenolysis. Releases **norepinephrine** at most target organs (acetylcholine at preganglionic synapses).
  - **Parasympathetic ("rest and digest"):** ↓ HR, constricted pupils, bronchoconstriction, ↑ digestion, ↑ salivation. Releases **acetylcholine** at both pre- and postganglionic synapses.
  - **Vagus nerve (CN X):** Major parasympathetic nerve, carries ~75% of all parasympathetic fibers from brain to thoracic and abdominal organs.

**MCAT mnemonic:** Sympathetic = "Stress." Parasympathetic = "Peaceful." Sympathetic uses NE peripherally, parasympathetic uses ACh.

### Organization of the Brain

**Hindbrain (rhombencephalon)** — keeps you alive:
- **Medulla oblongata:** Vital reflexes — breathing, heart rate, blood pressure, vomiting.
- **Pons:** Sensory and motor pathway between cortex and medulla; sleep/breathing role.
- **Cerebellum:** Coordination, balance, motor learning, posture.
- **Reticular formation:** Arousal, alertness, sleep-wake cycle (extends into midbrain).

**Midbrain (mesencephalon)** — sensorimotor reflexes:
- **Superior colliculi:** Visual reflexes (e.g., reflexive eye movements).
- **Inferior colliculi:** Auditory reflexes (e.g., turning toward sound).
- **Substantia nigra:** Produces dopamine; degeneration causes Parkinson's.

**Forebrain (prosencephalon)** — complex perception, emotion, cognition:
- Thalamus, hypothalamus, limbic system, basal ganglia, cerebrum (see next section).

### Parts of the Forebrain

**Thalamus:** The "sensory relay station." Every sensory modality except **olfaction** routes through here before going to cortex.

**Hypothalamus** (controls homeostasis via the "Four F's" — Feeding, Fighting, Fleeing, Sex):
- **Lateral hypothalamus (LH):** Hunger center. "Lateral = Lunch." Lesion = aphagia.
- **Ventromedial hypothalamus (VMH):** Satiety center. "Ventromedial = Very full." Lesion = hyperphagia.
- **Anterior hypothalamus:** Sexual behavior, sleep, body temperature.
- **Suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN):** Circadian rhythm pacemaker; receives retinal light input, controls melatonin release from pineal.

**Limbic system** (emotion + memory):
- **Amygdala:** Fear, aggression, emotional memory. Damage → reduced fear (Klüver-Bucy).
- **Hippocampus:** Forming new long-term explicit memories. Damage → anterograde amnesia (HM case).
- **Fornix:** White matter tract connecting hippocampus to other limbic structures.
- **Septal nuclei:** Pleasure-seeking, addiction circuits.
- **Cingulate cortex:** Emotion regulation, attention.

**Basal ganglia** (motor coordination, smooth voluntary movement):
- **Caudate, putamen, globus pallidus.**
- Disorders: Parkinson's (loss of dopamine input from substantia nigra), Huntington's (caudate/putamen degeneration → chorea).

**Cerebrum** — outer cortex divided into 4 lobes per hemisphere:

| Lobe | Primary Cortex | Functions |
|---|---|---|
| **Frontal** | Primary motor cortex (precentral gyrus) | Executive function, planning, motor control, **Broca's area** (left, speech production), personality |
| **Parietal** | Primary somatosensory cortex (postcentral gyrus) | Touch, spatial reasoning, body awareness |
| **Temporal** | Primary auditory cortex | Hearing, language comprehension (**Wernicke's area** in left), memory |
| **Occipital** | Primary visual cortex (V1) | Vision, visual processing |

**Aphasias:**
- **Broca's aphasia (expressive/non-fluent):** Damage to Broca's area. Patient understands speech but produces slow, halting, telegraphic speech. "Broca = Broken speech."
- **Wernicke's aphasia (receptive/fluent):** Damage to Wernicke's area. Patient produces fluent but meaningless "word salad" and cannot understand speech. "Wernicke = Wordy but Wrong."
- **Conduction aphasia:** Damage to **arcuate fasciculus** (connecting Broca's and Wernicke's). Comprehension and production intact, but cannot repeat what is said.

**Lateralization:** The two hemispheres specialize.
- **Dominant (usually left):** Language, analysis, logic, math.
- **Non-dominant (usually right):** Spatial reasoning, music, intuition, face recognition, emotion.

**Corpus callosum:** Massive white matter bundle connecting the two hemispheres.

**Split-brain studies (Sperry):** Patients with severed corpus callosum (treatment for severe epilepsy). Each hemisphere processes information independently. Object shown to right visual field (left hemisphere) → patient can name it. Same object to left visual field (right hemisphere) → patient cannot verbally name it but can pick it out by hand.

### Influences on Behavior — Genetics and Endocrine

**Heritability:** The proportion of variation in a trait within a population that is due to genetic differences.
- **Twin studies:** Compare monozygotic (MZ, identical) vs dizygotic (DZ, fraternal) concordance rates. Higher MZ concordance vs DZ → greater genetic contribution.
- **Adoption studies:** Compare adopted children to biological vs adoptive parents to disentangle genes from environment.
- **Family studies:** Look for trait clustering within families.
- **Gene-environment interaction:** Genes don't act in a vacuum. Environment can switch genes on/off (epigenetics) and modify the expression of genetic predispositions.

**Endocrine system — hypothalamus-pituitary axis:**
The hypothalamus controls the pituitary, which controls most other endocrine glands.

**Anterior pituitary** (mnemonic **FLAT PEG**):
- **F**SH — follicle-stimulating hormone (gonads)
- **L**H — luteinizing hormone (gonads)
- **A**CTH — adrenocorticotropic hormone (adrenal cortex → cortisol)
- **T**SH — thyroid-stimulating hormone (thyroid)
- **P**rolactin — milk production
- **E**ndorphins — pain reduction
- **G**H — growth hormone

**Posterior pituitary** (does not synthesize hormones — releases them from hypothalamus):
- **Oxytocin:** Bonding, uterine contraction, milk letdown, social trust.
- **ADH (vasopressin):** Water retention by kidneys.

**HPA axis** (stress pathway): Hypothalamus → CRH → anterior pituitary → ACTH → adrenal cortex → **cortisol**.

**Adrenal glands:**
- **Adrenal cortex:** Steroid hormones — cortisol (stress), aldosterone (Na+ retention), small amounts of sex hormones.
- **Adrenal medulla:** Epinephrine and norepinephrine (sympathetic NS, fight-or-flight).

**Thyroid:** T3 and T4 — metabolism, growth. Calcitonin (lowers blood calcium).

**Gonads:** Testes (testosterone — sex drive, aggression, secondary sex characteristics in males); Ovaries (estrogen, progesterone — menstrual cycle, pregnancy).

**Pineal gland:** Melatonin — circadian rhythm and sleep regulation.

### Development of the Nervous System

**Neurulation (weeks 3-4 of embryogenesis):**
- The **notochord** signals the overlying **ectoderm** to thicken into the **neural plate**.
- Edges of the neural plate rise as **neural folds**, flanking a central depression called the **neural groove**.
- Neural folds meet at the midline and fuse → **neural tube** (future CNS). The rostral end closes first; the caudal end last.
- **Neural crest cells** pinch off at the crest of the folds and migrate throughout the body. (Full derivative list — PNS ganglia, Schwann cells, melanocytes, adrenal medulla, facial bone/cartilage, odontoblasts — lives in `BB/BB_Organ_Systems.md` §7.4.)
- Neural tube → **3 primary brain vesicles** → **5 secondary vesicles** → adult brain structures.
- **Neural tube defects:**
  - **Anencephaly:** failure of the **rostral** neural tube to close → absent forebrain. Lethal.
  - **Spina bifida:** failure of the **caudal** neural tube to close → exposed spinal cord.
  - Folic acid supplementation prevents both.

**Alar plate vs basal plate (spinal cord and brainstem differentiation):**
- Along the inner wall of the neural tube, a longitudinal groove called the **sulcus limitans** divides each half into two plates.
- **Alar plate (dorsal):** becomes the **sensory** neurons of the spinal cord dorsal horn. Mnemonic: "**A**lar = **A**fferent = **A**bove."
- **Basal plate (ventral):** becomes the **motor** neurons of the spinal cord ventral horn. Mnemonic: "**B**asal = **B**elow = motor."
- This dorsal-sensory / ventral-motor pattern extends up into the brainstem — cranial nerve nuclei follow the same arrangement.
- Connects directly to the **Bell-Magendie law** (dorsal roots carry sensory info, ventral roots carry motor) — the structural basis is alar vs basal plate origin.

**Placenta (brief — full treatment in BB):**
- Fetal origin (**trophoblast** of the blastocyst) + maternal **endometrium** (decidua).
- Key functions: gas exchange for the fetus, hormone production (notably **hCG**), immune barrier (only **IgG** crosses).
- Detailed content — hCG handoff timing, three fetal shunts (ductus venosus, foramen ovale, ductus arteriosus), notochord germ-layer trap — lives in `BB/BB_Organ_Systems.md` §3 (Circulatory) and §7 (Reproductive).

**Infant reflexes** (primitive, present at birth, disappear with CNS maturation):
- **Rooting reflex:** Infant turns head toward stimulus on cheek (feeding).
- **Sucking reflex:** Sucks anything that touches the roof of mouth.
- **Moro reflex:** Sudden loss of support → arms flung out, then drawn in (like an embrace).
- **Babinski reflex:** Stroking sole of foot → toes fan out. Normal in infants, abnormal in adults (suggests upper motor neuron damage).
- **Grasping (palmar) reflex:** Object placed in palm → infant grasps.

**Fixed action patterns:** Innate, stereotyped behaviors triggered by a specific stimulus (sign stimulus). Once initiated, they run to completion. Example: greylag goose retrieving an egg.

**Sensitive periods vs critical periods:**
- **Critical period:** A narrow window during which a specific experience MUST occur for normal development (e.g., visual cortex development).
- **Sensitive period:** A broader window during which the organism is more responsive to environmental input but development can still occur outside of it.

**Imprinting (Lorenz):** Newly hatched birds form attachment to the first moving object they see (usually the mother). Demonstrates a critical period — once the window closes, imprinting cannot occur.

### Methods to Study the Brain

| Method | What it measures | Spatial resolution | Temporal resolution |
|---|---|---|---|
| **EEG** | Electrical activity at scalp | Poor | Excellent (ms) |
| **CT** | X-ray cross-sections (structure) | Good | None (snapshot) |
| **MRI** | Magnetic field, soft tissue (structure) | Excellent | None (snapshot) |
| **fMRI** | Blood oxygenation (function) | Good | Moderate (seconds) |
| **PET** | Radioactive tracer uptake (function/metabolism) | Moderate | Poor (minutes) |
| **Lesion studies** | Behavior after damage | Variable | N/A |

**Trade-off:** Methods with high temporal resolution (EEG) sacrifice spatial detail. Methods with high spatial resolution (MRI/fMRI) sacrifice timing.
