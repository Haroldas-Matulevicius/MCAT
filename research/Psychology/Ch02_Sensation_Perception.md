## CHAPTER 2: Sensation and Perception

**Maps to AAMC FC:** 6A (sensing the environment).
**MCAT weight:** ~6-8 questions per exam. Vision, hearing, and perception principles dominate.
**Overview:** How sensory receptors transduce stimuli into neural signals (sensation) and how the brain interprets them (perception).

### Sensation vs. Perception

These are two different processes, and the MCAT loves testing the distinction.

- **Sensation** = detection. Your sensory receptors pick up a stimulus and convert it to neural signals (**transduction**). This is bottom-level, raw data.
- **Perception** = interpretation. Your brain takes those signals and makes meaning out of them. This is where context, expectations, and experience come in.

Example: Your retina detects wavelengths of 700 nm (sensation). Your brain interprets that as "red apple" (perception).

### Thresholds

- **Absolute threshold:** the minimum stimulus intensity you can detect 50% of the time.
- **Difference threshold (JND — just noticeable difference):** the smallest change in stimulus intensity that you can detect 50% of the time.
- **Weber's law:** ΔI / I = k. The JND is proportional to the original stimulus. If you're holding a 10 lb weight, you might notice an extra 1 lb (10%). If you're holding 100 lbs, you'd need 10 lbs added to notice. The ratio stays constant. MCAT loves calculation questions with this.
- **Subliminal stimuli:** below absolute threshold. You can't consciously detect them, but they may still influence behavior (priming effects).

### Signal Detection Theory (SDT)

This replaces the simple "can you detect it or not" model with a framework that accounts for **response bias** — your willingness to say "yes, I detected it."

| | Stimulus Present | Stimulus Absent |
|---|---|---|
| **Say "Yes"** | **Hit** | **False Alarm** |
| **Say "No"** | **Miss** | **Correct Rejection** |

Detection depends on both **sensitivity** (how good your sensory system is) and **criterion** (how willing you are to say "yes"). A radiologist looking for tumors might lower their criterion (say "yes" more) to avoid misses, but that increases false alarms. This is a cost-benefit decision, not just a sensory one.

**MCAT application:** SDT shows up in passages about medical screening, security, or any detection scenario. Know the 2x2 cold.

### Sensory Adaptation

When a stimulus is constant, your receptors decrease their firing rate over time. You stop noticing it. Examples: the feel of clothes on your skin, background noise, the smell of your own home.

**Important exception:** Pain receptors do NOT fully adapt. Pain signals danger.

### Vision

**Eye anatomy pathway:** Light enters through the **cornea** (most refraction happens here) → **aqueous humor** → **pupil** (controlled by **iris**) → **lens** (fine-tunes focus via **accommodation**, controlled by ciliary muscles) → **vitreous humor** → **retina**.

**Photoreceptors:**
- **Rods:** ~120 million. Sensitive to dim light. Concentrated in the periphery. Contain **rhodopsin**. Scotopic (night) vision. One type only — no color.
- **Cones:** ~6 million. Require bright light. Concentrated in the **fovea** (center of retina, highest acuity). Three types (S, M, L for short, medium, long wavelengths). Photopic (daylight) and color vision.

**MCAT trap:** The fovea has the highest visual acuity because cones there have a 1:1 ratio with ganglion cells. In the periphery, many rods converge on one ganglion cell — great for sensitivity, terrible for detail.

**Color vision theories** (both correct, at different levels):
- **Trichromatic theory (Young-Helmholtz):** Three types of cones (R, G, B). Color = relative activation of these three. Explains color blindness (missing a cone type). Operates at **retinal level**.
- **Opponent-process theory (Hering):** Color is processed in antagonistic pairs: red-green, blue-yellow, black-white. Cells excited by one color, inhibited by the other. Explains afterimages. Operates at **ganglion cell and LGN level**.

**Visual pathway:** Retina → **optic nerve** → **optic chiasm** (nasal fibers cross) → **lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN)** of thalamus → **primary visual cortex (V1)** in occipital lobe.

- **Feature detectors (Hubel & Wiesel):** V1 neurons respond to specific features — edges, angles, movement.
- **Parallel processing:** color, motion, form, and depth processed simultaneously in different areas, then integrated.

### Hearing and Vestibular Sense

**Ear anatomy:**
- **Outer ear:** pinna (collects sound) → auditory canal → **tympanic membrane** (eardrum, vibrates).
- **Middle ear:** **ossicles** — malleus, incus, stapes (smallest bones in body). Amplify vibrations ~22x. Stapes pushes on **oval window**.
- **Inner ear:** **cochlea** (fluid-filled, coiled tube). Vibrations create waves in cochlear fluid → push on **basilar membrane** → bend **hair cells** (mechanoreceptors) → neural signals → **auditory nerve** → auditory cortex (temporal lobe), with relay through **medial geniculate nucleus (MGN)** of thalamus.

**Pitch perception theories:**
- **Place theory:** Different frequencies vibrate different locations on basilar membrane. High-frequency → **base** (near oval window, stiff/narrow). Low-frequency → **apex** (far end, flexible/wide). Best explains high-frequency hearing.
- **Frequency theory (temporal theory):** Rate of firing of auditory nerve matches frequency. Works for low frequencies (<1000 Hz). For intermediate frequencies, **volley principle** — groups of neurons fire in alternation to collectively match the frequency.

**MCAT loves:** "Base = high pitch, apex = low pitch." Counterintuitive and frequently tested.

**Hearing loss:**
- **Conductive:** Problem in outer or middle ear (blocked canal, damaged ossicles, perforated eardrum). Often treatable.
- **Sensorineural:** Damage to cochlear hair cells or auditory nerve. Usually permanent. From aging, loud noise.

**Vestibular sense:** Detects head position and acceleration.
- **Semicircular canals:** Three orthogonal canals → rotational movement (3 planes).
- **Otolith organs:** Utricle and saccule → linear acceleration and gravity. Fluid movement bends hair cells.

### Other Senses

**Somatosensation:** Touch, pressure, temperature, pain. Receptors with different specializations:
- **Meissner's corpuscles** — light touch.
- **Pacinian corpuscles** — deep pressure/vibration.
- **Merkel's discs** — sustained pressure.
- **Free nerve endings** — pain/temperature.

**Pain and gate control theory (Melzack & Wall):** Pain signals travel via small nerve fibers to spinal cord. A "gate" in the spinal cord can be closed by activation of **large nerve fibers** (non-pain touch/pressure) — this is why rubbing a stubbed toe helps. Also modulated by descending signals from the brain (distraction, mood, expectations alter pain). **Endorphins** also close the gate.

**Taste (gustation):** Five basic tastes — **sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami**. Taste receptor cells on taste buds, clustered in papillae on tongue. Cranial nerves **VII (facial), IX (glossopharyngeal), X (vagus)** carry signals to the brain.

**Smell (olfaction):** Olfactory receptor neurons in nasal cavity → **olfactory bulb** → olfactory cortex. Unique among senses because it does NOT relay through the thalamus first (goes directly to cortex and limbic system). This is why smells trigger strong emotional memories.

### Object Recognition / Perception

**Bottom-up processing (data-driven):** Building perception from raw sensory input. Start with individual features and assemble into a whole. Example: reading an unfamiliar character by character.

**Top-down processing (knowledge-driven):** Using prior knowledge, expectations, and context to interpret sensory input. Example: reading sloppy handwriting because you know what word to expect. Why "Th3 c4t s4t on th3 m4t" is easy.

**MCAT distinction:** If context/expectation matters → top-down. If you're analyzing raw features without prior expectation → bottom-up.

**Gestalt principles** (your brain organizes visual input into coherent wholes):
- **Proximity:** Objects near each other are grouped together.
- **Similarity:** Similar objects are grouped together.
- **Closure:** We fill in gaps to see complete shapes.
- **Continuity:** We follow smooth, continuous lines rather than choppy ones.
- **Figure-ground:** We separate a main figure from the background.
- **Common fate:** Objects moving in the same direction are grouped.

**Depth cues:**
- **Binocular** (require both eyes): retinal disparity (slightly different images from each eye), convergence (eyes turn inward for close objects).
- **Monocular** (one eye is enough): relative size (smaller = farther), interposition/overlap (blocking = closer), linear perspective (parallel lines converge), texture gradient, motion parallax (closer things move faster across visual field), aerial perspective (farther objects are hazier).
