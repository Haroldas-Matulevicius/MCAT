## CHAPTER 3: Learning, Memory, and Behavior

**Maps to AAMC FC:** 7C (learning) + 6B (memory).
**MCAT weight:** ~5-7 questions per exam. Conditioning, memory systems, and biological basis are guaranteed.
**Overview:** How experience changes behavior (learning) and how information is encoded, stored, and retrieved (memory).

### Habituation, Dishabituation, Sensitization (Non-Associative Learning)

- **Habituation:** Decreased response to a repeated, non-threatening stimulus (you stop noticing the AC humming).
- **Dishabituation:** Sudden recovery of response when a new stimulus is introduced.
- **Sensitization:** Increased response to a stimulus, often after a threatening or intense event. Can generalize to other stimuli.

### Classical Conditioning (Pavlov)

This is **learning through association**. A neutral stimulus becomes associated with a stimulus that naturally produces a response.

**Core terms:**
- **Unconditioned stimulus (UCS):** Naturally produces a response without learning (food).
- **Unconditioned response (UCR):** Natural, unlearned reaction to UCS (salivation to food).
- **Conditioned stimulus (CS):** Previously neutral stimulus that, after pairing with UCS, now produces a response (bell).
- **Conditioned response (CR):** Learned reaction to CS (salivation to bell).

**Key processes:**
- **Acquisition:** CS and UCS repeatedly paired. CS must come **before** UCS (forward conditioning is most effective).
- **Extinction:** CS presented repeatedly without UCS → CR gradually disappears.
- **Spontaneous recovery:** After extinction, CR reappears after a rest period (weaker than before).
- **Generalization:** Similar stimuli to CS also produce CR (Little Albert feared white rat, then also feared white rabbit).
- **Discrimination:** Ability to distinguish between similar stimuli (responding to CS but not to similar stimuli).
- **Higher-order conditioning:** A new neutral stimulus paired with an established CS (rather than the original UCS).

**Biological preparedness and the Garcia effect:**
- Some associations learned more easily due to evolutionary preparedness.
- **Taste aversion (Garcia effect):** If you eat something and get sick hours later, you develop a strong aversion to that food — even though the CS-UCS gap is very long. Violates the usual requirement for CS-UCS close timing.
- **One-trial learning:** Taste aversion can form after a single pairing. Evolutionary advantage: avoiding poisonous food.

### Operant Conditioning (Skinner)

Learning through **consequences**. Behavior is shaped by what happens after it.

**The 2x2 matrix** (know this cold):

| | Adding stimulus (+) | Removing stimulus (-) |
|---|---|---|
| **Increase behavior (reinforcement)** | **Positive reinforcement** (give reward) | **Negative reinforcement** (remove something unpleasant) |
| **Decrease behavior (punishment)** | **Positive punishment** (add something unpleasant) | **Negative punishment** (take away something desired) |

Examples:
- Positive reinforcement: Dog sits → gets a treat.
- Negative reinforcement: Put on seatbelt → annoying buzzer stops. Take aspirin → headache goes away.
- Positive punishment: Child hits sibling → gets spanked.
- Negative punishment: Teenager breaks curfew → loses phone privileges.

**MCAT trap:** "Positive" and "negative" do NOT mean good and bad. They mean adding (+) and removing (-) a stimulus. "Reinforcement" always increases behavior; "punishment" always decreases it.

**Schedules of reinforcement:**

| Schedule | Pattern | Response Rate | Resistance to Extinction |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Fixed-ratio (FR)** | Reward after a set number of responses | High, with brief **post-reinforcement pause** | Moderate |
| **Variable-ratio (VR)** | Reward after an unpredictable number of responses | **Highest**, no pauses | **Highest** (slot machines, social media) |
| **Fixed-interval (FI)** | Reward after a set time period | **Scallop pattern** (ramps up near end) | Low |
| **Variable-interval (VI)** | Reward after unpredictable time periods | Slow and steady | High |

**Mnemonic:** Variable-ratio is the "gambling schedule" — hardest to extinguish.

**Other operant concepts:**
- **Shaping:** Reinforcing successive approximations to a target behavior. Used to train novel behaviors.
- **Escape learning:** Behavior that terminates an ongoing aversive stimulus (leaving a loud room).
- **Avoidance learning:** Behavior that prevents an aversive stimulus (taking an alternate route to avoid traffic). Harder to extinguish — you never experience the aversive stimulus to learn it's gone.
- **Instinctive drift:** Tendency for learned behavior to revert to innate (instinctive) behavior patterns. An animal trained to deposit coins may start "washing" them instead — resembling natural foraging.

### Observational Learning (Bandura)

Learning by watching others — no direct reinforcement required.

**Bobo doll experiment:** Children watched an adult model behave aggressively toward a Bobo doll (hitting, kicking, verbal aggression). Children who observed the aggressive model were significantly more likely to imitate the aggressive behavior, especially when the model was **rewarded** or faced no consequences.

**Vicarious reinforcement/punishment:** You're more likely to imitate behavior you've seen rewarded in others, less likely to imitate behavior you've seen punished.

**Mirror neurons:** Neurons that fire both when you perform an action AND when you observe someone else performing that action. Thought to be the neural basis for observational learning and empathy. (Their exact role is still debated.)

### Memory — Encoding

- **Automatic processing:** Encoding without effort (where you ate lunch, sequence of events).
- **Effortful processing:** Requires conscious attention (studying for the MCAT).
- **Levels of processing (Craik & Lockhart):** Deeper processing = better retention.
  - **Shallow** = structural (how does it look?).
  - **Intermediate** = phonemic (how does it sound?).
  - **Deep** = semantic (what does it mean?).
- **Elaborative rehearsal:** Connecting new information to existing knowledge. Far better than maintenance rehearsal (rote repetition).
- **Self-referencing effect:** Relating information to yourself enhances encoding.
- **Spacing effect:** Distributed practice > massed practice (cramming). This is literally why Anki works.

### Memory — Storage (three-stage model)

1. **Sensory memory:** Brief, large-capacity buffer.
   - **Iconic** (visual): ~0.5 seconds.
   - **Echoic** (auditory): ~3-4 seconds (longer, why you can "replay" what someone just said).

2. **Short-term memory (STM) / Working memory:** Capacity **7 ± 2 items** (Miller's magic number). Duration ~20-30 seconds without rehearsal. **Chunking** increases effective capacity.
   - **Working memory (Baddeley's model):** Active manipulation of information. Components:
     - **Phonological loop** (verbal information).
     - **Visuospatial sketchpad** (visual/spatial information).
     - **Central executive** (directs attention).
     - **Episodic buffer** (integrates information).

3. **Long-term memory (LTM):** Essentially unlimited capacity and duration.
   - **Explicit (declarative)** — conscious recall, requires hippocampus for consolidation:
     - **Episodic:** Personal experiences ("I had pasta last Tuesday").
     - **Semantic:** General facts ("Rome is the capital of Italy").
   - **Implicit (non-declarative)** — unconscious, does NOT require hippocampus:
     - **Procedural:** Motor skills, habits (riding a bike). Cerebellum + basal ganglia.
     - **Priming:** Prior exposure influences later responses.
     - **Classical conditioning:** Conditioned emotional responses.

### Memory — Retrieval

- **Recall:** Generating information from memory (fill-in-the-blank). Hardest.
- **Recognition:** Identifying previously learned information (multiple choice). Easier.
- **Relearning:** Learning material again faster than the first time (savings method).
- **Retrieval cues:** Stimuli that help access stored memories.
- **Context-dependent memory:** Recall is better when the retrieval environment matches the encoding environment.
- **State-dependent memory:** Recall is better when your internal state (mood, intoxication) matches encoding state.
- **Mood-congruent memory:** You preferentially recall memories that match your current mood.
- **Primacy effect:** Better recall of items at the start of a list (LTM).
- **Recency effect:** Better recall of items at the end of a list (STM).

### Forgetting

- **Encoding failure:** Information never entered LTM in the first place.
- **Decay:** Memory trace fades over time (mainly affects sensory and STM).
- **Forgetting curve (Ebbinghaus):** Most forgetting happens rapidly after learning, then levels off. Spaced review counteracts this.
- **Proactive interference:** OLD memories block NEW learning. "Pro = forward" — old interferes going forward. Example: You moved and keep driving to your old house.
- **Retroactive interference:** NEW memories block OLD recall. "Retro = backward" — new interferes going backward. Example: Learning your new phone number makes it hard to remember the old one.
- **Source monitoring error:** Remembering information but misattributing its source.
- **Misinformation effect (Loftus):** Post-event information alters memory of the original event. Loftus's car crash study: "How fast were the cars going when they *smashed* into each other?" → higher speed estimates than "hit."
- **Repressed/false memories:** Highly contested in psychology. False memories can be implanted via suggestion.

**MCAT tip for interference:** "ProOld, RetroNew." Proactive = old interferes with new. Retroactive = new interferes with old.

### Memory Disorders

- **Alzheimer's disease:** Progressive loss of memory and cognition. Associated with **acetylcholine** deficiency, **amyloid plaques**, and **neurofibrillary tangles** (tau protein). Hippocampus affected early. Treatment: **cholinesterase inhibitors** (e.g., donepezil) raise ACh levels. (See Chapter 7 for biological detail.)
- **Korsakoff's syndrome:** Caused by **thiamine (B1) deficiency**, usually from chronic alcoholism. Severe anterograde amnesia, **confabulation** (making up memories to fill gaps).

### Neurobiology of Learning and Memory

- **Long-term potentiation (LTP):** Repeated stimulation of a synapse strengthens the connection. The cellular mechanism of learning and memory. Involves **NMDA receptors** (a glutamate receptor) and AMPA receptors.
- **Synaptic plasticity:** Synapses change in strength with use; underlies all learning.
- **Hippocampus:** Critical for forming new explicit memories and consolidation. The famous **HM case** (Henry Molaison) had bilateral medial temporal lobe removal (including hippocampi) — could not form new explicit memories, but procedural learning was intact.
- **Amygdala:** Emotional memory, especially fear.
- **Cerebellum:** Procedural memory, classical conditioning.
- **Neural plasticity:** The brain's ability to reorganize itself through new connections, throughout life. Greater in childhood; decreases (but does not disappear) with age.
