## CHAPTER 7: Psychological Disorders

**Maps to AAMC FC:** 7A (psychological disorders).
**MCAT weight:** ~6-8 questions per exam. Schizophrenia, depression, anxiety/OCD, and biological basis are all guaranteed.
**Overview:** DSM-5 classification, major disorder categories, biological basis of disorders, and treatments.

### Understanding Psychological Disorders

**Classification:** **DSM-5** (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual, 5th edition). Uses descriptive criteria (symptoms, duration, impairment). The MCAT doesn't expect you to diagnose, but you need to recognize key features.

**Models:**
- **Biomedical model:** Disorders are medical diseases with biological causes.
- **Biopsychosocial model:** Disorders result from the interaction of biological, psychological, and social factors. **This is the MCAT-preferred model.**

**Rates of disorders:** Over a lifetime, ~50% of US adults will meet criteria for a mental disorder. Anxiety and depression are most common.

**Deinstitutionalization:** Mid-20th century shift away from large psychiatric hospitals toward community-based care. Driven by new medications (antipsychotics) and concerns about institutional abuses. Side effects: increased homelessness, incarceration of mentally ill.

### Anxiety Disorders

- **Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD):** Excessive, uncontrollable worry about multiple things for **6+ months**.
- **Specific phobia:** Irrational fear of a specific object or situation.
- **Social anxiety disorder:** Fear of social situations and being judged.
- **Panic disorder:** Recurrent unexpected **panic attacks** (intense fear with physical symptoms — racing heart, shortness of breath, chest pain). Can develop **agoraphobia** (fear of situations where escape might be difficult).
- **Separation anxiety disorder:** Excessive distress when separated from attachment figures.

### Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

- **Obsessions:** Intrusive, unwanted, persistent thoughts (e.g., contamination fears).
- **Compulsions:** Repetitive behaviors performed to reduce anxiety (e.g., hand washing).
- Note: OCD is **no longer classified as an anxiety disorder** in DSM-5 — it has its own category.

### Trauma- and Stressor-Related Disorders

**PTSD (post-traumatic stress disorder):** Follows exposure to a traumatic event. Four symptom clusters:
1. **Intrusion:** Flashbacks, nightmares, intrusive memories.
2. **Avoidance:** Avoiding reminders of the trauma.
3. **Negative changes in cognition/mood:** Detachment, distorted blame, persistent negative emotional state.
4. **Arousal/reactivity:** Hyperarousal, exaggerated startle, hypervigilance, sleep problems.

### Somatic Symptom and Related Disorders

- **Somatic symptom disorder:** Distressing physical symptoms that disrupt life, with excessive thoughts/feelings about them.
- **Illness anxiety disorder:** Preoccupation with having or acquiring a serious illness, despite few or no symptoms.

### Depressive Disorders

- **Major Depressive Disorder (MDD):** **5+ symptoms for 2+ weeks.** Symptoms: depressed mood, **anhedonia** (loss of pleasure), weight/appetite changes, sleep changes, fatigue, worthlessness/guilt, concentration difficulties, **suicidal ideation**.
- **Persistent depressive disorder (dysthymia):** Depressed mood most days for **2+ years**, less severe than MDD.
- **Premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD):** Severe mood symptoms tied to menstrual cycle.
- **Biological basis:** **Serotonin** and **norepinephrine** deficiency hypothesis. SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors) are first-line treatment.

### Bipolar and Related Disorders

- **Bipolar I:** Full **manic episodes** (elevated mood, grandiosity, decreased need for sleep, rapid speech, risky behavior, possible psychotic features) + depressive episodes.
- **Bipolar II:** **Hypomanic** episodes (less severe than mania, no psychotic features) + depressive episodes.

### Schizophrenia

- **Positive symptoms** (added to normal functioning):
  - **Hallucinations** — usually **auditory** (hearing voices).
  - **Delusions** — paranoid (being persecuted), grandiose (special powers), referential (TV is talking to me).
  - **Disorganized speech** (word salad, loose associations).
  - **Disorganized behavior.**
- **Negative symptoms** (taken away from normal functioning):
  - **Flat affect, avolition** (lack of motivation), **alogia** (reduced speech), **anhedonia, social withdrawal**.
- **Cognitive symptoms:** Working memory deficits, impaired executive function, attention problems.
- **Dopamine hypothesis:** Excess dopamine (especially D2 receptors in **mesolimbic pathway**) → positive symptoms. Supported by: antipsychotics block D2; amphetamines (↑ dopamine) can cause psychosis-like symptoms. Negative symptoms may involve **hypodopaminergic** activity in **mesocortical pathway** (prefrontal cortex).
- **Glutamate hypothesis:** **NMDA receptor hypofunction**. PCP (NMDA antagonist) produces schizophrenia-like symptoms.

### Dissociative Disorders

- **Dissociative identity disorder (DID):** Two or more distinct personality states.
- **Dissociative amnesia:** Inability to recall important personal information, usually after trauma.
- **Depersonalization/derealization disorder:** Persistent feeling of being detached from one's own body or from reality.

### Personality Disorders (know the clusters)

- **Cluster A (odd/eccentric):** **Paranoid, Schizoid** (detached, flat), **Schizotypal** (eccentric beliefs, magical thinking).
- **Cluster B (dramatic/emotional):** **Antisocial** (disregard for others' rights, lack of remorse), **Borderline** (unstable relationships, self-image, impulsivity), **Histrionic** (attention-seeking, excessive emotion), **Narcissistic** (grandiosity, need for admiration).
- **Cluster C (anxious/fearful):** **Avoidant** (social inhibition, inadequacy), **Dependent** (need to be taken care of), **OCPD** (rigidity, perfectionism — distinct from OCD).

### Eating Disorders

- **Anorexia nervosa:** Restriction of food intake, intense fear of weight gain, distorted body image. Significantly low body weight.
- **Bulimia nervosa:** Recurrent episodes of binge eating followed by compensatory behaviors (vomiting, laxatives, excessive exercise). Usually normal-to-overweight.

### Neurodevelopmental Disorders

- **ADHD:** Inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity. Onset before age 12. Treated with stimulants (paradoxically calming).
- **Autism spectrum disorder (ASD):** Deficits in social communication and restricted/repetitive behaviors. Wide range of severity.

### Biological Basis of Nervous System Disorders

- **Alzheimer's disease:**
  - **Amyloid plaques** (beta-amyloid protein deposits).
  - **Neurofibrillary tangles** (hyperphosphorylated tau protein).
  - **Acetylcholine deficit** — neurons in basal forebrain die first.
  - Hippocampus affected early → memory loss.
  - Treatment: **cholinesterase inhibitors** (donepezil, rivastigmine) ↑ ACh; NMDA receptor antagonist (memantine) for moderate-severe.
- **Parkinson's disease:**
  - Loss of dopaminergic neurons in **substantia nigra** → ↓ dopamine in basal ganglia.
  - Symptoms: resting **tremor**, **rigidity**, **bradykinesia** (slow movement), postural instability.
  - Treatment: **L-DOPA** (dopamine precursor; crosses blood-brain barrier).
- **Huntington's disease:**
  - **Autosomal dominant** (CAG repeat expansion in HTT gene).
  - Degeneration of **caudate and putamen** → **chorea** (jerky involuntary movements), cognitive decline, psychiatric symptoms.
  - Onset typically in 30s-40s. Fatal.
- **ALS (amyotrophic lateral sclerosis / Lou Gehrig's):** Progressive degeneration of upper and lower **motor neurons**. Muscle weakness, atrophy, paralysis. Cognition usually intact.

### Treatment of Psychological Disorders

**Psychotherapy types:**
- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT):** Identify and change maladaptive thought patterns and behaviors. Most empirically supported for many disorders (depression, anxiety, OCD, PTSD).
- **Psychoanalytic / psychodynamic:** Explore unconscious conflicts, defense mechanisms, early experiences. Long-term, less empirical support.
- **Humanistic (Rogerian / person-centered):** Therapist provides unconditional positive regard; client explores their own self-actualization.
- **Behavioral therapy:** Apply learning principles directly — exposure therapy for phobias, systematic desensitization, token economies.
- **Group therapy:** Multiple clients with similar issues meet with a therapist. Lower cost, social support, normalization.
- **Family therapy:** Treats the family system rather than the individual; useful in eating disorders, conduct disorders, family conflict.

**Biomedical treatments:**
- **SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors):** Block serotonin reuptake → ↑ synaptic serotonin. First-line for depression and most anxiety disorders. Examples: fluoxetine, sertraline, escitalopram.
- **SNRIs (serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors):** Block both serotonin and NE reuptake. Examples: venlafaxine, duloxetine.
- **Tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs):** Older, more side effects (anticholinergic, cardiotoxicity in overdose). Examples: amitriptyline.
- **MAOIs (monoamine oxidase inhibitors):** Inhibit the enzyme that breaks down monoamines. Dietary restrictions (tyramine → hypertensive crisis). Rarely used now.
- **Antipsychotics:**
  - **Typical (first-generation):** Block D2 receptors. Strong on positive symptoms, weak on negative. Side effects: extrapyramidal symptoms (Parkinsonism, tardive dyskinesia). Examples: haloperidol, chlorpromazine.
  - **Atypical (second-generation):** Block D2 + serotonin (5-HT2A) receptors. Better on negative symptoms, fewer EPS. Risk of metabolic syndrome. Examples: risperidone, olanzapine, clozapine.
- **Mood stabilizers — lithium:** First-line for bipolar disorder. Narrow therapeutic window; needs blood monitoring.
- **Anxiolytics — benzodiazepines:** Enhance GABA → sedation, anxiety reduction. Risk of dependence. Examples: diazepam, lorazepam, alprazolam.
- **Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT):** Controlled electrical seizure under anesthesia. For treatment-resistant depression, severe mania, catatonia. More effective than meds in some cases, but stigmatized.

**Evidence base and combination therapy:** For most disorders, **combination of psychotherapy + medication** has the strongest evidence. CBT alone is gold standard for many anxiety disorders; medication alone is rarely first-line outside of acute psychosis or severe mood disorders.
