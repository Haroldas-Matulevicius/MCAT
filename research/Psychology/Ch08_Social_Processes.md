## CHAPTER 8: Social Processes, Attitudes, and Behavior

**Maps to AAMC FC:** 7B (social processes) + 7D (attitudes and behavior).
**MCAT weight:** ~5-7 questions per exam. Group dynamics, conformity/obedience, and ELM/cognitive dissonance are heavily tested.
**Overview:** How groups influence individual behavior, how socialization shapes us, and how attitudes form and change.

### Group Behavior

- **Social facilitation (Zajonc):** The presence of others improves performance on **simple/well-practiced tasks** but impairs performance on **complex/novel tasks**. Why? Others increase arousal → enhances dominant response. If the dominant response is correct (easy task), you do better. If the dominant response is wrong (hard task), you do worse.

- **Social loafing:** Individuals exert less effort in group work (especially when individual contributions aren't identifiable). Reduced by: making contributions identifiable, increasing task importance, small groups.

- **Deindividuation:** Loss of self-awareness and individual identity in a group, leading to behavior you wouldn't normally do. Facilitated by anonymity, group size, arousal. Example: mob behavior, online trolling.

- **Bystander effect:** The more people present, the less likely any one person is to help. Driven by **diffusion of responsibility** (everyone assumes someone else will act) and **pluralistic ignorance** (everyone looks to others for cues about whether it's an emergency, and since no one else is reacting, nobody acts).

- **Group polarization:** Group discussion tends to push opinions toward the extreme of the direction they were already leaning. If a group mildly favors risk before discussion, they'll strongly favor risk afterward.

- **Groupthink (Janis):** Desire for group harmony/conformity overrides realistic evaluation of alternatives. Leads to poor decisions. More likely in highly cohesive groups with a strong leader. Symptoms: illusion of invulnerability, pressure on dissenters, self-censorship.

- **Peer pressure:** Influence from peers to conform in attitudes, behaviors, or values. Most powerful in adolescence.

### Conformity and Obedience

**Conformity (Asch line experiments):** Asch showed participants a line and asked which of three comparison lines matched. Confederate unanimously gave the wrong answer. About **75% conformed at least once**; overall conformity rate ~33%.

Two types:
- **Normative social influence:** Conforming to be liked/accepted (driven by desire to fit in). You go along even though you privately disagree.
- **Informational social influence:** Conforming because you genuinely believe the group knows better (driven by desire to be correct). You actually change your belief.

**Obedience (Milgram experiment):** Participants were told to deliver increasingly severe electric shocks to a "learner" (confederate) when they answered incorrectly. **65% went all the way to the maximum 450V** shock. Factors increasing obedience: proximity of authority figure, perceived legitimacy of authority, physical distance from victim, no role model for defiance.

### Reference Groups

Groups you compare yourself to when evaluating your own status, attitudes, and behavior. Can be groups you belong to or aspire to join.

### Socialization

**Norms** are unwritten social rules:
- **Folkways:** Informal customs (saying "bless you" after a sneeze). Violating them is awkward but not seriously punished.
- **Mores:** Norms with moral significance (honesty, sexual behavior). Violation carries stronger social sanctions.
- **Taboos:** Strongest prohibitions (incest, cannibalism). Violation triggers severe punishment and disgust.
- **Laws:** Formally codified norms enforced by the state.

**Sanctions:** Consequences for violating or following norms.
- **Positive vs negative:** Reward vs punishment.
- **Formal vs informal:** Official (a fine) vs unofficial (a frown).

**Agents of socialization:** Family (primary, earliest), peers (increasingly important in adolescence), media, education, workplace, religion.

**Types of socialization:**
- **Primary socialization:** Early childhood, learning basic norms and language.
- **Secondary socialization:** Later, learning the norms of a specific group or institution.
- **Anticipatory socialization:** Preparing for a future role (medical students learning to act like doctors).
- **Resocialization:** Discarding former behavior patterns and accepting new ones (boot camp, prison, religious conversion).

### Deviance Theories

- **Differential association (Sutherland):** Deviance is learned through interaction with others. You become deviant by associating with deviant people and learning their values and techniques.
- **Labeling theory (Becker):** Deviance is not inherent in an act but is the result of social labeling. **Primary deviance** = the initial act. **Secondary deviance** = the consequences of being labeled (the label becomes a **master status**, shaping how others treat you and how you see yourself). **Stigma** = a discrediting attribute that reduces a person from "whole" to "tainted."
- **Strain theory (Merton):** Deviance results from the gap between culturally valued goals and available legitimate means to achieve them. Five adaptations:

| Adaptation | Goals? | Means? | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Conformity** | Accept | Accept | Working a regular job |
| **Innovation** | Accept | Reject | Drug dealing to get rich |
| **Ritualism** | Reject | Accept | Going through the motions at a dead-end job |
| **Retreatism** | Reject | Reject | Dropping out of society entirely |
| **Rebellion** | Replace | Replace | Revolutionary movements |

- **Control theory:** Strong social bonds (attachment, commitment, involvement, belief) prevent deviance.
- **Conflict theory of deviance:** Powerful groups define what counts as deviant in ways that protect their interests.

### Attitudes and Behavior

**Three components (ABC):**
- **Affective:** Feelings about the attitude object.
- **Behavioral:** Tendency to act on the attitude.
- **Cognitive:** Beliefs about the attitude object.

**Attitude-behavior consistency:** Attitudes don't always predict behavior. **LaPiere study (1934):** Travelled the US with a Chinese couple, recorded that nearly all establishments served them. Later survey: most owners said they wouldn't serve Chinese customers. Big gap between attitude and behavior.

### Elaboration Likelihood Model (Petty & Cacioppo)

- **Central route:** Careful, thoughtful processing of the actual message content. Requires motivation and ability to think deeply. Produces **lasting** attitude change. Example: Reading a detailed policy paper and changing your political view.
- **Peripheral route:** Processing based on superficial cues (attractiveness of speaker, number of arguments, emotional appeal). Requires little cognitive effort. Produces **temporary** attitude change. Example: Buying a product because a celebrity endorses it.

### Cognitive Dissonance (Festinger)

When your **attitudes** and **behavior** conflict, you experience psychological discomfort (dissonance). To reduce it, you change either your attitude or your behavior (usually the attitude, since behavior already happened).

Classic study: Participants did a boring task, then were paid either $1 or $20 to tell the next participant it was fun. The **$1 group** rated the task as more enjoyable afterward. Why? They couldn't justify lying for just $1 (insufficient justification), so they changed their attitude to reduce dissonance ("it must have actually been fun"). The $20 group had sufficient external justification and didn't need to change their attitude.

### Persuasion / Compliance Techniques

- **Foot-in-the-door:** Start with a small request, then escalate to a larger one. Agreeing to the small request creates consistency pressure.
- **Door-in-the-face:** Start with an unreasonably large request (expected to be refused), then make the real, smaller request. Works through reciprocity — they conceded, so you should too.
- **Lowball technique:** Get commitment to a favorable deal, then reveal hidden costs after commitment is made.
- **That's-not-all:** Make an offer, then improve it before the person can respond ("but wait, there's more!"). Triggers reciprocity.

**Role of social media:** Modern attitudes shaped heavily by social media — echo chambers, viral persuasion, influencer endorsements (peripheral route on steroids), and algorithmic content selection.
