## CHAPTER 9: Social Interaction

**Maps to AAMC FC:** 8C (social interaction).
**MCAT weight:** ~3-5 questions per exam. Goffman, status/roles, and bureaucracy are reliably tested.
**Overview:** Elements of social interaction (status, roles, groups, networks, organizations) and self-presentation.

### Status

- **Achieved status:** Earned through effort or choice (doctor, college graduate).
- **Ascribed status:** Assigned at birth or involuntarily (race, sex, birth order, royal lineage).
- **Master status:** The status that dominates how others see you, overriding all other statuses (e.g., being a convicted felon, being the President).

### Roles

- **Role conflict:** Tension between roles associated with **different** statuses. Example: A doctor who is also a parent — work demands conflict with family obligations.
- **Role strain:** Tension within **one** role. Example: A teacher who must be both a strict disciplinarian and a nurturing mentor.
- **Role exit (Ebaugh):** Disengaging from a role that has been central to your identity (retiring, divorcing, leaving the priesthood). Four stages: doubt, search for alternatives, action stage, creation of new identity.

### Groups

- **Primary group:** Small, close, long-lasting, emotionally significant (family, close friends).
- **Secondary group:** Larger, impersonal, task-oriented, temporary (coworkers, classmates).
- **In-group:** Group you identify with.
- **Out-group:** Group you don't identify with.
- **Reference group:** Group you compare yourself to for self-evaluation.

### Networks and Social Capital

- **Social network:** The web of relationships connecting individuals.
- **Weak ties (Granovetter):** Acquaintances rather than close friends. Often the most useful for information flow (job leads come from weak ties more than close friends, who already know what you know).
- **Social capital:** The resources available to you through your social networks and relationships. Who you know matters.

### Organizations and Bureaucracy

**Bureaucracy (Weber's ideal type):** Formal organization with:
- Division of labor.
- Hierarchy of authority.
- Written/formal rules.
- Impersonality (treat people by role, not relationship).
- Employment based on qualifications/merit.

Efficient but can become rigid, slow, alienating.

- **Iron law of oligarchy (Michels):** Even democratic organizations inevitably become ruled by a small elite.
- **McDonaldization (Ritzer):** Society adopts the characteristics of fast-food restaurants:
  - **Efficiency** (fastest way to fill a need).
  - **Calculability** (quantity over quality).
  - **Predictability** (same product everywhere).
  - **Control** (technology replaces human judgment).
  Critique of excessive rationalization.
- **Characteristics of formal organizations:** Explicit goals, formal structure, rules, division of labor, specialized roles.

### Self-Presentation and Interacting with Others

**Impression management:** The conscious or unconscious process of regulating how others perceive us. Includes self-presentation strategies (ingratiation, self-promotion, intimidation, exemplification, supplication).

**Goffman's dramaturgical approach:** Life is like a theater. We are all actors performing roles for an audience.
- **Front stage:** The self we present to others. We manage impressions — controlling how we appear.
- **Back stage:** Where we drop the performance and are our "true selves."

### Communication

- **Verbal communication:** Spoken or written language.
- **Nonverbal communication:** Body language, facial expressions, gestures, eye contact, posture, tone of voice. Often conveys more emotional information than words.

### Expressing and Detecting Emotion

- **Universal facial expressions (Ekman):** Cross-cultural recognition of happiness, sadness, fear, anger, surprise, disgust.
- **Eye contact:** Cultural variation — sustained eye contact is engagement in some cultures, aggression in others.
- **Body language:** Open vs closed posture, mirroring, proxemics (use of space).
- **Animal communication:** Signals (alarm calls), gestures (chimp behaviors), displays (peacock feathers), song (whales, birds). Generally simpler than human language but can be sophisticated.
