## CHAPTER 10: Social Thinking and Behavior

**Overview:** How we perceive and explain others' behavior, attraction and aggression, attachment, altruism, and prejudice/discrimination.

### Social Behavior

#### Attraction

- **Proximity / mere exposure:** We like things and people we encounter often. The mere exposure effect (Zajonc): repeated exposure increases liking.
- **Similarity:** "Birds of a feather flock together." We like people similar to us in attitudes, values, background.
- **Reciprocity:** We like people who like us.
- **Physical attractiveness:** The "halo effect" — attractive people are assumed to have other positive traits.

#### Aggression

- **Biological factors:** Testosterone, amygdala activation, serotonin deficiency, genetic predisposition.
- **Frustration-aggression hypothesis (Dollard):** Frustration (blocked goal) increases the likelihood of aggression. Aggression may be displaced toward a safer target.
- **Social learning theory (Bandura):** Aggression is learned through observation and imitation (Bobo doll again).

#### Attachment

**Bowlby:** Attachment is an innate biological need. Infants form a primary attachment bond with a caregiver (**monotropy**), and this bond serves as a **secure base** for exploration. **Contact comfort** (Harlow's monkey experiments — infant monkeys preferred soft cloth "mothers" over wire ones that gave food) is more important than feeding for attachment formation. There is a **critical period** for attachment in early childhood.

**Ainsworth's Strange Situation** (observe infant's reaction to caregiver leaving and returning):

| Type | Response to Separation | Response to Return | Caregiver Style |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Secure** (~70%) | Distressed but explores | Easily comforted, seeks contact | Responsive, sensitive |
| **Avoidant** (~20%) | Little distress | Ignores/avoids caregiver | Emotionally unavailable |
| **Anxious-ambivalent/resistant** (~10%) | Extremely distressed | Not easily comforted, ambivalent (seeks contact but resists) | Inconsistent |
| **Disorganized** (~5%) | Confused, contradictory behavior | Fearful, disoriented | Frightening, abusive |

Secure attachment in infancy predicts better social relationships, emotional regulation, and mental health later in life.

#### Altruism

- **Empathy-altruism hypothesis (Batson):** When we feel empathy for someone, we are motivated to help them for **their** sake (true altruism), not just to reduce our own distress.
- **Kin selection (Hamilton):** We're more likely to help genetic relatives (promotes our shared genes). The closer the genetic relationship, the more likely the help. Quantified by inclusive fitness.
- **Reciprocal altruism (Trivers):** We help non-relatives with the expectation of future reciprocation.
- **Inclusive fitness:** Total reproductive success including offspring AND relatives' reproductive success.
- **Social exchange theory:** Helping is a cost-benefit calculation (not truly selfless).
- **Social support types:** Emotional (caring), instrumental (tangible help), informational (advice), appraisal (feedback).

### Social Perception and Behavior — Attribution Theory

Attribution is how we explain the causes of behavior — both our own and others'.

- **Dispositional (internal) attribution:** The cause is something about the person (personality, character, ability).
- **Situational (external) attribution:** The cause is something about the environment or circumstances.
- **Heider:** Founded attribution theory; called us "naive psychologists" who try to explain behavior.

**Key biases:**

- **Fundamental attribution error (FAE):** We overattribute others' behavior to **dispositional** (internal) causes and underestimate situational factors. You see someone trip and think "they're clumsy" (disposition) rather than "the floor is uneven" (situation). The single most tested attribution concept on the MCAT.

- **Actor-observer bias:** Extension of FAE. We explain **our own** behavior situationally ("I failed because the test was unfair") but explain **others'** behavior dispositionally ("They failed because they didn't study"). You're the actor for your behavior (you see the situation) and the observer for others' behavior (you see the person).

- **Self-serving bias:** We attribute our **successes** to internal factors ("I'm smart") and our **failures** to external factors ("The test was unfair"). This protects self-esteem.

**MCAT distinction:** FAE applies to judgments of **others**. Self-serving bias applies to judgments of **yourself**. Actor-observer bias is about the systematic difference between how you explain your own vs. others' behavior.

- **Halo effect:** Positive impression in one area (attractiveness) bleeds into other judgments (intelligence, kindness).

### Cultural Differences in Attribution

- **Individualistic cultures** (US, Western Europe) emphasize personal responsibility → more dispositional attributions, more FAE.
- **Collectivistic cultures** (East Asia, Latin America) emphasize context → more situational attributions, less FAE.

### Stereotypes, Prejudice, and Discrimination

Three components (cognitive, affective, behavioral):
- **Stereotype** (cognitive): Generalized belief about a group. Not necessarily negative, but often oversimplified.
- **Prejudice** (affective): Negative attitude/feeling toward a group.
- **Discrimination** (behavioral): Negative action toward a group member.

Types of discrimination:
- **Individual discrimination:** Personal acts of discrimination.
- **Institutional discrimination:** Discrimination embedded in social structures, policies, and practices (redlining, sentencing disparities).

**Stereotype threat (Steele & Aronson):** When members of a stereotyped group are aware of a negative stereotype about their group, they experience anxiety that can **impair performance** on tasks related to that stereotype. This is NOT about believing the stereotype — it's about the fear of confirming it.

**Self-fulfilling prophecy (Merton; Rosenthal/Pygmalion in classrooms):** Expectations about a person or group lead to behavior that causes those expectations to come true. Teacher expects student to fail → gives less attention → student performs poorly → "See, I knew they'd fail."

**Ethnocentrism:** Judging other cultures by the standards of your own (viewing your culture as superior).
**Cultural relativism:** Understanding other cultures on their own terms.

**In-group/out-group dynamics:**
- **In-group bias:** Favoring members of your own group.
- **Out-group homogeneity:** Perceiving members of other groups as more similar to each other than they actually are ("They're all the same").
- **Scapegoat theory:** Prejudice arises from frustration — people blame an out-group for their problems.

**Contact hypothesis (Allport):** Prejudice can be reduced through intergroup contact, but only under specific conditions: **equal status**, **common goals**, **intergroup cooperation**, and **support of authorities/norms**.
