### Core Sociological Theories

These are lenses for analyzing ANY social phenomenon. The MCAT will describe a scenario and ask which perspective best explains it.

**Functionalism (Structural Functionalism) — Durkheim, Parsons:**
- Society is like an organism: each part serves a function that contributes to the stability of the whole.
- **Manifest functions:** Intended and recognized consequences of a social institution. Example: Education → teaches knowledge and skills.
- **Latent functions (Merton):** Unintended and often unrecognized consequences. Example: Education → provides childcare, builds social networks, acts as a marriage market.
- **Dysfunctions:** Elements that disrupt social stability.
- **Social solidarity:**
  - **Mechanical solidarity:** Bonds based on shared beliefs, values, and activities. Found in small, traditional, homogeneous societies.
  - **Organic solidarity:** Bonds based on interdependence and specialization. Found in large, complex, heterogeneous societies (everyone has a different job and depends on others).
- **Anomie:** A breakdown of social norms and values, leading to feelings of disconnection and purposelessness. Occurs during rapid social change. Durkheim linked anomie to higher suicide rates.

**Conflict theory — Marx, Weber:**
- Society is defined by competition over limited resources. Power and inequality are central.
- Those with power (**bourgeoisie/ruling class**) use social institutions to maintain their dominance over those without (**proletariat/working class**).
- **Class consciousness:** The working class recognizing their exploitation and shared interests.
- **False consciousness:** The working class accepting the dominant ideology that justifies their exploitation ("If I work hard enough, I'll be rich too").
- **Weber's extension:** Stratification is not just about economics (class) but also **status** (prestige) and **power/party** (political influence). Weber also emphasized **rationalization** — the increasing role of calculation and efficiency in modern life (connected to bureaucracy).

**Symbolic Interactionism — Mead, Blumer:**
- Focuses on small-scale, face-to-face interactions rather than large social structures.
- Meaning is created through social interaction and the use of **symbols** (language, gestures, objects).
- People act based on the meaning they assign to things, and those meanings are constantly negotiated in social interaction.
- Connected to Mead's "I" and "Me," the looking-glass self, and labeling theory.

**Social Constructionism (Berger & Luckmann):**
- Reality is not objective but is socially constructed through human interaction and agreement.
- Concepts like race, gender, mental illness, and even "childhood" are social constructions — they exist because society collectively agrees they do, not because of inherent natural categories.
- **MCAT application:** When a passage describes how a concept's meaning varies across cultures or historical periods, social constructionism is the relevant framework.

**Feminist Theory:**
- Focuses on gender inequality as a pervasive feature of social structure.
- Examines how gender intersects with race, class, and sexuality (**intersectionality**).
- Critiques traditional sociology for being male-centered.
- Connected to concepts of patriarchy, gender roles, glass ceiling, wage gap.

**Exchange / Rational Choice Theory:**
- Social behavior is based on a cost-benefit analysis. People seek to maximize rewards and minimize costs.
- Relationships persist when rewards outweigh costs.
- Connected to reciprocity, social exchange in relationships, and economic models of behavior.

### Social Institutions

Social institutions are established patterns of behavior that address fundamental social needs.

**Education:**
- **Manifest functions:** Transmit knowledge, socialize children, confer credentials.
- **Latent functions:** Childcare, social networking, delaying labor force entry.
- **Hidden curriculum:** Unspoken, unofficial lessons and values taught in school (punctuality, obedience, competition, acceptance of hierarchy). Conflict theorists emphasize this reproduces class inequality.
- **Teacher expectancy effect / Pygmalion effect (Rosenthal & Jacobson):** Teachers' expectations of students influence student performance. Told some students were "late bloomers," those students actually improved — because teachers gave them more attention and encouragement.
- **Tracking:** Sorting students into different academic paths. Functionalists say it matches students to appropriate education; conflict theorists say it reproduces inequality (lower-income students disproportionately placed in lower tracks).
- **Educational stratification:** Unequal access to quality education based on SES, race, geography.

**Family:**
- Functions: socialization, emotional support, regulation of sexual behavior, social placement.
- **Kinship:** Social bonds based on common ancestry, marriage, or adoption.
- **Family forms:** Nuclear (two parents + children), extended (includes grandparents, aunts, etc.), single-parent, blended.
- **Endogamy:** Marriage within a social group (same religion, race, class).
- **Exogamy:** Marriage outside a social group.

**Religion:**
- Functions (functionalist view): social cohesion, social control, emotional comfort, meaning-making.
- **Types:** Church (large, established, mainstream), sect (small, separated from mainstream), cult/new religious movement (novel beliefs, charismatic leader).
- **Secularization:** Decline of religion's influence in society.
- **Fundamentalism:** Strict adherence to core religious principles, often in reaction to secularization.
- **Religiosity:** Degree of religious involvement and belief.

**Government / Politics:**
- **Weber's three types of authority:**
  - **Traditional:** Based on long-established customs and practices (monarchy, tribal chiefs).
  - **Charismatic:** Based on personal qualities of the leader (MLK, Gandhi, cult leaders). Inherently unstable — what happens when the leader dies?
  - **Rational-legal:** Based on established laws, rules, and procedures (modern democracies, bureaucracies). Most stable.
- **Power:** The ability to achieve goals even against resistance (Weber).

**Economy:**
- **Capitalism:** Private ownership, profit motive, market competition.
- **Socialism:** Collective or government ownership of means of production.
- **Division of labor (Durkheim):** Specialization of tasks in society. Greater division of labor → organic solidarity.

**Healthcare:**
- **Medicalization:** The process by which normal human conditions or behaviors become defined as medical problems requiring treatment. Examples: ADHD, childbirth (treated as a medical event rather than a natural process), aging. Conflict theorists see this as expanding medical authority and pharmaceutical profits.
- **Sick role (Parsons):** Society has expectations for how a sick person should behave:
  - **Rights:** (1) Exempt from normal social responsibilities. (2) Not held personally responsible for their illness.
  - **Obligations:** (1) Must try to get well. (2) Must seek competent medical help.
  - **Criticism:** Doesn't account for chronic illness (you can't "try to get well" from diabetes), stigmatized conditions (mental illness, HIV), or those who lack access to healthcare.
- **Illness experience:** How individuals and social groups perceive and respond to illness. Shaped by culture, social context, and access to information.
- **Social epidemiology:** The study of how social factors (class, race, gender, environment) influence the distribution of health and disease. (See Ch 12 for full treatment.)

### Culture

**Elements of culture:** Symbols, language, values, beliefs, norms, rituals, material objects.

**Material culture:** Physical objects and technology (tools, clothing, architecture).
**Symbolic / Non-material culture:** Ideas, beliefs, values, norms, language.

**Culture lag (Ogburn):** Material culture (technology) changes faster than non-material culture (values, laws, norms). Example: Technology for genetic cloning exists before society has established ethical guidelines for its use.

**Culture shock:** Disorientation when encountering a very different culture. Stages: honeymoon, frustration, adjustment, acceptance.

**Assimilation:** A minority group adopts the culture of the dominant group, losing their original cultural identity.
**Multiculturalism / pluralism:** Multiple cultures coexist and maintain their identities within a society.

**Subcultures:** Groups within a larger culture that have distinct values, norms, and lifestyles but do not fundamentally oppose the dominant culture (e.g., Amish communities, medical students).
**Countercultures:** Subcultures that actively oppose dominant cultural norms and values (e.g., hippies, punk movement).

**Cultural transmission:** Passing culture from one generation to the next via socialization.
**Evolution of culture:** Cultures change over time through invention, discovery, and diffusion.

### Demographic Transition Model

This model describes how populations change as societies industrialize. Four stages:

| Stage | Birth Rate | Death Rate | Population Growth | Society Type |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Pre-industrial** | High | High | Stable (low growth) | Agrarian, no modern medicine |
| **2. Transitional** | High | **Declining** | **Rapid growth** | Improved sanitation, medicine, nutrition |
| **3. Industrial** | **Declining** | Low | Slowing growth | Urbanization, women's education, access to contraception |
| **4. Post-industrial** | Low | Low | Stable or declining | Modern developed nations |

Some demographers propose a **Stage 5:** Birth rate falls *below* death rate → population decline (Japan, parts of Europe).

### Malthusian Theory

Population grows **exponentially** (1, 2, 4, 8, 16...) while food production grows **arithmetically** (1, 2, 3, 4, 5...). Eventually, population outstrips food supply → famine, disease, war ("positive checks"). Malthus advocated "preventive checks" (delayed marriage, abstinence).

**Criticism:** Malthus didn't predict technological advances in agriculture (Green Revolution), contraception, or demographic transition.

### Population Pyramids

- **Expansive:** Wide base (many young people), narrow top. High birth rate, rapid growth. Typical of developing countries.
- **Constrictive:** Narrow base. Low birth rate, aging population, potential population decline. Typical of developed countries.
- **Stationary:** Roughly equal proportions at each age. Stable population.

### Fertility and Mortality Measures

- **Crude birth rate:** Births per 1,000 population per year.
- **Total fertility rate (TFR):** Average number of children per woman. Replacement level ~2.1 in developed countries.
- **Age-specific fertility rate:** Births per 1,000 women in a specific age group.
- **Crude mortality rate:** Deaths per 1,000 population per year.
- **Infant mortality rate:** Deaths under age 1 per 1,000 live births. Key indicator of a population's health and development.
- **Age-specific mortality rate:** Deaths per 1,000 in a specific age group.
- **Life expectancy:** Average number of years a person can expect to live.

### Migration

**Push factors:** Conditions driving people away from a location (war, poverty, persecution, environmental disasters).
**Pull factors:** Conditions attracting people to a new location (economic opportunity, safety, freedom, family).

**Types:** Immigration (into a country), emigration (out of a country), internal migration (within a country, e.g., rural to urban).

### Social Movements

- **Relative deprivation:** Feeling deprived relative to a comparison group. Can fuel social movements — it's not absolute poverty that motivates revolution, but the perception that others have more.
- Types: reform movements (change specific aspects of society), revolutionary movements (replace existing system), reactionary movements (return to previous state), new social movements (identity/quality of life issues — environmentalism, LGBTQ+ rights).

### Globalization

Increasing interconnectedness of economies, cultures, and populations worldwide. Driven by trade, technology, migration, and communication.
- **Positive effects:** Economic growth, cultural exchange, access to goods and information.
- **Negative effects:** Cultural homogenization, exploitation of developing countries, widening inequality, environmental degradation.

### Urbanization

**Urbanization:** The movement of population from rural to urban areas. Associated with industrialization, economic development, and social change.

**Suburbanization:** Movement from cities to surrounding suburbs. Associated with post-WWII America (highways, GI Bill, white flight).

**Gentrification:** Wealthier people move into previously lower-income urban neighborhoods, renovate properties, raise property values, and displace original residents. **Conflict theory** lens: reflects and reinforces class inequality.

**Urban decline / urban blight:** Deterioration of inner-city areas — poverty, crime, infrastructure decay.
