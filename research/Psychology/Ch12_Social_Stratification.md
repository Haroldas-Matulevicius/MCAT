## CHAPTER 12: Social Inequality

**MCAT weight:** ~3-5 questions per exam, but **rising** — health disparities and social determinants of health are among the fastest-growing topic areas.
**Overview:** Stratification, social class, mobility, intersectionality, and the social determinants of health.

> **KAPLAN GAP.** Only 5% of PS, but questions on health disparities and social determinants of health are increasing. Kaplan coverage is thin — supplement.

### Social Stratification

**Social stratification** is the ranking of people in a hierarchy based on their access to valued resources (wealth, power, prestige). It is a structural feature of society, not just individual outcomes.

**Theoretical perspectives:**
- **Davis-Moore thesis (functionalist):** Stratification is necessary and inevitable. Important positions require more talent and training, so they must offer greater rewards to motivate qualified people to fill them. Criticism: doesn't explain why some high-value jobs (teaching, social work) are poorly compensated.
- **Conflict theory:** Stratification results from exploitation and perpetuates inequality. The wealthy maintain power through control of institutions, ideology, and resources.
- **Weber:** Stratification has three dimensions: **class** (economic position), **status** (social prestige), **party/power** (political influence). These can be independent — a pastor may have high status but low class.

### Social Class

- **Working class:** Manual labor, hourly wages, less education and autonomy.
- **Middle class:** White-collar work, college education, salaried positions.
- **Upper class:** Wealth (often inherited), control of capital, elite social networks.

### Socioeconomic Status (SES)

SES is a composite measure: **income + education + occupation**. The single best predictor of life outcomes on the MCAT — health, education, life expectancy, mental health, and more.

### Social Mobility

- **Intergenerational mobility:** Change in social position from one generation to the next (your parents were working class, you become middle class).
- **Intragenerational mobility:** Change in social position within a single lifetime (you start as a cashier and become a CEO).
- **Vertical mobility:** Movement up or down the stratification hierarchy.
- **Horizontal mobility:** Change in position at the same level (switching from one middle-class job to another).

**Meritocracy:** The idea that social position is determined by individual effort and ability. Conflict theorists argue it's a myth that legitimizes inequality — structural barriers (racism, lack of access to education) prevent equal opportunity.

### Poverty

- **Absolute poverty:** Inability to meet basic needs (food, shelter, clean water).
- **Relative poverty:** Having significantly fewer resources than others in your society, even if basic needs are met.
- **Social exclusion:** Being marginalized from participation in social, economic, and political life.

### Cultural and Social Capital

**Cultural capital (Bourdieu):** Non-financial assets that promote social mobility. Three forms:
1. **Embodied:** Knowledge, skills, dispositions, mannerisms acquired through socialization (knowing how to network at a professional event, speaking "properly").
2. **Objectified:** Physical objects that signify cultural knowledge (books, art, instruments).
3. **Institutionalized:** Formal credentials (degrees, certifications).

Cultural capital is unevenly distributed by class. Upper-class children are socialized with cultural capital that gives them advantages in education and careers. This is how class is **reproduced** across generations even without direct transfer of wealth.

**Social capital:** The resources available to you through your social networks and relationships. Who you know matters. A well-connected person can get job referrals, mentorship, financial help, and information that a socially isolated person cannot. Social capital is a major mechanism of inequality — wealthier people tend to have more valuable social networks.

**Class consciousness vs false consciousness (Marx):** See Chapter 11 for full treatment. Class consciousness drives collective action; false consciousness perpetuates inequality.

### Intersectionality

**Intersectionality (Crenshaw):** Multiple social identities (race, gender, class, sexuality, disability) intersect to create unique experiences of privilege and oppression. A Black woman's experience is not simply "Black experience + woman experience" — it is a qualitatively different experience produced by the intersection of both identities.

**MCAT application:** When a passage describes disparities that are worse for people at the intersection of multiple marginalized identities, intersectionality is the relevant concept.

### Epidemiology — Core Measures

These measures describe how disease is distributed and tracked in populations. The MCAT increasingly tests these as part of social epidemiology.

- **Incidence:** **Number of NEW cases** of a disease in a population over a specified time period. Tells you about the rate of acquiring the disease. Formula: (new cases) / (population at risk × time).
- **Prevalence:** **Total number of cases** (new + existing) in a population at a given time. Tells you the disease burden. Formula: (total cases) / (total population).
- **Relationship:** Prevalence = Incidence × average duration. A chronic disease (long duration) can have low incidence but high prevalence. An acute disease that kills quickly can have high incidence but low prevalence.
- **Mortality (death rate):** Number of deaths in a population per unit time.
- **Morbidity:** Disease state; the rate of disease in a population.

### Social Epidemiology

The study of how social factors (class, race, gender, environment) influence the distribution of health and disease. Connects directly to the social determinants of health below.

### Health Disparities

**Health disparities:** Differences in health outcomes between groups. Lower SES → higher rates of chronic disease, mental illness, infant mortality, lower life expectancy. Racial and ethnic minorities experience worse health outcomes even after controlling for SES (due to racism, discrimination, and cumulative stress).

Disparities exist by:
- **Class:** Lower SES → worse outcomes nearly across the board.
- **Gender:** Women have higher rates of depression, autoimmune disease; men have higher rates of CV disease, suicide.
- **Race:** Black Americans have higher rates of hypertension, infant mortality, maternal mortality. Disparities persist after controlling for SES.

### Healthcare Disparities

**Healthcare disparities:** Differences in access to and quality of healthcare. Includes:
- Lack of insurance.
- Geographic barriers (rural areas, "healthcare deserts").
- Language barriers.
- Implicit bias among providers.
- Lack of culturally competent care.
- Cost barriers (high deductibles, out-of-pocket).

### Social Determinants of Health

Health is not just about biology and individual behavior. It is shaped by the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age. Key social determinants:

- **SES:** The dominant determinant. Poverty → poor nutrition, housing instability, exposure to environmental toxins, chronic stress, limited access to healthcare.
- **Education:** Higher education → better health literacy, higher income, healthier behaviors.
- **Neighborhood / physical environment:** Food deserts, proximity to pollution, crime, access to parks and recreational facilities.
- **Discrimination and racism:** Chronic exposure to discrimination → elevated cortisol, cardiovascular disease, mental health disorders. **Weathering hypothesis (Geronimus):** Cumulative impact of social and economic adversity on health of marginalized populations causes accelerated biological aging.
- **Social support / social networks:** Strong social connections → better health outcomes, lower mortality.

### Environmental Justice

**Environmental justice:** The fair treatment of all people regardless of race, income, or national origin with respect to environmental policies and hazards. In practice, **environmental racism** means that pollution sources (factories, waste sites, highways) are disproportionately located in low-income communities and communities of color. This creates health disparities through exposure to toxins, polluted air and water, and lack of green space.

### Global Health

Patterns of disease and well-being across countries. Wealthy countries have higher rates of chronic, lifestyle-related disease (CV disease, cancer, diabetes); poorer countries have higher rates of infectious disease, maternal/child mortality, and undernutrition. The "double burden" affects middle-income countries dealing with both.

### Second Sickness

**Second sickness:** Illness that arises not from the primary disease itself, but from inadequate or harmful societal/healthcare response to it. Stigma, lack of treatment access, financial ruin from medical bills, social isolation due to illness, and discrimination by providers all constitute "second sickness." Particularly relevant to mental illness, HIV, addiction, and chronic illness in marginalized populations.

## Classical vs. Operant Conditioning at a Glance

| Feature | Classical | Operant |
|---|---|---|
| **Learning type** | Associating two stimuli | Associating behavior with consequence |
| **Behavior type** | Involuntary/reflexive | Voluntary |
| **Key figure** | Pavlov | Skinner |
| **Mechanism** | CS paired with UCS | Reinforcement or punishment |
| **Extinction** | CS presented without UCS | Behavior not reinforced |
| **Biological example** | Taste aversion (Garcia) | Shaping, schedules of reinforcement |
